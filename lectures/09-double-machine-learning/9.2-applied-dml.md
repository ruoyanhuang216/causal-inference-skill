# 9.2 — Applied DML & the Two Gotchas

> **Part of:** [Lecture 9 — Double / Debiased Machine Learning](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§9.1](./9.1-dml-core.md) — FWL, the partially linear model, cross-fitting.

The mechanics of §9.1, walked end-to-end on a real scenario, then the two
caveats that separate a working DML pipeline from a plausible-looking wrong one.

---

## 1. The business problem

You're a lead DS at a B2B SaaS company (think Snowflake).

- **Feature $D$:** a new "Auto-Scaling Compute" feature, strictly **opt-in.**
- **Outcome $Y$:** next quarter's ARR.
- **Confounders $X$:** **500+ telemetry metrics** — API calls, storage limits,
  active users, query latency, historical spend, industry codes.
- **The problem:** sophisticated enterprises are far more likely to adopt
  Auto-Scaling *and* to grow ARR naturally. The 500-metric → ARR relationship is
  wildly nonlinear. OLS chokes on the dimensions; PSM
  ([Lecture 8](../08-selection-on-observables/)) can't balance 500 continuous
  covariates. **DML is the tool.**

---

## 2. The walkthrough

### Step 1 — K-fold cross-fitting setup

Split 10,000 clients into **$K = 5$** folds of 2,000. Walk through the loop with
**Fold 1 held out**, Folds 2–5 (8,000 clients) as the training set.

### Step 2 — Train the nuisance models (Folds 2–5)

A flexible nonlinear learner (LightGBM, a neural net):

- **Model Y** — `LGBMRegressor` predicting ARR $Y$ from the 500 features $X$.
  **Do not include $D$.**
- **Model D** — `LGBMClassifier` predicting adoption $D$ from the same $X$.

### Step 3 — Out-of-sample residuals (Fold 1)

Predict on the 2,000 unseen Fold-1 clients:

```math
\tilde{Y}_i = Y_i - \hat{Y}(X_i) \qquad \text{(ARR surprise: more revenue than telemetry predicted)}
```

```math
\tilde{D}_i = D_i - \hat{D}(X_i) \qquad \text{(adoption surprise: adopted when the model said they wouldn't, or vice-versa)}
```

### Step 4 — The causal stage (Fold 1)

A dead-simple single-variable OLS through the origin:

```math
\tilde{Y}_i = \theta \, \tilde{D}_i + \epsilon_i
```

You've scrubbed the confounding out of both sides. You're comparing the
**surprise in adoption** against the **surprise in revenue** — that's the causal
effect $\hat\theta_1$ for Fold 1.

### Step 5 — Rotate and average

Repeat Steps 2–4 with each fold held out in turn, then average:

```math
\hat{\theta}_{\text{DML}} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \hat{\theta}_k
```

(In practice the residuals from all folds are pooled into one final OLS, which
is equivalent and gives the standard error directly.)

---

## 3. Gotcha 1 — feature importance is not causal

The tempting blunder: read `feature_importances_` off the LightGBM nuisance
models and hand the business "insights" — *"API calls are the #1 driver of
ARR!"*

**They are nuisance models. Their importances are prediction artifacts, not
causal effects.** The Model-Y importances tell you *what predicts ARR*, which is
soaked in the same confounding you're trying to remove — "storage used" predicts
ARR because big companies have both, not because storage *causes* revenue.

> **The defense:** *"The ML models in Steps 2–3 are strictly for prediction, not
> interpretation. They're 'nuisance' parameters — mathematical vacuums that suck
> up variance, nothing more. Only the final $\theta$ is causal. If you want to
> know which features have causal effects, each one needs its own DML (or a CATE
> model — Lecture 10), not a feature-importance readout."*

This is the single most common way DML gets misused in industry: a correct
$\theta$ shipped alongside a table of confounded "drivers" that leadership then
acts on.

---

## 4. Gotcha 2 — overlap still bites

DML feels like magic, but it is **still a selection-on-observables method**
(Lecture 8): it removes *observed* confounding, and an unobserved confounder
breaks it exactly as before. It also inherits the **overlap** requirement, in a
form that's easy to miss.

If Model D predicts adoption *perfectly* (AUC → 1.0), then your telemetry
perfectly separates adopters from non-adopters — so $\hat D(X) \approx D$, and
the residual $\tilde D \to 0$. Now look at the final stage:

```math
\hat\theta = \frac{\tilde{D} \cdot \tilde{Y}}{\tilde{D} \cdot \tilde{D}} \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{0}{0}
```

**The independent variable is a column of zeros; the math explodes.** DML
*requires* residual, unexplained randomness in *why* people adopt — the same
overlap condition as Lecture 8 §8.1, now expressed as "$\tilde D$ must have
variance."

> **The defense:** *"A near-perfect adoption model isn't a triumph — it's an
> overlap violation. If telemetry perfectly explains who adopts, there's no
> quasi-random variation left to identify the effect. We need some genuine
> noise in the adoption decision, and I check that $\tilde D$ has real
> variance before trusting $\theta$."*

The paradox mirrors [§8.1's R² illusion](../08-selection-on-observables/8.1-propensity-and-unconfoundedness.md#4-overlap-and-the-r--auc-illusion):
a *better* propensity model can make the causal design *worse*.

---

## 5. The pre-flight checklist

Before trusting $\hat\theta$:

1. **Nuisance out-of-sample $R^2$** (Model Y) and AUC (Model D). Too low →
   regularization bias, orthogonality can't save you (§9.1 §3.1). Suspiciously
   high AUC → overlap violation (§4).
2. **$\text{Var}(\tilde D) > 0$** with margin. If the treatment residual is
   near-degenerate, stop.
3. **Cross-fitting is on** (§9.1). Re-run over 2–3 random fold seeds; $\theta$
   should be stable.
4. **Report $\theta$ alone as causal.** Nuisance importances stay in the
   appendix, labeled "predictive, not causal."

---

## 6. Common misreadings

**"The LightGBM says API calls drive ARR."**
Nuisance-model importance is prediction, confounded. Not causal. §3.

**"Our adoption model has 0.98 AUC — great first stage."**
That's an overlap violation: $\tilde D \to 0$ and $\theta$ is undefined. You
need unexplained variance in adoption. §4.

**"DML handles confounding, so unobservables are fine."**
DML removes *observed* confounding only. It's selection-on-observables; an
unobserved confounder breaks it. §4.

**"Include D in Model Y so it predicts ARR better."**
Never — Model Y must predict $Y$ from $X$ *only*. Including $D$ leaks the
treatment into the nuisance and destroys the residualization. §2 Step 2.

**"One DML run gives me every feature's causal effect."**
It gives *one* $\theta$ for the one treatment you residualized. Per-feature
causal effects need per-feature designs or a CATE model (Lecture 10). §3.
