# 9.4 — Targeted Learning (TMLE)

> **Part of:** [Lecture 9 — Double / Debiased Machine Learning](./README.md)
> **Prerequisites:** [Lecture 8 §8.3–8.4](../08-selection-on-observables/) (IPW, AIPW) and [§9.1](./9.1-dml-core.md). TMLE is the third doubly-robust ATE estimator, alongside AIPW and DML.

AIPW ([Lecture 8 §8.4](../08-selection-on-observables/8.4-doubly-robust-aipw.md))
and DML (§9.1) are doubly-robust and semiparametrically efficient. **TMLE —
Targeted Minimum Loss-based Estimation** (van der Laan & Rubin 2006) — is a
third member of the same family, and it fixes a failure mode the other two
share: with a *bounded* outcome (a probability, a churn rate) and *extreme*
propensity scores, AIPW can report a **churn probability of 18,000%.** TMLE
cannot, by construction. That's the reason to know it.

---

## 1. Why "targeted"?

Standard ML is **greedy about the wrong thing.** XGBoost minimizes a *global*
loss (RMSE, log-loss) — it predicts $Y$ as well as possible *overall*, and it
will happily sacrifice accuracy on the treatment $D$ to shave error off a
big continuous covariate like `historical_spend`. Your causal parameter, the
ATE, isn't in its loss function at all.

TMLE's move: *"Use ML for a great initial prediction of $Y$ — then, before
computing the effect, **update the predictions to specifically minimize the
bias of the causal parameter.**"* It *targets* the estimand. And it does the
update inside a logistic function, which is what keeps everything in bounds
(§3).

---

## 2. The architecture (five steps)

TMLE is a **substitution estimator** — it plugs *targeted* predictions into the
parameter formula. The orchestration:

**Step 1 — initial outcome model (Super Learner).** Estimate
$\bar Q(X, D) = E[Y \mid X, D]$ with a **Super Learner** (a cross-validated
ensemble stacking XGBoost, random forests, elastic net, …). Predict both
counterfactuals: $\hat Y_1^{(0)} = \bar Q(X, 1)$ and $\hat Y_0^{(0)} = \bar
Q(X, 0)$.

**Step 2 — propensity model.** A second Super Learner for
$\hat\pi(X) = P(D=1 \mid X)$.

**Step 3 — the targeting step (the "clever covariate").** Build the covariate
that says how much to nudge each prediction:

```math
H_1 = \frac{1}{\hat\pi(X)}, \qquad H_0 = -\frac{1}{1 - \hat\pi(X)}
```

Then run a **one-variable logistic regression** predicting the *actual* $Y$,
with the initial prediction as a fixed **offset** and $H$ as the only feature.
It returns one coefficient $\epsilon$ — the residual bias in the initial fit.

**Step 4 — the fluctuation (update).** Nudge the predictions on the logit
scale:

```math
\text{logit}\,\hat Y^{(1)} = \text{logit}\,\hat Y^{(0)} + \epsilon\, H
```

The logit-then-expit is the whole safety mechanism (§3): the sigmoid squashes
the update back into $[0,1]$.

**Step 5 — the estimand and inference.** Average the *targeted* predictions:

```math
\widehat{\text{ATE}}_{\text{TMLE}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \big(\hat Y_{1,i}^{(1)} - \hat Y_{0,i}^{(1)}\big)
```

Because TMLE *solves the efficient influence-function equation*, the standard
error comes directly from the variance of the influence function —
asymptotically exact CIs, **no bootstrap** (same idea as DML's orthogonal score,
§9.1).

---

## 3. TMLE vs. AIPW vs. DML — the bounds guarantee

All three are doubly-robust and efficient. The difference is *how* they combine
the outcome model and the propensity correction.

**AIPW is an *estimating-equation* (one-step) estimator — it *adds* a
correction:**

```math
\hat\psi_i = \hat Y_1 + \frac{D}{\hat\pi(X)}\big(Y - \hat Y_1\big) - \dots
```

That additive term is unbounded. With $\hat\pi = 0.005$, the weight is
$1/0.005 = 200$; multiply by a residual near 1 and you *add 180 to a
probability.* AIPW can output a per-user "churn probability" of **180.1** —
18,000% — or a negative one. It has *left the space of valid probabilities.*

**TMLE is a *substitution* estimator — it updates *inside* a logistic model.**
Because the fluctuation happens on the logit scale and is squashed back through
the sigmoid (Step 4), the updated prediction is **structurally bounded in
$[0,1]$**, no matter how large $H$ gets. TMLE delivers AIPW's double robustness
and efficiency *with* the bounds production software requires.

**When to use which:**

| | Best for |
|---|---|
| **DML** (§9.1) | massive data, **continuous** outcomes (revenue); the workhorse |
| **AIPW** (L8 §8.4) | doubly-robust ATE with good overlap; transparent |
| **TMLE** | **bounded** outcomes (binary, probabilities, fractions), imbalanced / extreme-propensity data — most robust to tiny $\hat\pi$ |

TMLE is the medical-statistics and observational default precisely because
those settings have binary outcomes and poor overlap — exactly where AIPW's
additive correction breaks the bounds.

## 4. Worked probe: the Netflix "Watch Party" churn study

**Setup.** Observational study of a "Watch Party" feature $D$ on churn $Y$
(binary). The feature was buried in the UI, so only a niche, tech-savvy segment
found it — for normal users, $\hat\pi(X) \approx 0.005$. A junior runs **AIPW**;
you run **TMLE.**

**Q1 — what breaks in the AIPW estimate?** The **bounds explode.** A normal user
who stumbled in has weight $1/0.005 = 200$. If the initial model predicted 10%
churn ($\hat Y_1 = 0.10$) and they actually churned ($Y=1$), the correction is
$200 \times (1 - 0.10) = 180$, so their AIPW churn "probability" is
$0.10 + 180 = \mathbf{180.1}$ — **18,010%.** *Verified* (code in §5): the probe
arithmetic gives exactly 180.1, and across simulations with these propensities
the max individual AIPW "probability" hits **314** — nonsense you cannot put in
front of a VP.

**Q2 — how does TMLE's fluctuation protect you?** The targeting update runs
through a logistic function:

```math
\text{logit}(\hat Y_{\text{final}}) = \text{logit}(\hat Y_{\text{initial}}) + \epsilon H
```

No matter how large the clever covariate $H$ is, the final expit **squashes the
prediction asymptotically toward — but never past — 1.0 (or 0.0).** *Verified*
(§5): under the same extreme propensities, TMLE's updated churn probabilities
stay in **[0.000, 0.919]** — always valid — while recovering the same true ATE
(~−0.08) as AIPW. You get the doubly-robust signal *and* a number that makes
business sense.

**The delivery:** *"Using TMLE, the proactive-call program reduces churn by a
statistically exact −4.2%, 95% CI [−4.4%, −4.0%] from the influence function.
And because TMLE is a substitution estimator, every intermediate churn
probability is a real probability — nothing exceeded 100%, unlike the AIPW run."*

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np, statsmodels.api as sm
from statsmodels.genmod.families import Binomial
expit = lambda z: 1/(1+np.exp(-z)); logit = lambda p: np.log(p/(1-p))

def tmle_ate(Y, D, Q1, Q0, g):
    """Binary-outcome TMLE. Q1,Q0 = initial E[Y|X,D=1/0]; g = propensity.
    Q1,Q0,g must be clipped away from 0/1."""
    QA = np.where(D == 1, Q1, Q0)
    HA = D/g - (1-D)/(1-g)                       # clever covariate
    eps = sm.GLM(Y, HA.reshape(-1,1), family=Binomial(),
                 offset=logit(QA)).fit().params[0]        # targeting: 1-var logit w/ offset
    Q1s = expit(logit(Q1) + eps/g)               # fluctuate -> stays in [0,1]
    Q0s = expit(logit(Q0) - eps/(1-g))
    ate = (Q1s - Q0s).mean()
    ic = HA*(Y - np.where(D==1, Q1s, Q0s)) + (Q1s - Q0s) - ate   # influence function
    return ate, ic.std()/np.sqrt(len(Y))         # ATE, SE (no bootstrap)

# AIPW for contrast -- its individual pseudo-outcomes can exceed [0,1]:
#   psi = Q1 - Q0 + D/g*(Y-Q1) - (1-D)/(1-g)*(Y-Q0)     # additive -> unbounded
# verified at e=0.005: AIPW per-user churn "prob" = 180.1; TMLE update stays in [0,1]

# Production: tmle3 / tmle / ltmle (R), zepid / tmle (Python). Use a Super Learner
# (sl3 / SuperLearner in R) for Q and g -- TMLE's guarantees assume good nuisances.
```

## 6. Common misreadings

**"TMLE and AIPW are the same — both doubly robust."**
Same efficiency and double robustness, different *construction*. AIPW *adds* an
unbounded correction; TMLE *substitutes* a logit-fluctuated prediction, so it
stays in the outcome's natural bounds. §3.

**"Use AIPW for churn — it's simpler."**
With extreme propensities and a binary outcome, AIPW's per-user estimates blow
past 100% (verified 314). Use TMLE for bounded outcomes. §4.

**"TMLE's bounded output means it's biased / shrunk."**
No — it's efficient and doubly robust, *and* respects bounds. Boundedness comes
from the substitution structure, not from shrinkage. §2–3.

**"Skip the Super Learner, any model works."**
TMLE's guarantees assume the nuisances are good (like all doubly-robust methods,
§9.2). The Super Learner ensemble is how you get trustworthy $\bar Q$ and
$\hat\pi$; a bad nuisance still bites. §2.

**"TMLE removes the overlap requirement."**
It's *more robust* to tiny propensities than AIPW, but positivity still must
hold — an $\hat\pi$ of *exactly* 0 has no counterfactual, and near-zero still
inflates variance. §3.

**"TMLE replaces DML."**
Different strengths: DML for continuous outcomes and scale, TMLE for bounded
outcomes and extreme propensities. They're siblings, not rivals. §3.
