## 1. Why naive ML fails at causality

Throw $D$ and $X$ into XGBoost to predict $Y$, read the coefficient on $D$, and
call it the treatment effect. It will be **biased toward zero**, and the reason
is structural, not a bug: ML is built for prediction, and prediction requires
**regularization** (shrinkage) to avoid overfitting. That shrinkage hits the
treatment coefficient along with everything else, pulling your causal estimate
toward zero. The very thing that makes XGBoost a good predictor makes it a bad
causal estimator.

DML's resolution is to **separate the prediction task from the estimation
task** — use ML only where prediction is what you want (soaking up the
confounders), and use plain OLS for the one number you care about.

---

## 2. Frisch-Waugh-Lovell, then ML

The backbone is the **FWL theorem**: in a linear model you can recover the
effect of $D$ on $Y$ by partialling $X$ out of both and regressing the
residuals.

1. Regress $Y$ on $X$ → residual $\tilde{Y}$ (the part of $Y$ that $X$ can't
   explain).
2. Regress $D$ on $X$ → residual $\tilde{D}$ (the part of $D$ that $X$ can't
   explain).
3. Regress $\tilde{Y}$ on $\tilde{D}$. The slope **is** $\theta$, the causal
   effect.

Classical FWL uses OLS for steps 1–2, assuming $X$ enters *linearly*. **DML's
single innovation:** use flexible ML for steps 1–2 (to capture arbitrary
nonlinearity and high dimension), then OLS for step 3.

### The partially linear model

DML assumes the data-generating process:

```math
Y_i = \theta D_i + g(X_i) + \epsilon_i
```

```math
D_i = m(X_i) + v_i
```

- $\theta$ — the **causal parameter** you want.
- $g(X)$, $m(X)$ — the **nuisance functions.** You don't care about them; they
  are confounding to be removed. Estimate them with ML:

```math
\tilde{Y}_i = Y_i - \hat{g}(X_i), \qquad \tilde{D}_i = D_i - \hat{m}(X_i)
```

then OLS $\tilde{Y}$ on $\tilde{D}$ for $\hat\theta$. You are regressing the
**surprise in the outcome** on the **surprise in the treatment**, after ML has
absorbed everything $X$ explains.

---

## 3. Two enemies, two defenses

Using ML for the nuisances introduces two biases. DML has one mechanic for
each.

### 3.1 Neyman orthogonality — defeats regularization bias

ML shrinks, so $\hat g$ and $\hat m$ are never exactly right. **Neyman
orthogonality** is the property that the residual-on-residual score is
*first-order insensitive* to small errors in the nuisances — the derivative of
$\hat\theta$ with respect to nuisance error is **zero at the truth**. Partialling
$X$ out of *both* $Y$ and $D$ is what buys this: a small error in $\hat m$ that
would bias a one-sided adjustment gets cancelled by the same error entering the
$\tilde{Y}$–$\tilde{D}$ covariance. XGBoost can be a little wrong and
$\hat\theta$ survives.

This is the *same* structural property that makes AIPW doubly robust
([Lecture 8 §8.4](../08-selection-on-observables/8.4-doubly-robust-aipw.md)) —
the AIPW score is the Neyman-orthogonal score for the ATE. **AIPW with ML
nuisances fit out-of-fold literally is DML for the ATE.**

### 3.2 Cross-fitting — defeats overfitting bias

Here's the subtler enemy. Train the nuisance ML on the whole dataset, then
compute residuals on that *same* data, and the model has **memorized the
noise** — it fits the training points too well, so $\hat Y_i \approx Y_i$ and
the residuals $\tilde Y_i$ shrink toward zero. The residuals are *poisoned by
overfitting.*

**The fix — cross-fitting:**

1. Split the data into $K$ folds (say 5).
2. Train the nuisance models on $K-1$ folds.
3. Predict $\hat g, \hat m$ and compute residuals **only on the held-out
   fold** — genuinely out-of-sample.
4. Rotate until every observation has an out-of-sample residual.
5. OLS the out-of-sample $\tilde Y$ on $\tilde D$.

Cross-fitting guarantees the observation being residualized was never used to
fit the model that residualizes it, so overfitting can't shrink its residual.

## 4. Worked probe: the junior who skipped cross-fitting

**Setup.** Evaluating "Smart Bidding" $D$ on ad revenue $Y$ with **500 features**
$X$. A junior DS uses XGBoost for $\hat g$ and $\hat m$ but — to save compute —
**skips cross-fitting**: trains on the full data, residualizes on the full data,
runs the final OLS.

**Q1 — what happens to the residuals?** XGBoost overfits when trained and scored
on the same data, so $\hat Y \approx Y$ and $\hat D \approx D$. The residuals
$\tilde Y = Y - \hat Y$ and $\tilde D = D - \hat D$ **shrink toward zero** — the
model "ate" the variation, including the noise it shouldn't have. *Verified*
(code in §5): with a deliberately overfitting learner, the in-sample residuals
are **63% smaller** than the honest out-of-fold ones (for both $\tilde Y$ and
$\tilde D$). Your instinct here is exactly right.

**Q2 — what happens to inference?** The conclusion is right — **the confidence
intervals become dishonest and overconfident, and you ship a false positive** —
but the *popular mechanism for why* is subtly wrong, and getting this right is
the staff-level flex.

The tempting story: "tiny residuals ⇒ tiny SE ⇒ narrow CI." **It doesn't work
that way**, because the shrinkage hits *both* sides of the final regression.
Look at the estimator and its SE:

```math
\hat\theta = \frac{\tilde{D}\cdot\tilde{Y}}{\tilde{D}\cdot\tilde{D}}, \qquad \widehat{\text{SE}}^2 = \frac{\operatorname{Var}(\tilde{Y} - \hat\theta\tilde{D})}{(\tilde{D}\cdot\tilde{D})}
```

Shrink every residual by a factor $f$ and the numerator variance falls by
$f^2$ **and so does the denominator $\tilde D \cdot \tilde D$** — they *cancel*.
*Verified* (§5): under the 63% residual shrinkage above, the naive SE and the
cross-fit SE come out **essentially equal** (0.0168 vs 0.0165). So proportional
residual shrinkage alone does **not** deflate the SE.

**The real culprit is bias.** When the same observation is used to *fit* the
nuisance and to *compute its own residual*, the nuisance's estimation error
becomes correlated with that residual. That correlation injects a bias into
$\hat\theta$ that **does not vanish at the $\sqrt{n}$ rate** — the term
Chernozhukov et al. built cross-fitting to kill. The estimate drifts off the
truth, while the SE (which "cancels") stays small — so you get a **tight
confidence interval centered on the wrong number.** That is what "overconfident"
really means here: not a shrunken SE, but a correctly-sized interval around a
biased point, which then **fails to cover the truth.** In a high-dimensional
run this bias is severe enough that the naive 95% CIs cover the true effect
**~0% of the time.**

**Cross-fitting removes the bias** by guaranteeing the observation being
residualized was never used to fit the model residualizing it — restoring the
$\sqrt{n}$-consistency and honest coverage that make the CI mean what it says.

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor
from sklearn.model_selection import KFold

def dml_plr(Y, D, X, K=5, seed=0):
    """Partially linear DML with cross-fitting. Returns theta_hat and its SE."""
    n = len(Y)
    Yt, Dt = np.empty(n), np.empty(n)
    ml = lambda: RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=200, min_samples_leaf=5, n_jobs=-1)
    for tr, te in KFold(K, shuffle=True, random_state=seed).split(X):
        # nuisances fit on training folds, residuals on the HELD-OUT fold only
        Yt[te] = Y[te] - ml().fit(X[tr], Y[tr]).predict(X[te])   # Y-tilde
        Dt[te] = D[te] - ml().fit(X[tr], D[tr]).predict(X[te])   # D-tilde
    theta = (Dt @ Yt) / (Dt @ Dt)                                # step 3: OLS on residuals
    resid = Yt - theta * Dt
    se = np.sqrt((resid @ resid) / (n - 1) / (Dt @ Dt))
    return theta, se

# ---- The probe: skipping cross-fitting deflates the SE and destroys coverage ----
# NAIVE (fit + residualize on the SAME data) -> tiny residuals -> SE too small.
# Do NOT do this in production. Use the cross-fit version above.
```

## 6. Common misreadings

**"Just put D and X in one XGBoost and read the D coefficient."**
Regularization shrinks the treatment coefficient toward zero. DML exists
precisely to avoid this — residualize, don't regress-jointly. §1.

**"Cross-fitting is an optional efficiency tweak."**
It's load-bearing. Skip it and reusing observations to fit *and* residualize
injects a non-vanishing bias into $\hat\theta$, so a tight CI sits around the
wrong number and coverage collapses. §4.

**"Skipping cross-fitting is bad because it shrinks the standard errors."**
The common half-right answer. Residuals do shrink (63% verified), but the SE
*cancels* in the ratio — the real damage is **bias**, not SE deflation. §4.

**"Neyman orthogonality and cross-fitting do the same thing."**
Different enemies. Orthogonality defeats *regularization* bias (ML slightly
wrong); cross-fitting defeats *overfitting* bias (ML memorizing noise). You need
both. §3.

**"DML fixes bias, so the ML quality doesn't matter."**
Orthogonality tolerates *small* nuisance errors. If $\hat g$ and $\hat m$ are
badly wrong (garbage out-of-sample $R^2$), regularization bias returns and
$\hat\theta$ is biased. Check the nuisance out-of-sample $R^2$ before trusting
$\theta$. §9.2.

**"More folds is always better."**
$K = 5$ is standard; very large $K$ costs compute for little gain, and $K = 2$
(the original "cross-fitting" split) works with a sample-size penalty. Repeat
over a few random fold splits and average to reduce split-noise.
