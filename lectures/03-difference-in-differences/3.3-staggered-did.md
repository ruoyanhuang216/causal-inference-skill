# 3.3 — Staggered DiD: The TWFE Trap and Its Fixes

> **Part of:** [Lecture 3 — Difference-in-Differences](./README.md)
> **Read when:** units adopt treatment at *different times* — the single most common way a competent analyst produces a confidently wrong number.

This is the longest chapter in the lecture, and it earns it: variation in
treatment timing is where the reflexive DiD regression silently breaks,
and the fix spawned a whole literature of estimators. The arc is one story:
**frame the failure (§1–4), state the one rule that repairs it (§5), pick
the right tool for your situation (§6), then the tools in detail (§7).**

---

## 1. The setup that looks harmless

A PM rolls a feature out in waves: Cohort A in January, Cohort B in March,
Cohort C in June. You have panel data on everyone. The reflexive move — and
it's what almost everyone reaches for — is **two-way fixed effects (TWFE)**:

```math
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta D_{it} + \epsilon_{it}
```

Unit fixed effects $\alpha_i$, time fixed effects $\lambda_t$, a single
treatment dummy $D_{it}$ that flips on when unit $i$'s wave arrives. Read
$\beta$ as "the effect." Ship.

**This is wrong, and not subtly.** Under staggered timing with effects that
vary — across cohorts or over time since treatment — $\beta$ can be biased,
and in realistic cases it **flips sign**: a genuinely positive treatment
reports as negative. The discovery (Goodman-Bacon 2021, and the literature
it launched) rewrote applied DiD.

---

## 2. What TWFE is actually computing: the Goodman-Bacon decomposition

Goodman-Bacon's result: that single $\beta$ is not a clean effect. It is a
**weighted average of every possible 2×2 DiD** you can build from the data.
With early adopters, late adopters, and a never-treated group, TWFE silently
runs all four of these comparisons and averages them:

| # | Comparison (treatment vs. control) | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Early adopters vs. Never-treated** | ✅ Good |
| 2 | **Late adopters vs. Never-treated** | ✅ Good |
| 3 | **Early adopters vs. Late adopters** — *before the late group is treated* | ✅ Good — the late group is a valid not-yet-treated control |
| 4 | **Late adopters vs. Early adopters** — *after the early group is treated* | ❌ **BAD — the Forbidden Comparison** |

Comparisons 1 and 2 are textbook DiD: a treated cohort against a clean,
never-treated control. Comparison 3 is also fine — before the late cohort
gets its treatment, it's a legitimate *not-yet-treated* control for the
early cohort.

**Comparison 4 is the poison.** When TWFE evaluates the late cohort against
the early cohort in a period where the **early cohort is already treated**,
it is using *treated units as the control group*. The early cohort's outcome
is no longer a clean $Y(0)$ counterfactual — it already contains the early
cohort's treatment effect. Subtracting a contaminated control injects that
effect, with the wrong sign, into the late cohort's estimate.

The reason this is invisible is that TWFE never *shows* you the four
comparisons. It hands back one number, and comparison 4 is folded silently
inside it — sometimes with a **negative weight** (§3).

---

## 3. The negative-weighting trap, concretely

Here is the mechanism, in the form it actually bites.

The UK gets a feature in January. It works, and its effect **grows over
time** — revenue climbs steadily for six months (a *dynamic* treatment
effect, the completely normal case). France gets the same feature in March.

Now TWFE forms the forbidden comparison (#4 above): **France in April vs.
the UK in April**, using the UK as control. But the UK's April revenue is
still climbing from its *January* treatment. So the "control" is rising fast
— and against a rising control, France's perfectly good March launch looks
like a *relative decline*. TWFE records that as a negative contribution.

Goodman-Bacon showed these forbidden comparisons can receive **negative
weights** in the overall average. Combine negative weights with dynamic
effects and the sum can invert the sign of the true effect entirely.

**The bias is worst exactly when the treatment is a growing success** —
which is the situation you most want to measure correctly. A feature whose
effect builds over time (adoption, network effects, habit formation) is the
textbook trigger.

---

## 4. Verified: the sign actually flips

Not a cautionary tale — a simulation (code in §9). 100 units, two cohorts
(early adopts at t=2, late at t=12), 20 periods. The true effect is
**positive and grows** at 0.5 per period since treatment. Averaged over all
treated observations, the **true ATT is +3.98**.

```
  True ATT (average over treated observations):  +3.98
  TWFE beta_D:                                    −1.24
  Bias:                                           −5.22   (−131%)
```

**A treatment with a true effect of +3.98 reports as −1.24.** Not
attenuated — *reversed*. A PM reading that TWFE output concludes the feature
*hurts* revenue and kills a genuine winner.

Push the dynamics steeper (effect grows at 1.0/period, true ATT +7.96) and
TWFE returns **−2.46** — the gap only widens. The bias scales with how
dynamic the effect is, which is the opposite of reassuring.

And the fix works: a clean **not-yet-treated** comparison — the early
cohort's change measured against the late cohort *while the late cohort is
still untreated* (comparison 3) — recovers ATT(g=2, t=5) = **2.006** against
a true **2.0**. The truth was always in the data. TWFE just averaged it with
poison.

---

## 5. The one rule that fixes everything

**Never let an already-treated unit serve as a control.** That's it. Every
estimator in §6–7 is a different implementation of that single sentence —
they differ in *how* they enforce it and *what treatment patterns* they can
handle, not in the goal.

Before switching estimators, you can **diagnose how bad your TWFE actually
is** with the Goodman-Bacon decomposition itself. It reports what fraction
of $\beta$ came from each of the four comparison types and how much weight —
including negative weight — the forbidden comparison (#4) carried:

- **Small forbidden share** (a large never-treated group, short panels, few
  already-treated controls) → plain TWFE may be nearly fine.
- **Large forbidden share** → your estimate is built on poison, and a robust
  estimator is mandatory, not optional.

Run the decomposition first (`bacondecomp` in R, `bacon` via `pip install
bacondecomp`). It tells you whether you have a problem before you invest in
the fix.

## 6. Choosing an estimator: use-case first

There is no single "best" modern estimator — there's a best one *for your
situation*. The selection turns on four questions, and the first is the one
the junior analyst always skips (§8):

```
  Q1. Is treatment ABSORBING (once on, always on)?
        NO  ───────────────────────────────► de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfœuille
        │                                     (§7.4) — the ONLY one that
        │                                     handles toggling treatment
        │ YES
        ▼
  Q2. Is the deliverable an EVENT-STUDY plot to prove parallel trends?
        YES ───────────────────────────────► Sun-Abraham (§7.3)
        │
        ▼
  Q3. Do you need MAXIMUM efficiency / tight CIs, with a big untreated pool?
        YES ───────────────────────────────► Borusyak et al. / Gardner (§7.5)
        │                                     (imputation)
        ▼
  Q4. Do you need to stay in OLS (SQL/PySpark, time-varying covariates)?
        YES ───────────────────────────────► Wooldridge ETWFE (§7.2)
        NO / default ──────────────────────► Callaway-Sant'Anna (§7.1)
                                              — the transparent gold standard
```

The consolidated trade-off table:

| Estimator | Core idea | Best when | Breaks when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Callaway-Sant'Anna** (§7.1) | Group-time 2×2, explicit clean control | Standard rollout, absorbing treatment, want transparency | Treatment toggles on/off |
| **Wooldridge ETWFE** (§7.2) | Saturated OLS interactions | You need OLS + SQL/PySpark, time-varying covariates | Treatment toggles on/off |
| **Sun-Abraham** (§7.3) | Cohort × relative-time, then average | The deliverable is a clean event-study chart | Treatment toggles on/off |
| **de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfœuille** (§7.4) | Switchers vs. stayers | **Non-absorbing** / toggling treatment | — (this *is* the toggling fix) |
| **Borusyak et al. / Gardner** (§7.5) | Impute $Y(0)$ from untreated cells | Need max efficiency, big untreated pool | $Y(0)$ model misspecified |

**Practical guidance (and it's the correct instinct):** run more than one.
Report a robust estimator *alongside* TWFE so the delta is visible. Always
present an event study with pre-treatment coefficients. If two robust
estimators disagree, that disagreement is a finding — usually about your
control-group definition or your $Y(0)$ specification.

## 7. The estimators in detail

### 7.1 Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) — the group-time gold standard

**Idea:** refuse to run one regression. Instead compute a clean 2×2 for
every cohort $g$ (defined by *when* they were first treated) and every time
$t$, always against an uncontaminated control.

```math
ATT(g, t) = \underbrace{E\big[Y_t - Y_{g-1} \mid G = g\big]}_{\text{cohort } g\text{'s change from its base period}} - \underbrace{E\big[Y_t - Y_{g-1} \mid C\big]}_{\text{clean control's change}}
```

- $G = g$: units first treated at time $g$.
- $Y_{g-1}$: the **base period** — strictly the period before $g$ was
  treated, so it's a genuine pre-treatment level.
- $C$: the control, which you must **explicitly choose** — either the
  **never-treated** ($D_{it} = 0$ for all $t$) or the **not-yet-treated**
  (units whose treatment hasn't started by $t$). Never the already-treated.

**Then aggregate**, whichever way the decision needs:

- **Event-study:** average $ATT(g,t)$ over all $(g,t)$ with $t - g = e$ →
  the effect at $e$ periods since treatment, across cohorts.
- **Overall ATT:** cohort-size-weighted average of all post-treatment
  $ATT(g,t)$.

**Why it's the default:** non-parametric (no functional form to
misspecify), a doubly-robust option, and — the underrated part — it *forces
you to name your control group*. That single explicit decision prevents most
staggered-DiD errors. In this repo's verified example (§4), the manual
$ATT(g{=}2, t{=}5)$ using not-yet-treated controls returned **2.006** vs. a
true **2.0**, while TWFE returned −1.24. Package: `did` (R), `csdid` /
`differences` (Python).

### 7.2 Wooldridge (2021) — Extended TWFE (ETWFE)

**Idea:** you don't have to leave regression-land. Just *saturate* the model
— interact every cohort with every period — and OLS becomes mathematically
incapable of forming the forbidden comparison.

```math
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \sum_{g \in \mathcal{G}} \sum_{t \geq g} \beta_{g,t} \, \big(\mathbb{1}[G_i = g] \times \mathbb{1}[\text{Time} = t]\big) + X_{it}\gamma + \epsilon_{it}
```

Each $\beta_{g,t}$ estimates $ATT(g,t)$ **directly** — the same quantity as
Callaway-Sant'Anna, recovered by OLS. By giving every cohort-time cell its
own coefficient, the regression can't collapse them into a single
contaminated $\beta$.

**Why industry likes it:** it's *just OLS*. Runs in raw SQL / PySpark with no
specialized causal package, plugs into whatever clustered-SE machinery you
already have, and makes **time-varying covariates** $X_{it}$ trivial to add.
Mentally: an A/B test for every cohort, inside one regression. Package:
`etwfe` (R), or hand-rolled interactions anywhere.

### 7.3 Sun & Abraham (2021) — clean event studies

**Idea:** specifically repairs the **event-study plot** under staggered
timing. The problem it targets: in a naive TWFE event study, the *pre-trend*
coefficients — your parallel-trends placebos — get **contaminated** by other
cohorts' *post-treatment* effects. Your falsification test is corrupted by
the very thing it's supposed to detect.

Two steps:

```math
\textbf{1.}\quad Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \sum_{g}\sum_{e \neq -1} \mu_{g,e} \, \big(\mathbb{1}[G_i = g] \times \mathbb{1}[t - g = e]\big) + \epsilon_{it}
```

estimating a cohort-specific effect $\mu_{g,e}$ (CATT) for each cohort $g$ at
relative time $e$. Then average by cohort share:

```math
\textbf{2.}\quad ATT_e = \sum_g w_g^e \, \hat{\mu}_{g,e}
```

By isolating $\mu_{g,e}$ *before* averaging, the pre-period coefficients stay
pure. **Reach for this when the deliverable is an unassailable event-study
chart** to prove parallel trends to executives. Package:
`eventstudyinteract` (Stata), `fixest::sunab` (R).

### 7.4 de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfœuille (2020) — the switcher estimator

**Idea:** everything above assumes **absorbing** treatment — once treated,
always treated. The moment treatment can turn **on and off**, Callaway-
Sant'Anna and Wooldridge break, because "cohort defined by first-treatment
time" stops being meaningful. This estimator handles fluid treatment by
comparing **switchers** to **stayers** across single time boundaries.

```math
DID_{M,t} = \sum_{g} \frac{N_{g,t}}{N_{+,t}} \big( \Delta Y_{g,t} - \Delta Y_{0,t} \big)
```

- $\Delta Y_{g,t}$: change from $t-1$ to $t$ for units that **switched**
  treatment state at $t$.
- $\Delta Y_{0,t}$: change over the same boundary for units that **stayed**
  untreated.
- $N$: sample sizes, used as weights.

It pairs up units transitioning states across one time step, so it's robust
to units toggling in and out — subscription pauses, feature flags flipped on
and off, campaigns started and stopped. Package: `did_multiplegt` /
`DIDmultiplegtDYN`.

### 7.5 Imputation — Borusyak-Jaravel-Spiess (2024) & Gardner (2022)

**Idea:** a clean break from "find a control group." Instead, learn the
untreated potential outcome $Y(0)$ from **all the untreated cells** and
impute what the treated units would have done.

```math
\textbf{1.}\quad \hat{Y}_{it}(0) = \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\lambda}_t + X_{it}\hat{\beta} \quad \text{estimated on } D_{it} = 0 \text{ observations ONLY}
```

```math
\textbf{2.}\quad \hat{\tau}_{it} = Y_{it}^{\text{actual}} - \hat{Y}_{it}(0) \quad \text{for treated cells, then average}
```

**Borusyak-Jaravel-Spiess** do this in one efficient imputation step;
**Gardner (did2s)** does it as an explicit two-stage regression (partial out
fixed effects on untreated data, then estimate on the residuals).

**Why it wins on precision:** it pools *all* untreated data to learn the time
trends, rather than fracturing into tiny 2×2 blocks like CS. That pooling
gives **tighter confidence intervals** — it's the most *efficient* of the
family. Conceptually it's the panel-data cousin of CausalImpact ([Lecture 2
§2.4](../02-experimentation/2.4-geo-experiments.md)). The cost: efficiency
comes from a correctly specified $Y(0)$ model, so it leans harder on that
specification than the design-based CS does. Package: `did_imputation` /
`didimputation`, `did2s`.

## 8. Worked probe: the AdTech auto-bidder

**Setup.** A major AdTech platform tests a new **Auto-Bidding** algorithm.
Advertisers can **turn it on whenever they want — try it a week, turn it
off, turn it back on a month later.** You want its causal effect on ROAS. A
junior DS proposes **Callaway-Sant'Anna**, citing a blog post that "TWFE is
dead, CS is the modern gold standard."

### 8.1 Why the junior is wrong

**Callaway-Sant'Anna assumes absorbing treatment, and this treatment isn't
absorbing.** CS is built entirely on the concept of a **cohort** $g$ = *the
time a unit was first treated*, and once treated, always treated. That
definition is the load-bearing beam of the whole method — the base period
$Y_{g-1}$, the never/not-yet-treated control, the group-time $ATT(g,t)$ all
presuppose it.

Here an advertiser is on in week 1, off in week 3, on again in week 8.
**What is their cohort $g$?** The question has no answer. There's no "first
and only" treatment time, so:

- $Y_{g-1}$ (the base period) is undefined — which "before" is *the* before?
- The not-yet-treated control pool is incoherent — a unit that's currently
  off was treated last month, so it's a contaminated control, exactly the §3
  poison CS was built to avoid.

The junior pattern-matched "staggered → CS" without checking the treatment
*structure*. "TWFE is dead" is true; "CS is therefore always the answer" is
the actual error. **The absorbing-vs-non-absorbing question (Q1 in §6) comes
before naming any estimator.**

### 8.2 The pivot: de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfœuille

Use **$DID_M$** (§7.4). It's the one estimator in the family built for
**non-absorbing** treatment. It doesn't need a coherent cohort — it works off
*transitions*, comparing advertisers who **switched** auto-bidding state at a
given boundary against those who **stayed** put. Because it only ever
compares a single-step transition to a contemporaneous stayer, constant
toggling is fine — that's precisely the design target.

**Staff-level framing:** *"The blog post is right that naive TWFE is biased
under staggered adoption. But CS and the other cohort-based estimators assume
treatment is absorbing — once on, always on. Our advertisers toggle
auto-bidding freely, so 'cohort' is undefined and CS's clean-control logic
collapses. For non-absorbing treatment we use de Chaisemartin-
D'Haultfœuille's switcher estimator, which compares state-changers to
state-holders at each time step and is robust to arbitrary toggling."*

## 9. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# ---- Reproduce the sign flip ----
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
T = 20
cohorts = {"early": (2, range(50)), "late": (12, range(50, 100))}
rows = []
for name, (g, units) in cohorts.items():
    for i in units:
        ui = rng.normal(0, 0.3)                          # unit fixed effect
        for t in range(T):
            treated = t >= g
            te = 0.5 * (t - g + 1) if treated else 0.0   # DYNAMIC: grows over time
            y = ui + 0.1 * t + te + rng.normal(0, 0.2)
            rows.append(dict(unit=i, t=t, g=g, D=int(treated), Y=y, tau=te))
df = pd.DataFrame(rows)

true_att = df.loc[df.D == 1, "tau"].mean()
twfe = smf.ols("Y ~ C(unit) + C(t) + D", data=df).fit().params["D"]
print(f"true ATT = {true_att:+.2f}   TWFE = {twfe:+.2f}   bias = {twfe-true_att:+.2f}")
# true ATT = +3.98   TWFE = -1.24   bias = -5.22   <-- SIGN FLIP

# ---- Diagnose the forbidden-comparison weight BEFORE switching estimators ----
# pip install bacondecomp ; from bacondecomp import bacon
# bacon(df, "Y", "D", id_var="unit", time_var="t")
# The "Later vs Earlier Treated" row is comparison #4 -- the poison. If its
# weight is large (esp. negative), plain TWFE is untrustworthy.

# ---- A clean not-yet-treated comparison (comparison #3) recovers the truth ----
def att_gt(df, g, t, base):
    treat = df[df.g == g]
    d_treat = (treat[treat.t == t].set_index("unit").Y
               - treat[treat.t == base].set_index("unit").Y).mean()
    ctrl = df[df.g > t]                          # NOT-YET-treated at time t
    d_ctrl = (ctrl[ctrl.t == t].set_index("unit").Y
              - ctrl[ctrl.t == base].set_index("unit").Y).mean()
    return d_treat - d_ctrl

print(f"ATT(g=2,t=5) clean = {att_gt(df, 2, 5, base=1):.3f}  (true 2.0)")
# ATT(g=2,t=5) clean = 2.006  (true 2.0)

# ---- Production packages ----
# Callaway-Sant'Anna : R `did`, Python `csdid` / `differences`
# Wooldridge ETWFE   : R `etwfe`, or hand-rolled cohort x time interactions
# Sun-Abraham        : R `fixest::sunab`, Stata `eventstudyinteract`
# dCDH (non-absorb.) : `did_multiplegt` / `DIDmultiplegtDYN`
# Imputation         : `did_imputation` / `didimputation`, `did2s`
```

## 10. Common misreadings

**"TWFE with unit and time fixed effects is the standard, so it's safe."**
Safe with a *single* treatment time. With staggered timing and any effect
heterogeneity it silently forms the forbidden comparison (#4) and can flip
signs. §2–4.

**"My β is positive, so heterogeneity bias isn't a problem for me."**
A positive β under staggered timing might be a *biased-downward* version of a
much larger true effect — or a coincidence of weights. Decompose (§5) before
trusting a right-looking sign.

**"Staggered timing just means more data, which is better."**
Timing variation is what *creates* the forbidden comparisons. It's the
hazard, not extra safety — the README §3 lesson: the danger axis is timing,
not sample size.

**"The bias is small, a few percent."**
The verified example is −131% and reverses the sign. The bias scales with how
dynamic the effect is, and growing-over-time effects are the norm. §4.

**"TWFE is dead, so always use Callaway-Sant'Anna."**
CS assumes *absorbing* treatment. For toggling treatment it's as broken as
TWFE — use de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfœuille. Ask "absorbing or not?" *first*
(§6 Q1, §8).

**"These robust estimators all give the same answer, so pick any."**
They target subtly different aggregations and lean on different assumptions
(design-based vs. model-based $Y(0)$). Disagreement between them is
diagnostic, not noise. §6.

**"ETWFE is just old TWFE, which we know is biased."**
The *saturation* is the whole point — full cohort × time interactions block
the forbidden comparison. Plain TWFE has one $\beta$; ETWFE has one per cell.
§7.2.

**"Imputation is best because its CIs are tightest."**
Tight CIs come from leaning on the $Y(0)$ model. If that model is
misspecified, you get a precise wrong answer. Efficiency and robustness trade
off. §7.5.

**"My event study looks clean, so parallel trends holds — even under TWFE."**
A naive TWFE event study contaminates pre-period coefficients with other
cohorts' post-effects (Sun-Abraham). Use Sun-Abraham or CS for the plot.
§7.3.
