## 1. The design

Two groups, two periods. One group gets treated between the periods; the
other never does. This is the atom of DiD, and — with a believable
control — one of the most defensible designs in observational causal
inference. It's also the industry default, because we constantly launch
in one market and hold back another.

The intuition is subtraction, done twice (README §1): difference each
market against its own past to kill its fixed level, then difference the
two changes to kill the common trend. What survives is the ATT.

---

## 2. The regression, coefficient by coefficient

The 2×2 is one OLS regression:

```math
Y_{it} = \alpha + \beta \, \text{Treat}_i + \gamma \, \text{Post}_t + \delta \, (\text{Treat}_i \times \text{Post}_t) + \epsilon_{it}
```

Four coefficients, and each is one cell of the 2×2 table. Knowing exactly
what each one *is* is the difference between running DiD and understanding
it:

| Coef | Is | In the Miami/Orlando example |
|---|---|---|
| $\alpha$ | Control group, pre-period mean | Orlando's rides in May |
| $\beta$ | Baseline treatment−control gap | How much bigger Miami is than Orlando, pre-launch |
| $\gamma$ | Control group's over-time change | How much Orlando grew May→June on its own |
| $\delta$ | **The DiD estimate (ATT)** | Miami's lift *above* its baseline gap *and* the natural June trend |

$\delta$ is the whole point. It is the interaction, and it recovers the
2×2 table exactly:

```math
\hat{\delta} = (\bar{Y}_{T,\text{post}} - \bar{Y}_{T,\text{pre}}) - (\bar{Y}_{C,\text{post}} - \bar{Y}_{C,\text{pre}})
```

$\beta$ and $\gamma$ are not nuisances to be ignored — they are the two
confounders DiD is explicitly subtracting. $\beta$ absorbs the
time-invariant level difference (Miami is just bigger). $\gamma$ absorbs
the common shock (June is summer everywhere). **$\delta$ is what's left
after both are removed**, and it's causal *only if* removing exactly those
two things was enough — which is parallel trends (README §2).

## 3. Geo-DiD: the industry version

In practice you rarely have one clean control city. You have one treated
market and a pool of untreated ones.

**The simple approach:** let $\text{Treat}_i = 0$ cover *all* untreated
cities and run the same regression. The control is now the pooled average
of, say, ten cities. More control units, less noise, same estimator.

**The limitation, and it's fatal when it bites:** the simple average of
ten cities may have a completely different growth trajectory than your
treated city. If Miami was growing 5% month-over-month pre-launch and the
ten-city average was growing 2%, parallel trends is dead before you
start — the control is drifting away from the treatment even without any
intervention, and $\delta$ will pick up that drift and call it an effect.

This limitation is exactly what **synthetic control** (§3.2) fixes:
instead of averaging the ten cities equally, *weight* them so the blend
tracks the treated city's pre-trend. Geo-DiD is the special case where all
the weights are forced equal. Hold that thought — §3.2 picks it up
directly.

## 4. Diagnostics: defending an assumption you can't test

You cannot prove parallel trends. You *can* assemble a defense that would
survive a staff-level review. Here is the playbook, and the order matters.

### 4.1 The event study (leads and lags)

Don't just run the 2×2. Estimate a separate effect for **every period**
relative to treatment, with unit and time fixed effects:

```math
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \sum_{k \neq -1} \delta_k \, (\text{Treat}_i \times \mathbb{1}[t - t^* = k]) + \epsilon_{it}
```

The period just before treatment ($k = -1$) is the omitted baseline, so
every $\delta_k$ is measured relative to it. Then plot the $\delta_k$:

```
   δ_k
    │                                    ●
    │                              ●
    │                        ●              ← post: the treatment effect,
    │                  ●                      ideally rising after k=0
  0 ┼──●────●────●──○──────────────────────
    │  ●    ●    ●  ↑                       ← pre: MUST be flat at zero
    │            (k=-1, omitted)
    └──┬────┬────┬────┬────┬────┬────┬──── relative time k
      -4   -3   -2   -1    0    1    2
```

- **Pre-treatment ($k < 0$): the coefficients must be statistically
  zero.** If they aren't, treatment and control were already diverging
  before anything happened. Parallel trends is violated and $\delta$ is
  not causal.
- **Post-treatment ($k \geq 0$): the shape is your result.** A jump that
  then flattens is a level effect; a steady climb is a dynamic effect
  (remember this for §3.3 — dynamic effects are what break staggered
  TWFE).

**The event study is the single most important DiD chart.** It is what
you put in front of executives, and it is what a good reviewer asks for
first.

### 4.2 Placebo tests

- **Placebo in time.** Pretend treatment happened earlier, in a window
  where nothing actually did. Run the same DiD. $\delta$ should be zero —
  a nonzero placebo effect means your design manufactures effects from
  ordinary variation.
- **Placebo in space.** Keep the real timing, but assign "treatment" to a
  random untreated city. $\delta$ should be zero. If a city that got
  nothing shows an effect, your control pool isn't clean.

### 4.3 The trap the diagnostics can't catch

Everything above tests a *consequence* of parallel trends. None of it
tests the assumption (README §2). Roth (2022): pre-trend tests are often
underpowered against exactly the slope violations that bias you most, so
"pre-trends look flat" can coexist with a badly biased $\delta$. Present
the diagnostics as a defense that raises your credibility — never as a
QED.

## 5. Worked example: Miami, Orlando, and the tourist spike

**Setup.** Ops launches a driver-incentive program in **Miami** on June 1.
They use **Orlando** as control, run a 2×2 over **May (pre) / June (post)**,
and get a large, highly significant positive $\delta$. They want to roll
out nationwide.

You know something they don't: **Miami is a heavy tourist market that
spikes hard at the start of June for summer vacation; Orlando's tourism is
spread evenly across the year.**

### 5.1 Which assumption is violated?

**Parallel trends.** Full stop — and it's worth being precise, because the
tempting answer (SUTVA) is wrong.

Absent the incentive program, Miami's rides were going to jump in June
anyway, because of tourists. Orlando's weren't. So the two markets would
*not* have changed by the same amount even with no treatment:

```math
\underbrace{E[\Delta Y(0) \mid \text{Miami}]}_{\text{big June tourist jump}} \;\neq\; \underbrace{E[\Delta Y(0) \mid \text{Orlando}]}_{\text{flat}}
```

The DiD attributes Miami's entire excess jump to the program, when the
tourist surge is doing the work. $\delta$ is biased **upward**, and the
program looks like a triumph when it may have done little.

**Why it's not SUTVA.** SUTVA is about *spillover* — treatment in Miami
leaking into Orlando. That's not what's happening. Nothing about Miami's
program touches Orlando; the two markets are simply on different natural
trajectories. This is a **differential-trend** problem, which is precisely
what parallel trends rules out and what SUTVA has nothing to say about.
Conflating them (as it's tempting to do) misdiagnoses the fix.

### 5.2 What chart proves it? — the subtle part

The instinct is "show them the event study" (§4.1). **You can't.** Look at
the design: **one pre-period (May) and one post-period (June).** An event
study needs *multiple* pre-periods to plot pre-trend coefficients — with a
single pre-period, there are no leads to test. **A 2×2 has exactly zero
testable pre-trends.** This is a genuine and underappreciated limitation:
the most-cited DiD diagnostic is unavailable in the most common DiD
design.

So you go to the diagnostic that *is* available: **placebo in time using
prior years.** Take the same May→June transition from years with no
program, and run the identical DiD:

```
   Historical DiD (May → June), no program in any of these years:
     2021:  δ = +18%   ← Miami's tourist jump, mislabeled as "effect"
     2022:  δ = +21%
     2023:  δ = +19%
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────
   2024 (actual, with program):  δ = +22%
```

**The placebo δ's are large and positive every year — with no treatment
whatsoever.** That is the proof: the "effect" the Ops team is celebrating
is Miami's recurring seasonal pattern. The true incremental lift is at
most the 2024 excess over the historical baseline (+22% − ~+19% ≈ +3%),
not the +22% they're about to bet the national rollout on.

This is your answer's structure exactly: *"even in years with no
treatment, this regression produces a large positive δ, because Miami
always jumps in June. The design cannot separate the program from the
tourists."* It's the right call, and the reason it's right — not "run an
event study" but "run a historical placebo, because the event study
doesn't exist in a 2-period design" — is the staff-level distinction.

### 5.3 How to fix it

- **Better control.** Swap Orlando for a market that *shares Miami's
  tourist seasonality* — another summer-vacation beach market. Then the
  June jump is common to both and differences out.
- **Synthetic control** (§3.2). Weight a donor pool to match Miami's
  pre-period trajectory including its seasonality.
- **More pre-periods.** Collect a year of monthly data so an event study
  becomes possible and the seasonality is visible in the pre-trend.
- **Triple differences** (§3.5) if a within-market group is exposed to the
  seasonality but not the program.

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# panel: unit, t, Y, plus Treat (group) and Post (time) indicators

# ---- The 2x2. delta is the ATT. ----
did = smf.ols("Y ~ Treat * Post", data=df).fit(
    cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["unit"]}
)
print(f"delta (ATT) = {did.params['Treat:Post']:.3f}")
# The interaction 'Treat:Post' is delta. 'Treat' is beta, 'Post' is gamma.

# ---- Geo-DiD: Treat=0 covers the whole untreated pool, same estimator ----
# (identical call; Treat just has more 0s)

# ---- Event study. REQUIRES multiple pre-periods -- a 2x2 cannot do this. ----
df["k"] = df["t"] - df["treat_time"]          # relative time
df.loc[~df["ever_treated"], "k"] = -1          # controls pinned to baseline
es = smf.ols(
    "Y ~ C(unit) + C(t) + C(k, Treatment(reference=-1)):ever_treated",
    data=df,
).fit(cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["unit"]})
# Plot the C(k):ever_treated coefficients. Pre-period (k<0) must be ~0.

# ---- Placebo in time: the ONLY pre-trend check a 2-period design allows ----
def placebo_in_time(history, fake_treat_year):
    """Run the identical May->June DiD on a year with NO treatment.
    A large delta here = the design manufactures effects from seasonality."""
    yr = history[history.year == fake_treat_year]
    return smf.ols("Y ~ Treat * Post", data=yr).fit().params["Treat:Post"]

for year in [2021, 2022, 2023]:
    print(f"placebo {year}: delta = {placebo_in_time(hist, year):+.3f}")
# If these are large and positive with no treatment, the real delta is suspect.
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"δ is significant and positive, so the program works."**
Significance is not identification. A significant δ under violated
parallel trends is a precisely-estimated wrong number. The Miami placebos
are all significant, and all spurious.

**"The tourist problem is a SUTVA violation."**
No — SUTVA is about spillover between units. Miami and Orlando being on
different natural trajectories is a *parallel-trends* violation. Different
diagnosis, different fix. §5.1.

**"Just run the event study to check pre-trends."**
Not in a 2×2 — you have one pre-period and zero testable leads. Go to a
historical placebo instead. This distinction is the whole staff-level
point of §5.2.

**"Pre-trends are flat, so parallel trends holds."**
Pre-trends ≠ parallel trends (Roth 2022). Flat pre-trends are necessary,
not sufficient, and the test is underpowered against the violations that
matter. §4.3.

**"More control cities always help."**
Only if they share the treated unit's trend. Ten cities averaged into a
trajectory that drifts from Miami's is *worse* than one well-matched city.
That's the door to §3.2.

**"DiD gives the ATE."**
It gives the **ATT** — the effect on the treated market. Miami's response
need not generalize to markets that never adopted, which is exactly the
leap the Ops team wants to make with the national rollout.
