# 3.4 — Modern Staggered-DiD Estimators

> **Part of:** [Lecture 3 — Difference-in-Differences](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§3.3](./3.3-staggered-twfe-trap.md) — you must know *why* TWFE breaks before choosing a fix.

§3.3 proved TWFE can flip signs under staggered timing. This chapter is
the toolbox that fixes it. **Every estimator here implements one rule:
never use an already-treated unit as a control.** They differ in *how* —
and, crucially, in *what treatment patterns they can handle*. Getting the
selection right (§7) matters more than mastering any single one.

---

## 1. Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) — the group-time gold standard

**Idea:** refuse to run one regression. Instead compute a clean 2×2 for
every cohort $g$ (defined by *when* they were first treated) and every time
$t$, always against an uncontaminated control.

```math
ATT(g, t) = \underbrace{E\big[Y_t - Y_{g-1} \mid G = g\big]}_{\text{cohort } g\text{'s change from its base period}} - \underbrace{E\big[Y_t - Y_{g-1} \mid C\big]}_{\text{clean control's change}}
```

- $G = g$: units first treated at time $g$.
- $Y_{g-1}$: the **base period** — strictly the period before $g$ was
  treated, so it's a genuine pre-treatment level.
- $C$: the control, which you must **explicitly choose** — either the
  **never-treated** ($D_{it} = 0$ for all $t$) or the **not-yet-treated**
  (units whose treatment hasn't started by $t$). Never the already-treated.

**Then aggregate**, whichever way the decision needs:

- **Event-study:** average $ATT(g,t)$ over all $(g,t)$ with $t - g = e$ →
  the effect at $e$ periods since treatment, across cohorts. Builds an
  honest leads-and-lags plot.
- **Overall ATT:** cohort-size-weighted average of all post-treatment
  $ATT(g,t)$.

**Why it's the default:** non-parametric (no functional form to
misspecify), a doubly-robust option, and — the underrated part — it
*forces you to name your control group*. That single design decision,
made explicit, prevents most staggered-DiD errors. Package: `did` (R),
`csdid` / `differences` (Python).

**In this repo's verified example** (§3.3): the manual $ATT(g{=}2, t{=}5)$
using not-yet-treated controls returned **2.006** against a true **2.0**,
while TWFE returned −1.24. This is that estimator.

## 2. Wooldridge (2021) — Extended TWFE (ETWFE)

**Idea:** you don't have to leave regression-land. Just *saturate* the
model — interact every cohort with every period — and OLS becomes
mathematically incapable of forming the forbidden comparison.

```math
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \sum_{g \in \mathcal{G}} \sum_{t \geq g} \beta_{g,t} \, \big(\mathbb{1}[G_i = g] \times \mathbb{1}[\text{Time} = t]\big) + X_{it}\gamma + \epsilon_{it}
```

Each $\beta_{g,t}$ estimates $ATT(g,t)$ **directly** — the same quantity as
Callaway-Sant'Anna, recovered by OLS. By giving every cohort-time cell its
own coefficient, the regression can't collapse them into a single
contaminated $\beta$.

**Why industry likes it:** it's *just OLS*. Runs in raw SQL / PySpark with
no specialized causal package, plugs into whatever clustered-SE machinery
you already have, and makes **time-varying covariates** $X_{it}$ trivial to
add. Mentally: an A/B test for every cohort, inside one regression.
Package: `etwfe` (R), or hand-rolled interactions anywhere.

## 3. Sun & Abraham (2021) — clean event studies

**Idea:** specifically repairs the **event-study plot** under staggered
timing. The problem it targets: in a naive TWFE event study, the
*pre-trend* coefficients — your parallel-trends placebos — get
**contaminated** by other cohorts' *post-treatment* effects. Your
falsification test is corrupted by the very thing it's supposed to detect.

Two steps:

```math
\textbf{1.}\quad Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \sum_{g}\sum_{e \neq -1} \mu_{g,e} \, \big(\mathbb{1}[G_i = g] \times \mathbb{1}[t - g = e]\big) + \epsilon_{it}
```

estimating a cohort-specific effect $\mu_{g,e}$ (CATT) for each cohort $g$
at relative time $e$. Then average by cohort share:

```math
\textbf{2.}\quad ATT_e = \sum_g w_g^e \, \hat{\mu}_{g,e}
```

By isolating $\mu_{g,e}$ *before* averaging, the pre-period coefficients
stay pure. **Reach for this when the deliverable is an unassailable
event-study chart** to prove parallel trends to executives. Package:
`eventstudyinteract` (Stata), `fixest::sunab` (R).

## 4. de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfœuille (2020) — the switcher estimator

**Idea:** everything above assumes **absorbing** treatment — once treated,
always treated. The moment treatment can turn **on and off**, Callaway-
Sant'Anna and Wooldridge break, because "cohort defined by first-treatment
time" stops being meaningful. This estimator handles fluid treatment by
comparing **switchers** to **stayers** across single time boundaries.

```math
DID_{M,t} = \sum_{g} \frac{N_{g,t}}{N_{+,t}} \big( \Delta Y_{g,t} - \Delta Y_{0,t} \big)
```

- $\Delta Y_{g,t}$: change from $t-1$ to $t$ for units that **switched**
  treatment state at $t$.
- $\Delta Y_{0,t}$: change over the same boundary for units that **stayed**
  untreated.
- $N$: sample sizes, used as weights.

It pairs up units transitioning states across one time step, so it's
robust to units toggling in and out — subscription pauses, feature flags
flipped on and off, campaigns started and stopped. Package: `did_multiplegt`
/ `DIDmultiplegtDYN`.

## 5. Imputation — Borusyak-Jaravel-Spiess (2024) & Gardner (2022)

**Idea:** a clean break from "find a control group." Instead, learn the
untreated potential outcome $Y(0)$ from **all the untreated cells** and
impute what the treated units would have done.

```math
\textbf{1.}\quad \hat{Y}_{it}(0) = \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\lambda}_t + X_{it}\hat{\beta} \quad \text{estimated on } D_{it} = 0 \text{ observations ONLY}
```

```math
\textbf{2.}\quad \hat{\tau}_{it} = Y_{it}^{\text{actual}} - \hat{Y}_{it}(0) \quad \text{for treated cells, then average}
```

**Borusyak-Jaravel-Spiess** do this in one efficient imputation step;
**Gardner (did2s)** does it as an explicit two-stage regression (partial
out fixed effects on untreated data, then estimate on the residuals).

**Why it wins on precision:** it pools *all* untreated data to learn the
time trends, rather than fracturing into tiny 2×2 blocks like CS. That
pooling gives **tighter confidence intervals** — it's the most *efficient*
of the family. Conceptually it's the panel-data cousin of CausalImpact
([Lecture 2 §2.4](../02-experimentation/2.4-geo-experiments.md)). The
cost: efficiency comes from a correctly specified $Y(0)$ model, so it leans
harder on that specification than the design-based CS does. Package:
`did_imputation` / `didimputation`, `did2s`.

## 6. The trade-off table

| Estimator | Core idea | Best when | Breaks when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Callaway-Sant'Anna** | Group-time 2×2, explicit clean control | Standard rollout, absorbing treatment, want transparency | Treatment toggles on/off |
| **Wooldridge ETWFE** | Saturated OLS interactions | You need OLS + SQL/PySpark, time-varying covariates | Treatment toggles on/off |
| **Sun-Abraham** | Cohort × relative-time, then average | The deliverable is a clean event-study chart | Treatment toggles on/off |
| **de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfœuille** | Switchers vs. stayers | **Non-absorbing** / toggling treatment | — (this is the toggling fix) |
| **Borusyak et al. / Gardner** | Impute $Y(0)$ from untreated cells | Need max efficiency, big untreated pool | $Y(0)$ model misspecified |

**Practical guidance (from your notes, and correct):** run more than one.
Report a robust estimator *alongside* TWFE so the delta is visible. Always
present an event study with pre-treatment coefficients. If two robust
estimators disagree, that disagreement is a finding — usually about your
control-group definition or your $Y(0)$ specification.

## 7. Worked probe: the AdTech auto-bidder

**Setup.** A major AdTech platform tests a new **Auto-Bidding** algorithm.
Advertisers can **turn it on whenever they want — try it a week, turn it
off, turn it back on a month later.** You want its causal effect on ROAS. A
junior DS proposes **Callaway-Sant'Anna**, citing a blog post that "TWFE is
dead, CS is the modern gold standard."

### 7.1 Why the junior is wrong

**Callaway-Sant'Anna assumes absorbing treatment, and this treatment isn't
absorbing.** CS is built entirely on the concept of a **cohort** $g$ = *the
time a unit was first treated*, and once treated, always treated. That
definition is the load-bearing beam of the whole method — the base period
$Y_{g-1}$, the never/not-yet-treated control, the group-time $ATT(g,t)$ all
presuppose it.

Here an advertiser is on in week 1, off in week 3, on again in week 8.
**What is their cohort $g$?** The question has no answer. There's no
"first and only" treatment time, so:

- $Y_{g-1}$ (the base period) is undefined — which "before" is *the*
  before?
- The not-yet-treated control pool is incoherent — a unit that's currently
  off was treated last month, so it's a contaminated control, exactly the
  §3.3 poison CS was built to avoid.

The junior pattern-matched "staggered → CS" without checking the treatment
*structure*. "TWFE is dead" is true; "CS is therefore always the answer" is
the actual error. **The absorbing-vs-non-absorbing distinction is the first
question to ask, before naming any estimator.**

### 7.2 The pivot: de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfœuille

Use **$DID_M$** (§4). It's the one estimator in the family built for
**non-absorbing** treatment. It doesn't need a coherent cohort — it works
off *transitions*, comparing advertisers who **switched** auto-bidding
state at a given boundary against those who **stayed** put:

```math
DID_{M,t} = \sum_{g} \frac{N_{g,t}}{N_{+,t}} \big( \Delta Y_{g,t} - \Delta Y_{0,t} \big)
```

For every week, it pairs advertisers who flipped on (or off) against
advertisers who held their state, and differences the ROAS changes. Because
it only ever compares a single-step transition to a contemporaneous stayer,
constant toggling is fine — that's precisely the design target. Package:
`did_multiplegt`.

**Staff-level framing:** *"The blog post is right that naive TWFE is biased
under staggered adoption. But CS and the other cohort-based estimators
assume treatment is absorbing — once on, always on. Our advertisers toggle
auto-bidding freely, so 'cohort' is undefined and CS's clean-control logic
collapses. For non-absorbing treatment we use de Chaisemartin-
D'Haultfœuille's switcher estimator, which compares state-changers to
state-holders at each time step and is robust to arbitrary toggling."*

## 8. Common misreadings

**"TWFE is dead, so always use Callaway-Sant'Anna."**
CS assumes absorbing treatment. For toggling treatment it's as broken as
TWFE — use de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfœuille. Ask "absorbing or not?" *first*.
§7.

**"These estimators all give the same answer, so pick any."**
They target subtly different aggregations and lean on different assumptions
(design-based vs. model-based $Y(0)$). Disagreement between them is
diagnostic, not noise. §6.

**"ETWFE is just old TWFE, which we know is biased."**
The *saturation* is the whole point — full cohort × time interactions block
the forbidden comparison. Plain TWFE has one $\beta$; ETWFE has one per
cell. §2.

**"Imputation is best because its CIs are tightest."**
Tight CIs come from leaning on the $Y(0)$ model. If that model is
misspecified, you get a precise wrong answer. Efficiency and robustness
trade off. §5.

**"My event study looks clean, so parallel trends holds — even under TWFE."**
A naive TWFE event study contaminates pre-period coefficients with other
cohorts' post-effects (Sun-Abraham). A "clean" pre-trend from the wrong
estimator can be an artifact. Use Sun-Abraham or CS for the plot. §3.
