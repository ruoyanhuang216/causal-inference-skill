## The idea, in one screen

[§3.1](./3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md) ended on a limitation: geo-DiD
averages the control cities *equally*, and if that average drifts from the
treated city's pre-trend, parallel trends is dead on arrival. Launch in New
York growing 5% a month, control average growing 2%, and your DiD counts the
3% gap as an effect that isn't there.

**Synthetic control's move:** stop averaging equally. *Learn* weights on the
donor cities so the weighted blend tracks New York's pre-treatment trajectory —
maybe $\widehat{\text{NY}} = 0.59\,\text{Boston} + 0.41\,\text{Austin}$ — then
use that blend as the counterfactual and read the post-period gap as the
effect. It reframes control-group construction as an **optimization problem:
find the weights that force the pre-trends parallel**, rather than assuming
parallel trends and hoping. **Geo-DiD is the special case where every weight is
pinned to $1/J$.**

**Inference is by permutation, not standard errors.** With a single treated
unit there is one cluster in the treatment arm, so cluster-robust SEs are
*undefined* (the [§3.4 few-clusters problem](./3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#34-the-catch-that-ties-the-lecture-together-few-clusters)
at its limit). Instead you turn each donor into a fake treated unit and rank
the real treated unit's post/pre RMSPE ratio against that placebo distribution.
This is why synthetic control exists as a distinct method rather than a DiD
variant.
