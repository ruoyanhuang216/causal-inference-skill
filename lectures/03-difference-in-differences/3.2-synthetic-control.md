# 3.2 — Synthetic Control, Step by Step

> **Part of:** [Lecture 3 — Difference-in-Differences](./README.md)
> **Use when:** one treated market, a pool of untreated ones, and a simple average of them doesn't track the treated unit's pre-trend.

---

## 1. The core philosophy

§3.1 ended on a limitation: geo-DiD averages the control cities *equally*,
and if that average drifts from the treated city's pre-trend, parallel
trends is dead on arrival. Launch in New York growing 5% a month, control
average growing 2%, and your DiD counts the 3% gap as an effect that
isn't there.

**Synthetic control's move:** stop averaging equally. Instead, *learn*
weights on the donor cities so the weighted blend tracks New York's
pre-treatment trajectory as closely as possible. Maybe

```math
\widehat{\text{NY}} = 0.59 \cdot \text{Boston} + 0.41 \cdot \text{Austin} + 0 \cdot \text{Seattle} + 0 \cdot \text{Denver}
```

reproduces New York's pre-period almost exactly. Then you use that blend
as the counterfactual. **It reframes control-group construction as an
optimization problem: find the weights that force the pre-trends
parallel** — rather than assuming parallel trends and hoping.

Geo-DiD is the special case where every weight is pinned to $1/J$.
Synthetic control frees the weights. That's the entire idea; the rest is
mechanics.

> **Framing note.** This chapter is the DiD-adjacent, practical version of
> synthetic control — enough to build one and defend it. The full method
> family (augmented SCM, synthetic DiD, matrix completion, penalized

## 2. The method in five steps

### Step 1 — Define the target and the donor pool

- **Treated unit:** $Y_1$ (New York).
- **Donor pool:** $J$ untreated units $Y_2, \dots, Y_{J+1}$ (Boston,
  Austin, Seattle, Denver, …).
- **Time:** $T_{\text{pre}}$ periods before treatment, $T_{\text{post}}$
  after.

The donor pool must be **clean**: no donor can have been treated, and none
can be contaminated by spillover from the treated unit (SUTVA again). A
donor that got a smaller version of the same intervention poisons the
counterfactual.

### Step 2 — Learn the weights (the optimization)

Find weights $W = (w_2, \dots, w_{J+1})$ minimizing the pre-period fit
error between New York and the synthetic blend:

```math
\min_{W} \; \sum_{t=1}^{T_{\text{pre}}} \left( Y_{1,t} - \sum_{j=2}^{J+1} w_j Y_{j,t} \right)^2
```

subject to the **classic Abadie constraints**:

```math
w_j \geq 0 \quad \text{(no shorting a city)} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \sum_{j} w_j = 1 \quad \text{(convex hull)}
```

These two constraints are doing real conceptual work:

- **Non-negativity** forbids a city contributing *negatively*. You can't
  build "New York" by subtracting Seattle. This keeps the synthetic unit
  interpretable and inside the space of real cities.
- **Sum-to-one (convex hull)** means the synthetic unit is a weighted
  *average*, so it can never extrapolate outside the range of the donors.
  If New York is bigger than every donor city, no convex combination can
  match it — and that failure to fit is a *feature*: it tells you honestly
  that your donor pool can't reproduce the treated unit, rather than
  silently extrapolating.

The convex-hull constraint also induces **sparsity**: the optimizer
typically lands on a handful of nonzero weights and zeroes the rest, which
is why synthetic controls are interpretable ("60% Boston, 40% Austin")
where a ridge regression would smear small weights across all donors.

**Industry note.** With hundreds of donors, the strict constraints
overfit the pre-period. Modern practice relaxes them: **ridge / L2
penalty** (allows small negative weights, trades interpretability for
stability), or **Bayesian structural time series** with spike-and-slab
priors (this is what Google's CausalImpact does — see [Lecture 2 §2.4](../02-experimentation/2.4-geo-experiments.md#4-estimation)).
The bias-variance trade is real: strict constraints = interpretable but
overfit-prone; relaxed = stable but you can no longer say "60% Boston."

### Step 3 — Freeze the weights, build the counterfactual

The weights were learned on the **pre-period only**. Freeze them and
project forward:

```math
\hat{Y}_{1,t}^{\text{synth}} = \sum_{j=2}^{J+1} \hat{w}_j Y_{j,t} \qquad \text{for } t > T_{\text{pre}}
```

This is the crux: the post-period synthetic values are **out-of-sample**.
You never fit to them. That's what makes the post-period gap a credible
counterfactual rather than a curve you drew through the data.

### Step 4 — The treatment effect

The effect at each post-period is observed minus synthetic:

```math
\tau_t = Y_{1,t} - \hat{Y}_{1,t}^{\text{synth}}
```

and the ATT is the average gap over the post-period:

```math
\widehat{\text{ATT}} = \frac{1}{T_{\text{post}}} \sum_{t > T_{\text{pre}}} \tau_t
```

### Step 5 — Inference by permutation (in-space placebos)

Here's the problem: you have **one** treated unit. There is no sampling
distribution in the usual sense, so classical standard errors don't apply.
The fix is a **permutation test** — turn every donor into a fake treated
unit:

1. Pretend Boston was treated. Build "synthetic Boston" from the *other*
   donors. Compute its placebo gap.
2. Repeat for every donor in the pool.
3. Compare New York's real gap to the distribution of placebo gaps.

**If New York's post-period gap is larger than, say, 95% of the placebo
gaps, the effect is significant at ≈ 0.05.** The logic: if the
intervention did nothing, New York's gap should look like any donor's
random gap. A gap far out in the tail is the signal.

Refinement: rank by the **post/pre RMSPE ratio**, not the raw gap. A donor
that fits badly in the pre-period will have a big post gap for reasons that
have nothing to do with treatment; dividing by pre-period fit error
controls for that.

## 3. Inference: why not cluster-robust standard errors?

A natural question coming out of [§3.1](./3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#3-inference-the-standard-error-is-where-did-dies):
DiD fixed its standard-error problem with cluster-robust SEs at the
treatment-assignment level. **Does synthetic control do the same?**

**No — and the reason is the few-clusters problem from
[§3.1 §3.4](./3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#34-the-catch-that-ties-the-lecture-together-few-clusters),
taken to its limit.** CRVE is consistent in the *number of clusters*. The
canonical synthetic control setting has **one treated unit** — one
California, one West Germany, one launch city. That is a single cluster in
the treatment arm. There is no sampling distribution of treated units to
average over, so there is nothing for a cluster-robust variance to
estimate. CRVE here isn't merely unreliable — it's *undefined*.

So SC swaps sampling-based inference for **randomization-based inference**:
the permutation / in-space placebo test of §2 Step 5. The question itself
changes shape:

- **DiD asks** (Neyman): "accounting for correlated errors, is the average
  effect distinguishable from sampling noise?" → a variance question →
  CRVE.
- **SC asks** (Fisher): "is the treated unit's gap larger than what this
  exact procedure produces on units that were *not* treated?" → a
  permutation question → the placebo distribution.

### 3.1 How the weighting is handled — the elegant part

Your specific question: with a *weighted* control ($0.59\,\text{Boston} +
0.41\,\text{Austin}$), doesn't estimating the weights add uncertainty that
a naive gap ignores?

It does — and permutation inference captures it **for free**, because **the
weights are re-fit inside every placebo run.** When you pretend Boston is
treated, you solve the whole convex optimization again from the *remaining*
donors to build "synthetic Boston." Every placebo carries its own freshly
estimated weights, so the variability of the weight-fitting step is already
baked into the reference distribution. You never estimate a weight and then
treat it as a known constant — the *entire* procedure, weights included, is
what gets permuted.

This is why "how do you compute the SE of the weighted estimator" has a
surprising answer: **you don't compute an SE at all. You compute a rank.**

### 3.2 Why you *can't* just derive an analytic SE

Suppose you tried the DiD route anyway — an analytic variance for
$\hat{\tau} = Y_{1,\text{post}} - \sum_j \hat{w}_j Y_{j,\text{post}}$. It
fails, for a reason worth understanding.

The weights $\hat{w}$ solve a **constrained optimization** ($w_j \geq 0$,
$\sum_j w_j = 1$). In any real fit, most constraints **bind** — the §2
example put *exactly zero* weight on Seattle and Denver. An estimator
sitting on the boundary of its parameter space is **not asymptotically
normal**, so the delta method — which linearizes around an *interior* point
— has no valid expansion to run. The very sparsity that makes synthetic
control interpretable ("59% Boston, 41% Austin") is what kills the analytic
standard error. Permutation inference sidesteps this completely: it never
requires the estimator to be normal.

### 3.3 Verified

The permutation test run on a **20-donor** panel (the §5
`placebo_inference` mechanism — a larger pool than §4's four-donor fit,
precisely so the p-value floor is low enough to matter):

```
  true effect = 0:   treated RMSPE-ratio = 1.13,  median placebo = 2.36  ->  p = 0.86
  true effect = 15:  treated RMSPE-ratio = 19.0,  median placebo = 2.80  ->  p = 0.048
```

With no effect the treated unit is unremarkable among the placebos
(p = 0.86). With a real effect its gap is the most extreme of all 21 units,
giving p = 1/21 ≈ 0.048.

Note what sets the **floor** on your p-value: with $J$ donors, the smallest
achievable p-value is $1/(J+1)$. **Ten donors caps your best possible
p-value at 0.09** — you cannot reach 5% significance no matter how large
the true effect. A credible SC needs a reasonably large donor pool for
*inference*, not only for fit. This is the permutation-inference analogue
of §3.1's "few clusters" ceiling.

### 3.4 The spectrum: it depends how many treated units you have

Single-unit permutation is the classic case, not the only one. As treated
units accumulate you move back toward design-based variance:

| Treated units | Inference |
|---|---|
| **1** | Permutation / in-space placebos (this section). Cannot cluster. |
| **A handful** | Jackknife over treated units — **Synthetic DiD** (Arkhangelsky et al. 2021) supplies exactly this variance estimator |
| **Many** (geo experiments, several treated DMAs) | CRVE becomes meaningful again — you're back in [§3.1](./3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#3-inference-the-standard-error-is-where-did-dies)'s world |

And a modern single-unit alternative worth knowing: **conformal inference**
(Chernozhukov, Wüthrich & Zhu 2021) tests the sharp null by permuting
residuals over *time* rather than over units — the right tool when you have
one treated unit, a long pre-period, and don't want to lean on the donor
pool as the permutation reference.

## 4. Worked example: fitting it for real

Not a hypothetical — an actual fitted run (code in §5). Donors: Boston,
Austin, Seattle, Denver. The true data-generating process makes New York
$0.6 \cdot \text{Boston} + 0.4 \cdot \text{Austin}$ in the pre-period,
then adds a **+15** treatment effect in the post-period.

**Fitted convex-hull weights (pre-period only):**

```
  Boston   0.592      ← true 0.60
  Austin   0.408      ← true 0.40
  Seattle  0.000      ← zeroed by the convex-hull sparsity
  Denver   0.000      ← zeroed
  pre-period RMSE: 0.69
```

**Recovered effect:** ATT = **+15.29** against a true **+15.0**.

Three things this shows concretely:

- The optimizer **recovered the generative weights** (0.592/0.408 vs.
  0.60/0.40) without being told them — it learned them from pre-period fit
  alone.
- The convex hull **zeroed the two irrelevant donors** exactly, giving the
  interpretable "New York is 59% Boston, 41% Austin." Sparsity is not
  imposed; it falls out of the constraint.
- Freezing the weights and reading the out-of-sample gap **recovered the
  effect** to within noise.

This is why the "0.6 Boston + 0.4 Austin" story isn't hand-waving: it's
what the optimizer does when the donor pool can genuinely reconstruct the
treated unit.

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize

def synthetic_control(Y_treated, Y_donors, T_pre):
    """
    Y_treated : (T,) outcomes for the treated unit
    Y_donors  : (T, J) outcomes for J donor units
    T_pre     : number of pre-treatment periods
    Returns weights, synthetic series, per-period effect.
    """
    Dpre, ypre = Y_donors[:T_pre], Y_treated[:T_pre]
    J = Y_donors.shape[1]

    # minimize pre-period MSE ...
    loss = lambda w: np.sum((ypre - Dpre @ w) ** 2)
    # ... subject to the Abadie constraints:
    constraints = [{"type": "eq", "fun": lambda w: w.sum() - 1.0}]  # sum to 1
    bounds = [(0.0, 1.0)] * J                                       # non-negative

    res = minimize(loss, np.full(J, 1.0 / J),
                   bounds=bounds, constraints=constraints, method="SLSQP")
    w = res.x
    synth = Y_donors @ w                    # frozen weights, projected forward
    effect = Y_treated - synth              # tau_t = observed - synthetic
    return w, synth, effect

w, synth, effect = synthetic_control(NY, donors_matrix, T_pre=12)
print("weights:", dict(zip(donor_names, w.round(3))))
print(f"pre-period RMSE: {np.sqrt(np.mean(effect[:12]**2)):.2f}")   # must be small
print(f"ATT (post gap):  {effect[12:].mean():.2f}")
# weights: Boston 0.592, Austin 0.408, Seattle 0.0, Denver 0.0
# ATT (post gap): 15.29

# ---- Inference: in-space placebos ----
def placebo_inference(units, treated_idx, T_pre):
    """Turn each donor into a fake-treated unit; compare gap magnitudes."""
    ratios = {}
    for j in range(units.shape[1]):
        y = units[:, j]
        donors = np.delete(units, j, axis=1)
        _, _, eff = synthetic_control(y, donors, T_pre)
        pre_rmspe = np.sqrt(np.mean(eff[:T_pre] ** 2))
        post_rmspe = np.sqrt(np.mean(eff[T_pre:] ** 2))
        ratios[j] = post_rmspe / max(pre_rmspe, 1e-8)   # rank by ratio, not raw gap
    return ratios

# p-value = share of placebo ratios >= the treated unit's ratio
# ---- Production: pysyncon, SparseSC, or CausalImpact for the BSTS variant ----
```

## 6. Common misreadings

**"Fit the weights on all the data."**
No — fit on the **pre-period only**. The post-period must stay
out-of-sample or the gap isn't a counterfactual, it's a curve you drew
through the outcome you're trying to explain. §2 Step 2–3.

**"Great pre-period fit proves the method worked."**
A near-perfect pre-fit with many donors is a red flag for *overfitting*,
not a green light. With enough donors you can match any pre-trend by
chance; the honest checks are a *parsimonious* donor pool and the placebo
distribution. This is why the industry relaxes the constraints and
penalizes.

**"Use standard errors for the p-value."**
One treated unit has no classical sampling distribution. Inference is by
permutation — in-space placebos, ranked by RMSPE ratio. §2 Step 5, §3.

**"Cluster the standard errors like we did in DiD."**
You can't — one treated unit is one cluster in the treatment arm, and CRVE
is undefined there. This is the few-clusters problem ([§3.1 §3.4](./3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#34-the-catch-that-ties-the-lecture-together-few-clusters))
at its limit, and it's the whole reason SC uses permutation instead. §3.

**"The estimated weights need their own standard error term."**
They don't get a separate SE — the weights are re-fit inside every placebo,
so their uncertainty is already in the permutation distribution. And you
couldn't compute one analytically anyway: binding constraints put the
estimator on the boundary, where the delta method has no valid expansion.
§3.1, §3.2.

**"Any city can go in the donor pool."**
Donors must be untreated and spillover-free. A donor that caught a whiff
of the treatment biases the counterfactual toward the treated unit,
shrinking the estimated effect.

**"The convex-hull constraint is just regularization."**
It's more: it keeps the synthetic unit *interpretable* (a real weighted
average of real cities) and *honest* (it can't extrapolate beyond the
donors, so a treated unit outside the donor hull fails to fit visibly
instead of silently). Relaxing to ridge buys stability but forfeits both.

**"Synthetic control removes the parallel-trends assumption."**
It *enforces* the pre-trend match rather than assuming it — but it still
assumes the match would have *continued* absent treatment. That's parallel
trends on the synthetic control, and it's still untestable in the
post-period. You've made the assumption far more credible, not eliminated
it.
