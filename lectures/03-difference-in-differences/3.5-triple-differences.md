# 3.5 — Triple Differences (DDD)

> **Part of:** [Lecture 3 — Difference-in-Differences](./README.md)
> **Use when:** parallel trends for a plain DiD is implausible because a *market-wide shock* hit your treatment or control group — but you have a within-market split into exposed and unexposed segments.

---

## 1. The problem: a shock that breaks parallel trends

Plain DiD needs the treatment and control groups to move together absent
treatment (README §2). Sometimes a concurrent shock guarantees they won't.

**The scenario.** You launch a Premium Subscription for **iOS users in the
UK** (treatment), using **iOS users in France** as control. On launch day,
Apple happens to run a massive **UK-only** iPhone marketing blitz. UK iOS
engagement spikes — from Apple's campaign, not your feature. Your DiD
attributes the whole spike to Premium and wildly overstates the effect.
Parallel trends is dead: a UK-specific shock hit your treatment group and
not your control.

A plain DiD cannot separate "your feature" from "the UK shock," because
both live in the same treatment cell.

---

## 2. The intuition: a DiD of a DiD

The fix: find a **second control group inside the UK** that is exposed to
the same UK shock but *not* to your treatment, use it to measure the
shock's size, and subtract it out.

**Android users are that group.** UK Android users feel every UK-specific
trend — but they're immune to the Apple *iPhone* campaign and can't get
your iOS-only Premium feature. So:

- **DiD #1 — the eligible segment** (UK iOS vs. France iOS) captures
  **[your feature] + [the Apple UK shock]**.
- **DiD #2 — the ineligible segment** (UK Android vs. France Android)
  captures **[the Apple UK shock, or whatever UK-specific trend Android
  also sees]** — but *not* your feature.
- **DDD = DiD #1 − DiD #2** strips the shared shock and isolates the
  feature.

You've differenced three times: over **time**, over **market**, and over
**segment**. Hence triple differences.

---

## 3. The regression

Three dummies and *every* interaction — a fully saturated model:

- $T$ = treatment market (1 = UK, 0 = France)
- $P$ = post-period (1 = after launch)
- $E$ = eligible segment (1 = iOS, 0 = Android)

```math
Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 T + \beta_2 P + \beta_3 E + \beta_4 (T{\times}P) + \beta_5 (T{\times}E) + \beta_6 (P{\times}E) + \beta_7 (T{\times}P{\times}E) + \epsilon
```

The coefficients, and what each one absorbs:

| Coef | Captures |
|---|---|
| $\beta_1, \beta_2, \beta_3$ | Main effects: market, time, segment baselines |
| $\beta_4$ $(T{\times}P)$ | **DiD for the *ineligible* segment** — the UK shock on Android. The "shock coefficient." |
| $\beta_5$ $(T{\times}E)$ | Baseline iOS−Android gap within the UK |
| $\beta_6$ $(P{\times}E)$ | General iOS−Android time trend across markets |
| $\beta_7$ $(T{\times}P{\times}E)$ | **The DDD estimator — the true causal effect** |

$\beta_7$ is the feature lift for **iOS users, in the UK, post-launch**,
*above and beyond* every two-way trend — including the UK shock that
$\beta_4$ soaked up. That's the whole design: $\beta_4$ catches the
confounder so $\beta_7$ doesn't have to.

---

## 4. The identifying assumption: it's weaker, but it's not free

DDD **relaxes** parallel trends — you no longer need UK and France to trend
together in levels. But it swaps in a new assumption, **parallel
*differential* trends**:

> Absent treatment, the **gap between iOS and Android in the UK** would have
> evolved on the same trajectory as the **gap between iOS and Android in
> France**.

You've moved the parallel-trends requirement up one level of abstraction —
from trends to *differences* of trends. That's genuinely easier to satisfy
(a shock that hits both segments equally cancels), which is why DDD is
powerful. But it can still fail: if the shock hits the two segments
*unequally*, the subtraction is imperfect (§5).

---

## 5. Worked probe: the hurricane

**Setup.** E-commerce platform launches **One-Click Checkout**. Rolled out
as a geo-experiment: **ON in Texas, OFF in Florida.** The feature is
**mobile-only** — desktop users never see it. Two-week test. Midway, a
**hurricane hits Florida**, knocks out power, and depresses Florida mobile
usage. Your DiD (Texas mobile vs. Florida mobile) is wrecked — the control
group tanked for reasons that have nothing to do with your button.

### 5.1 Structure the DDD

Three dimensions:

- $T$ = **treatment state**: Texas = 1, Florida = 0
- $P$ = **post**: after launch = 1
- $E$ = **eligible segment**: mobile = 1, desktop = 0 (desktop can't see
  the button)

The eligible segment (mobile) gets [feature + hurricane]. The ineligible
segment (desktop) gets [hurricane only] — desktop shopping is also
depressed by the Florida power outage, but desktop never sees One-Click.
Subtract the desktop DiD from the mobile DiD and the hurricane cancels.

### 5.2 What $\beta_4$ captures — the hurricane coefficient

$\beta_4$ is $T{\times}P$ at $E = 0$ (desktop): the **DiD for desktop
users**.

- Texas desktop sales: flat (normal).
- Florida desktop sales: **tanked** (hurricane).
- So the Texas−Florida desktop gap **widened massively** → $\beta_4$ is a
  large **positive** number.

That large $\beta_4$ *is* the hurricane, measured. In a plain mobile-only
DiD, that same widening gap would be **falsely credited to One-Click
Checkout**. The DDD estimates the hurricane on desktop and mathematically
subtracts it, leaving $\beta_7$ as the clean feature lift.

### 5.3 Verified

A simulation (code in §6) with a **true feature effect of +8** (Texas
mobile post only) and a **−20 hurricane shock** hitting *both* Florida
segments in the post-period:

```
  Naive DiD (mobile only):     +28.3    ← true effect is 8; hurricane inflates it 3.5x
  DDD three-way (beta_7):       +8.3    ← recovers the truth
  beta_4 (T×P, desktop DiD):   +20.0    ← the hurricane, measured and subtracted
```

The naive DiD reports **+28.3** for an **+8** effect — the −20 Florida
crash makes Texas look 20 better by comparison, and the mobile-only DiD
dumps all of it onto the feature. The DDD recovers **+8.3**, because
$\beta_4$ caught the +20 hurricane on desktop and the three-way difference
netted it out. This is the design working exactly as intended.

### 5.4 The staff-level caveat

A senior reviewer will ask: **"Is parallel-*differential*-trends actually
valid here? Does a hurricane hit desktop and mobile identically?"**

It almost certainly does **not**, and the honest answer earns the room:

> *"When the power goes out, home Wi-Fi dies, so desktop usage drops to
> roughly zero. But people still have phone batteries and LTE — they may
> use mobile* more *in the first 24 hours, buying emergency supplies before
> towers fail. So the hurricane hits desktop* harder *than mobile. That
> means $\beta_4$ (measured on desktop) over-states the mobile shock, and
> the DDD slightly over-corrects — biasing our feature estimate somewhat
> low. It's imperfect. But it's dramatically closer to the truth than a
> naive DiD, which would have reported +28 for an +8 effect."*

The point isn't that DDD is magic — it's that DDD's residual bias (a
second-order segment-asymmetry effect) is far smaller than the first-order
confound it removes. Knowing *which direction* the residual bias runs, and
saying so unprompted, is what separates a defensible estimate from an
overclaimed one.

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# T = treated state (TX=1), P = post, E = eligible segment (mobile=1)

# ---- Naive DiD on the eligible segment only: WRECKED by the shock ----

naive = smf.ols("Y ~ T * P", data=mobile).fit().params["T:P"]
print(f"naive DiD (mobile only): {naive:+.1f}")     # +28.3 for a true +8

# ---- DDD: fully saturated three-way interaction ----
ddd = smf.ols("Y ~ T * P * E", data=df).fit(
    cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["geo_segment"]}
)
print(f"DDD effect (T:P:E):      {ddd.params['T:P:E']:+.1f}")   # +8.3  <- truth
print(f"hurricane coef (T:P):    {ddd.params['T:P']:+.1f}")     # +20.0 <- the shock

# T:P:E is beta_7 (the DDD estimator). T:P is beta_4 (the ineligible-segment
# DiD -- here, the hurricane on desktop, which the three-way difference nets out).
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"DDD removes the parallel-trends assumption."**
It *relaxes* it to **parallel differential trends** — the iOS−Android gap
must trend the same across markets. Weaker and often more credible, but
still an untestable assumption, and it fails if the shock hits segments
unequally. §4, §5.4.

**"β₇ is just the treatment coefficient."**
$\beta_7$ is the **triple** interaction $T{\times}P{\times}E$. The lower
interactions ($\beta_4, \beta_5, \beta_6$) are the confounding trends being
differenced away; only the three-way term is the effect. §3.

**"Use DDD whenever you have subgroups."**
Only when a subgroup is exposed to the *confounding shock* but not the
*treatment*. Random subgroups don't help — the ineligible segment must
genuinely see the shock and genuinely miss the feature. §2.

**"The hurricane coefficient β₄ is a nuisance to ignore."**
$\beta_4$ is the whole reason DDD works — it *measures* the confounder so
$\beta_7$ can be clean. Reading $\beta_4$ tells you how big the shock was.
§5.2.

**"DDD is unbiased, so we don't need to defend it."**
Its residual bias is real (unequal shock across segments) — you defend it by
naming the *direction* of that bias, as in §5.4. "Imperfect but far better
than DiD" is the honest, and stronger, claim.
