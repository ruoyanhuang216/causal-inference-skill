# 14.3 — Dynamic, Search & Learning Models (Survey)

> **Part of:** [Lecture 14 — Structural Estimation](./README.md)
> **Scope:** a high-level survey (TBA) — enough to name each method, know its trade-off, and when to reach for it. Not step-by-step like §14.1–14.2.

BLP (§14.1) is *static* — consumers choose once. But many decisions are
**forward-looking**: a driver replaces an engine anticipating future costs, a
consumer searches before buying, a shopper learns a product's quality over
repeated purchases. These need dynamic structural models. The unifying challenge
is the same: **solving (or sidestepping) a dynamic-programming problem** inside
estimation.

---

## 1. Single-agent dynamic discrete choice

The agent picks action $a_t$ each period to maximize expected discounted utility:

```math
\max \; E\Big[\textstyle\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \beta^t\, u(a_t, s_t, \epsilon_t; \theta)\Big] \quad \text{s.t. } s_{t+1} = f(s_t, a_t)
```

The hard part is the **value function** — the expected future payoff at each
state. Three ways to handle it in estimation:

| Method | Idea | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| **NFXP** (Rust 1987) | *Nested fixed point*: at every parameter guess, fully solve the DP (value-function iteration), then evaluate the likelihood | Consistent, efficient; **expensive** — solves the DP at each guess. The classic bus-engine-replacement paper. |
| **CCP** (Hotz-Miller 1993) | Express the value function via **observed conditional choice probabilities** — no DP solve. Two stages: estimate CCPs from data, then map to structural parameters | **Fast**; but first-stage CCPs are noisy in sparse states, and some forms need "finite dependence" |
| **MPEC** (Su-Judd 2012) | Formulate estimation as *constrained optimization*: minimize the moment distance **subject to** the Bellman equation holding | Often faster/more robust than NFXP; uses off-the-shelf solvers (KNITRO, IPOPT) |

**Identification caveat:** the discount factor $\beta$ is notoriously hard to
separate from the per-period utility, and is **usually calibrated** (e.g.
$\beta = 0.95$) rather than estimated.

---

## 2. Dynamic games (multiple strategic agents)

When several forward-looking firms interact — entry/exit, dynamic pricing,
advertising, R&D races — you need equilibrium dynamics. Solving them directly is
brutal, so the practical estimators are two-step (estimate policies first, recover
payoffs second):

- **Bajari, Benkard & Levin (2007)** — estimate policy functions from data, then
  recover the payoff parameters that rationalize them.
- **Aguirregabiria & Mira (2007)** — nested pseudo-likelihood with CCP updates.
- **Pakes, Ostrovsky & Berry (2007)** — simple moment-based estimators for dynamic
  games.

---

## 3. Consumer search (Weitzman 1979)

When consumers don't see all options at once, they **search sequentially**, paying
a cost $c$ per look. Weitzman's elegant result: assign each option a **reservation
value**, search in *descending* reservation-value order, and **stop** when the best
found utility exceeds the next option's reservation value.

**What identifies the search cost:** how *many* options consumers examine, the
*order* they search, and the relationship between the chosen option and the set
size. Applied to financial markets (Hortaçsu-Syverson 2004), insurance (Honka
2014), and online shopping. **Consideration-set models** are the reduced cousin:
consumers first form a limited attention set, then choose from it — separating
*awareness* from *preference* (needs click/eye-tracking data, or partial
identification from choices alone).

---

## 4. Learning models (Erdem-Keane 1996)

For **experience goods**, consumers are uncertain about quality and *learn* over
time. They hold Bayesian priors, update via Bayes' rule on each signal (a
purchase, an ad, word-of-mouth), and trade off **exploration** (trying uncertain
products) against **exploitation** (buying the known-best) — the same
explore/exploit tension as bandits ([Lecture 2 §2.6](../02-experimentation/2.6-ramp-up-and-bandits.md))
and Empirical Bayes ([Lecture 7 §7.4](../07-panel-data/7.4-empirical-bayes.md)).

**What identifies learning:** the *dynamics* — switching probability *falls* over
time, preferred-brand purchase frequency *rises*, and responsiveness to
advertising *decays* as beliefs firm up. Applied to new-product adoption,
advertising effectiveness, and physician prescribing.

## 5. How it's estimated (the method menu)

| Method | When |
|---|---|
| **(Simulated) MLE** | tractable likelihood; most efficient |
| **(Simulated) GMM / MSM** | moment conditions available (BLP uses GMM) |
| **Indirect inference** | match an auxiliary model fit on real vs. simulated data |
| **Bayesian MCMC** | want the full posterior; hierarchical structure |
| **MPEC** | dynamic models, as a constrained-optimization formulation |

**Python tooling:** `pyblp` (BLP), `scipy.optimize` (general), `JAX`/`autograd`
(auto-diff for GMM/MLE gradients), `numba` (JIT for value-function iteration),
`Pyomo`/`casadi` (MPEC), `PyMC`/`emcee` (Bayesian).

---

## 6. The one thing to remember

All of these are **counterfactual machines**: you estimate structural parameters
(search costs, learning speed, dynamic payoffs) so you can simulate a policy you've
never observed — a new fee, a faster-learning product, a market with one more
entrant. That's the same bargain as all of Lecture 14: **strong assumptions bought
in exchange for extrapolation no experiment can give you.** If the policy you care
about actually *happened*, the reduced-form tools (Lectures 1–13) are more
credible; reach here only when you must simulate the unobserved.
