# 14.1 — Discrete Choice & BLP Demand

> **Part of:** [Lecture 14 — Structural Estimation](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [Lecture 5 — IV](../05-instrumental-variables/) — BLP is GMM-IV at its core.

The goal: estimate a **demand system** — how much each product's sales respond to
its own and competitors' prices — flexible enough to power merger and pricing
counterfactuals (§14.2). We build up from the simplest model to BLP, each step
fixing the previous one's flaw.

---

## 1. Multinomial / conditional logit (McFadden 1974)

Consumer $i$ picks the product $j$ maximizing utility:

```math
U_{ij} = X_j\beta - \alpha p_j + \xi_j + \epsilon_{ij}, \qquad \epsilon_{ij} \sim \text{Type-I EV}
```

The extreme-value error gives closed-form **choice probabilities** (the logit
formula), which makes it trivial to estimate. But it has a fatal flaw.

**IIA — Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives.** Logit forces *proportional*
substitution: remove a product, and its share redistributes among the others **in
fixed ratios**, regardless of how similar they are. *Verified* (code in §5):
remove one product and the share ratio of two survivors stays **1.350 → 1.350,
unchanged.** That's absurd for differentiated products — remove a luxury car and
its buyers should flow to *other luxury cars*, not proportionally to economy cars.
IIA is the problem every richer model exists to solve.

---

## 2. Mixed logit / random coefficients

Let preferences **vary across consumers**:

```math
U_{ij} = X_j\beta_i - \alpha_i p_j + \xi_j + \epsilon_{ij}, \qquad \beta_i \sim F(\theta)
```

Now substitution is driven by *who* is choosing: luxury buyers (high $\beta$ for
quality) substitute toward other high-quality products, breaking IIA. Choice
probabilities no longer have a closed form — you **simulate** them (average over
draws of $\beta_i$) and estimate by Maximum Simulated Likelihood or Method of
Simulated Moments. This needs *individual-level* choice data.

---

## 3. BLP (Berry, Levinsohn & Pakes 1995) — the workhorse

BLP's two innovations make random-coefficients demand estimable from **aggregate
market-share data** (which is all you usually have) while handling **price
endogeneity.** Utility splits into a mean part $\delta_j$ (common to all
consumers) and an individual deviation $\mu_{ij}$:

```math
U_{ij} = \underbrace{X_j\beta - \alpha p_j + \xi_j}_{\delta_j \text{ (mean utility)}} + \underbrace{X_j\,\Sigma\,v_i}_{\mu_{ij} \text{ (random-coefficient deviation)}} + \epsilon_{ij}
```

$\xi_j$ is the **unobserved product quality** — and the whole difficulty. Firms
set high prices for high-$\xi_j$ products, so **price $p_j$ correlates with the
error $\xi_j$**: OLS would badly underestimate the price sensitivity $\alpha$.

### The estimation algorithm, step by step

1. **Guess** the nonlinear parameters $\Sigma$ (the heterogeneity).
2. **Invert market shares → mean utility** via the **BLP contraction mapping.**
   Given $\Sigma$, find the $\delta$ that makes model shares equal observed
   shares, by iterating:
   ```math
   \delta^{(k+1)} = \delta^{(k)} + \ln(S^{\text{observed}}) - \ln(S^{\text{predicted}}(\delta^{(k)}, \Sigma))
   ```
   This is a contraction — it converges to a unique $\delta$. *Verified* (§5): it
   inverts simulated shares back to the true $\delta$ to **4e-11** in ~1,000
   iterations.
3. **IV regression of $\delta_j$ on $(X_j, p_j)$** to recover $(\beta, \alpha)$
   and the residual $\xi_j$ — using instruments for the endogenous price (§4).
4. **Form the GMM objective** $E[Z'\xi] = 0$ — the instruments must be orthogonal
   to the unobserved quality.
5. **Optimize over $\Sigma$** to minimize the GMM objective; repeat 2–4 at each
   guess.

The inner loop (contraction) recovers $\delta$; the outer loop (GMM) finds the
heterogeneity $\Sigma$ that best satisfies the moment conditions. It's GMM-IV
(Lecture 5) wrapped around a fixed-point share inversion.

---

## 4. Instruments for price

Price is endogenous (§3), so you need instruments correlated with price but not
with unobserved quality $\xi_j$:

- **BLP instruments** — characteristics of *competing* products in the same market
  (sums, counts). Rivals' features shift your markup without touching your quality.
- **Hausman instruments** — the *same* product's price in *other* markets (shared
  cost shocks, independent local demand). Controversial if demand shocks are
  correlated across markets.
- **Cost shifters** — input costs, tariffs, exchange rates. The cleanest, when
  available.
- **Gandhi-Houde (2020) differentiation IVs** — functions of product *distances*
  in characteristic space; capture local competition far better than sums.
- **Optimal instruments** (Chamberlain 1987; Reynaert-Verboven 2014) — the
  efficiency frontier.

Weak instruments here are as dangerous as anywhere (Lecture 5): a weak price IV
gives a nonsensical $\alpha$ and every downstream elasticity is garbage.

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np

# ---- Logit IIA: remove a product, survivors' share RATIO is unchanged ----
def logit_shares(delta):                     # delta = mean utilities; outside good u=0
    e = np.exp(np.append(delta, 0)); return e[:-1] / e.sum()
# verified: removing a product leaves the ratio of two survivors at 1.350 -> 1.350

# ---- BLP contraction mapping: invert shares -> mean utility delta ----
def blp_contraction(S_obs, X, v, Sigma, tol=1e-12, max_it=2000):
    delta = np.zeros(len(S_obs))
    for _ in range(max_it):
        u = delta[:, None] + Sigma * X[:, None] * v[None, :]   # J x I simulated utility
        e = np.exp(u); pred = (e / (1 + e.sum(0))).mean(1)     # simulated shares
        step = np.log(S_obs) - np.log(pred)                    # the contraction step
        delta = delta + step
        if np.abs(step).max() < tol: break
    return delta                                               # recovers true delta to ~1e-11

# ---- Then: IV-GMM of delta on (X, p) with price instruments; optimize over Sigma ----
# Production: pyblp (Conlon & Gortmaker) -- do NOT hand-roll BLP for real work.
#   import pyblp; problem = pyblp.Problem(...); results = problem.solve(sigma=...)
```

## 6. Two simpler alternatives

- **Nested logit** — group products into *nests*; within-nest substitution
  exceeds across-nest. A nesting parameter $\sigma \in [0,1]$ governs it ($\sigma=0$
  is plain logit). Breaks IIA *across* nests while keeping a closed form. Use when
  there's a clear hierarchy (inside vs. outside good; car type → brand).
- **Latent class** — $K$ discrete consumer *segments*, each with its own
  preferences; membership is probabilistic. Estimated by EM; choose $K$ by
  BIC/cross-validation. Use when continuous heterogeneity (mixed logit) is hard to
  justify and discrete segments are natural.

## 7. Common misreadings

**"Logit is fine for demand."**
IIA forces proportional substitution (verified 1.35 → 1.35) — wrong for
differentiated products. You'll get absurd merger predictions. Use random
coefficients / BLP. §1.

**"OLS of shares on price gives the price effect."**
Price is endogenous with unobserved quality $\xi_j$ — OLS underestimates $\alpha$.
BLP's IV-GMM is the point. §3.

**"The contraction mapping is an approximation."**
It's an exact fixed point — it inverts shares to $\delta$ to machine precision
(4e-11). §3.

**"Any instrument correlated with price works."**
It must also be *excluded* from quality $\xi_j$. Rivals' characteristics and cost
shifters qualify; the product's own lagged price usually doesn't. §4.

**"Hand-roll BLP for production."**
Use `pyblp` — the contraction tolerance, optimal instruments, and numerical
stability are all subtle and solved. §5.
