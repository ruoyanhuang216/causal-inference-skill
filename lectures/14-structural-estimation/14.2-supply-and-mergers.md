# 14.2 — Supply Side & Merger Simulation

> **Part of:** [Lecture 14 — Structural Estimation](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§14.1](./14.1-discrete-choice-and-blp.md) — the demand elasticities are the input here.

Demand estimation (§14.1) gives you how quantities respond to prices. The supply
side turns that into **marginal costs** and then into **counterfactual prices** —
the merger-simulation pipeline that antitrust agencies and pricing teams live on.

---

## 1. Nash-Bertrand pricing — back out marginal costs

Assume firms set prices simultaneously to maximize profit (Nash-Bertrand). For
multi-product firms, each price's first-order condition accounts for
**cannibalization** across the firm's own products. Stacked across all products,
the FOCs are:

```math
p - mc = -\big[\Omega \odot \tfrac{\partial S}{\partial p}\big]^{-1} S
```

- $\Omega$ — the **ownership matrix**: $\Omega_{jk} = 1$ if products $j$ and $k$
  are owned by the same firm, else 0.
- $\partial S / \partial p$ — the matrix of **share derivatives**, *from the
  estimated demand system* (§14.1).
- $S$ — market shares.

The right-hand side is the **markup.** Here's the move that makes it all work:
you *observe* prices $p$ and shares $S$, and you *estimated* $\partial S/\partial
p$ — so you can **solve for the one unknown, $mc$**:

```math
mc = p + \big[\Omega \odot \tfrac{\partial S}{\partial p}\big]^{-1} S
```

Marginal costs are almost never in the data; this recovers them from prices +
demand, under the Nash-Bertrand assumption. *Verified* (code in §4): with
observed prices $[10, 12, 8]$ and logit demand, the FOCs back out markups
$[2.86, 2.67, 2.50]$ and hence marginal costs $[7.14, 9.33, 5.50]$.

## 2. Merger simulation — the counterfactual

Once you have demand *and* costs, simulating a merger is mechanical — you change
*who owns what* and re-solve:

1. **Estimate demand** (BLP, §14.1) → the share-derivative matrix $\partial
   S/\partial p$.
2. **Recover marginal costs** from the pre-merger FOCs (§1), using the
   **pre-merger** ownership matrix $\Omega^{\text{pre}}$.
3. **Change the ownership matrix** to $\Omega^{\text{post}}$ — set the cross-terms
   between the two merging firms' products to 1 (now co-owned).
4. **Re-solve for the new equilibrium prices** — the merged firm now internalizes
   the cannibalization between the formerly-competing products, so it *raises*
   prices. Solve the post-merger FOC fixed point (marginal costs held fixed at
   step 2's values).
5. **Compute the changes** — in prices, quantities, consumer surplus, and profits.

The economic intuition: before the merger, Firm A ignored that cutting its price
stole customers from Firm B. After, the merged entity owns both, so it stops
competing against itself — prices rise. The *magnitude* depends entirely on the
demand substitution patterns (§14.1) — which is exactly why IIA (plain logit) is
dangerous here: it would mechanically predict substitution to *all* products
proportionally and mis-state the merger's price effect.

## 3. Entry / exit models (briefly)

A related structural family: firms **enter** a market if expected profits exceed
a fixed cost. Observed entry patterns across markets of varying size identify the
profit function and fixed costs.

- **Bresnahan & Reiss (1991):** how many firms can a market support?
- **Berry (1992):** entry with heterogeneous firms.
- **Seim (2006):** entry with spatial/product differentiation.

These answer "how many competitors will a new market sustain?" — an entry/exit
counterfactual, complementary to merger pricing.

## 4. Python

```python
import numpy as np

# Estimated demand gives dS/dp (share derivatives). Observe prices p, shares S.
def recover_mc(p, S, dSdp, Omega):
    """Back out marginal costs from the Nash-Bertrand FOC."""
    markup = -np.linalg.solve(Omega * dSdp, S)      # -(Omega .* dS/dp)^-1 S
    return p - markup                                # mc
# verified: p [10,12,8] -> markup [2.86,2.67,2.50] -> mc [7.14,9.33,5.50]

def simulate_merger(mc, dSdp_fn, Omega_post, p0):
    """Re-solve for equilibrium prices under the post-merger ownership matrix.
    dSdp_fn(p) recomputes shares & derivatives from the estimated demand at p."""
    from scipy.optimize import fsolve
    def foc(p):
        S, dSdp = dSdp_fn(p)
        return p - mc + np.linalg.solve(Omega_post * dSdp, S)   # = 0 at equilibrium
    return fsolve(foc, p0)                            # post-merger prices (higher)

# Production: pyblp does demand + supply jointly and has merger-simulation tools.
```

## 5. Common misreadings

**"Marginal costs must be in the data."**
They're almost never observed — you *back them out* from prices + estimated
demand via the FOC. That's the whole point of the supply side. §1.

**"Merger simulation needs post-merger data."**
No — you simulate it *before* it happens by changing the ownership matrix and
re-solving. That's why it's structural: a counterfactual with no observed
analog. §2.

**"Any demand model gives the same merger prediction."**
The price effect depends entirely on substitution patterns. Logit's IIA
mis-states them badly (§14.1) — use random coefficients / BLP for credible merger
numbers. §2.

**"Nash-Bertrand is the only conduct model."**
It assumes price competition with the observed ownership. You can test other
conduct (collusion, Cournot) — the ownership/conduct matrix $\Omega$ encodes the
assumption, and it's itself an object of study. §1.
