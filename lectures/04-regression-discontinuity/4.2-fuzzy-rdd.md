# 4.2 — Fuzzy Regression Discontinuity

> **Part of:** [Lecture 4 — Regression Discontinuity](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [Lecture 1 §4–6](../01-foundations-and-estimands.md) — Fuzzy RDD *is* an instrumental-variables LATE. The complier typology and the Wald ratio come straight from there.

---

## 1. When the cutoff is a nudge, not a switch

Sharp RDD assumes the cutoff flips treatment from probability 0 to 1. Reality
is messier. The rule is a strong guideline that humans and systems override:

> A loan is *supposed* to auto-approve at credit > 650. But loan officers
> sometimes manually deny applicants at 655 and grant exceptions at 645. The
> probability of approval jumps from maybe **20% to 80%** at 650 — not 0 to
> 1.

This is **Fuzzy RDD**: the treatment *probability* jumps discontinuously at
the cutoff, but not all the way. The relaxed assumption is exactly that —
$P(D = 1 \mid X)$ has a discontinuity at $c$, of any size greater than zero.

---

## 2. The estimand: a local Wald ratio

Because treatment take-up is imperfect, the jump in the outcome is no longer
the effect — it's diluted by everyone who didn't comply. You **divide the
jump in the outcome by the jump in the treatment probability**:

```math
\tau_{\text{FRDD}} = \frac{\displaystyle\lim_{x \downarrow c} E[Y \mid X = x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} E[Y \mid X = x]}{\displaystyle\lim_{x \downarrow c} E[D \mid X = x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} E[D \mid X = x]}
```

This is **exactly Lecture 1's Wald estimator**, localized to the cutoff:

- **Numerator — reduced form (ITT).** The jump in the *outcome* $Y$ at $c$.
  The effect of *crossing the cutoff*, compliance and all.
- **Denominator — first stage.** The jump in *treatment probability* $D$ at
  $c$. How much crossing actually moves take-up.

The threshold indicator $Z = \mathbb{1}[X \geq c]$ is an **instrument** for
treatment $D$. Crossing the cutoff is randomly-ish assigned (by continuity)
and affects the outcome *only through* treatment (the exclusion restriction).
That is the IV recipe from Lecture 1, with "crossing the cutoff" playing the
role of "getting the offer."

**If compliance were perfect**, the denominator is 1 and the formula
collapses to sharp RDD. Fuzzy is the general case; sharp is the special one.

---

## 3. Implementation: 2SLS local linear

Estimate it as **2SLS within a bandwidth** $h$ around $c$, with the centered
running variable (§4.1 §3.3 — the same centering logic applies). Let
$Z_i = \mathbb{1}[X_i \geq c]$:

**First stage** — predict treatment from the instrument:

```math
D_i = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 Z_i + \gamma_2 (X_i - c) + \gamma_3 Z_i (X_i - c) + u_i
```

**Second stage** — outcome on *predicted* treatment:

```math
Y_i = \alpha + \tau_{\text{FRDD}} \hat{D}_i + \beta_1 (X_i - c) + \beta_2 \hat{D}_i (X_i - c) + \epsilon_i
```

Run it through a proper 2SLS routine (or `rdrobust` with a `fuzzy=` argument)
so the standard errors account for the estimated first stage — don't
hand-build the ratio and bootstrap it yourself (Lecture 1 §7 made the same
point about the Wald SE).

*Verified* (code in §7): treatment probability jumping 0.2 → 0.8 at the
cutoff, true complier effect **5.0**. Reduced-form jump = **3.01**,
first-stage jump = **0.60**, Wald ratio = 3.01 / 0.60 = **5.03**. The dilution
by non-compliance (the outcome only jumps 3, not 5) is undone by dividing by
the 0.6 first stage.

---

## 4. The one extra diagnostic: weak instruments

Fuzzy RDD needs **all four sharp-RDD diagnostics** (§4.1 §4 — density,
covariate smoothness, placebo cutoffs, bandwidth sensitivity), plus one more
that sharp doesn't:

**The first-stage F-statistic.** Look at the estimand: if the denominator
(the jump in treatment probability) is near zero, $\tau_{\text{FRDD}}$
explodes and its standard errors blow up. A cutoff that barely moves take-up
is a **weak instrument**.

- Report the **first-stage F**. The old rule of thumb is $F > 10$; the modern
  standard (Lee, McCrary, Moreira & Porter 2022) is much stricter — closer to
  $F > 104$ — for honest 5% inference.
- If your rule is overridden so often that the probability barely jumps at the
  cutoff, **you cannot use fuzzy RDD.** There's no instrument.

---

## 5. Who is the effect for? Compliance types at the cutoff

Fuzzy RDD's estimand is a LATE, and a LATE applies to **compliers only**.
This is the same principal-stratification logic as
[Lecture 1 §4](../01-foundations-and-estimands.md#4-the-estimands) — the four
types, defined by how take-up $D$ responds to the instrument $Z$ (here,
crossing the cutoff). We don't re-derive it; we *localize* it to the RDD
threshold:

| Type | $D$ when below $c$ | $D$ when above $c$ | At an RDD cutoff, these are… |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Complier** | 0 | 1 | Treated *because* they crossed — the borderline units the rule actually flips |
| **Always-taker** | 1 | 1 | Treated regardless — overridden *onto* treatment even below the cutoff |
| **Never-taker** | 0 | 0 | Untreated regardless — overridden *off* treatment even above the cutoff |
| **Defier** | 1 | 0 | Treated only if *below* — assumed not to exist (**monotonicity**) |

**Fuzzy RDD identifies the effect for compliers only** — the units whose
treatment status was flipped *specifically because* their running variable
ticked across the cutoff. It says nothing about always-takers or
never-takers, because crossing the cutoff didn't change *their* treatment, so
the discontinuity carries zero information about them. Monotonicity (no
defiers) is what makes the Wald ratio a clean complier average rather than an
uninterpretable mixture — same as Lecture 1.

## 6. Worked probe: the shadowban

**Setup.** A social platform's Trust & Safety team auto-shadowbans at
`spam_score > 80`. But human moderators override constantly:

- Users just **below** 80 get shadowbanned **15%** of the time.
- Users just **above** 80 get shadowbanned **85%** of the time.

You run fuzzy RDD and get a significant $\tau_{\text{FRDD}}$ of a **10% drop
in engagement.**

### 6.1 Who are the always-takers?

**The 15% of users shadowbanned even though their score is below 80.**

Business translation: these are users so obviously malicious that human
moderators ban them *before* the automated model flags them. They're treated
regardless of the algorithmic threshold — their score being under 80 didn't
save them. That's the definition of an always-taker: treated on both sides of
the cutoff.

(Symmetrically, the never-takers are the ~15% *above* 80 who *don't* get
banned — the "VIP false positives" whom moderators manually clear.)

### 6.2 Does the 10% drop apply to them?

**No — and this is the trap that separates senior from staff.**

$\tau_{\text{FRDD}}$ is a **LATE**, and it applies **only to compliers**: the
users whose shadowban status flipped from 0 to 1 *specifically because* their
score crossed from 79 to 80. For always-takers, the 80-point rule changed
nothing — moderators were going to ban them anyway — so the data jump at 80
contains **zero information** about their treatment effect.

The honest report to a VP:

> *"For the **marginal, borderline** users who are shadowbanned **only
> because** of this 80-point rule — the compliers — engagement drops 10%. We
> **cannot** claim this for the hardcore spammers who get banned regardless
> (always-takers), or the VIP false-positives who never get banned
> (never-takers). 'Shadowbanning drops engagement 10% platform-wide' would be
> false — it's the effect on the borderline cases the rule actually decides."*

Saying "10% across the platform" overstates the reach of the estimand to
groups the design is blind to. Naming *which* users the LATE covers — and
which it can't — is the whole skill.

## 7. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf
from linearmodels.iv import IV2SLS

# df: X (running var), D (actual treatment 0/1), Y (outcome). c = cutoff.
c = 80.0
h = 5.0
s = df[np.abs(df["X"] - c) < h].copy()
s["Xc"] = s["X"] - c
s["Z"] = (s["X"] >= c).astype(int)     # the instrument: crossing the cutoff

# ---- The Wald ratio, by hand: reduced form / first stage ----
rf = smf.ols("Y ~ Xc + Z + Z:Xc", data=s).fit().params["Z"]   # numerator (ITT)
fs = smf.ols("D ~ Xc + Z + Z:Xc", data=s).fit().params["Z"]   # denominator (first stage)
print(f"reduced form = {rf:.3f}, first stage = {fs:.3f}, Wald = {rf/fs:.3f}")
# reduced form = 3.01, first stage = 0.60, Wald = 5.03

# ---- Proper 2SLS: same point estimate, correct SEs for the estimated stage ----
fuzzy = IV2SLS(
    dependent=s["Y"],
    exog=smf.add_constant(s[["Xc"]]),
    endog=s["D"],
    instruments=s["Z"],
).fit(cov_type="robust")
print(fuzzy.summary)                   # coef on D = tau_FRDD

# ---- The extra diagnostic: first-stage F. Weak instrument => unusable. ----
first = smf.ols("D ~ Xc + Z + Z:Xc", data=s).fit()
F = first.tvalues["Z"] ** 2
print(f"first-stage F = {F:.1f}   ({'OK' if F > 104 else 'WEAK -- cannot use fuzzy RDD'})")
# Modern threshold (Lee et al. 2022) ~ 104, not the old 10.

# ---- Production: rdrobust handles the fuzzy case with bias-corrected CIs ----

# rdrobust(df["Y"], df["X"], c=c, fuzzy=df["D"])
```

## 8. Common misreadings

**"The outcome jumps 3% at the cutoff, so the effect is 3%."**
That's the reduced form (ITT). The treatment effect is the reduced form
*divided by* the first stage. If take-up jumps 0.6, the effect is 3/0.6 = 5.
§2.

**"Fuzzy RDD estimates the effect of treatment for everyone near the cutoff."**
It estimates the effect for **compliers** near the cutoff — units the rule
actually flipped. Always-takers and never-takers are invisible to it. §5, §6.

**"A positive first stage is enough."**
It must be *strong*. A weak first stage (small jump in take-up) makes the Wald
ratio explode with huge SEs. Report the F; the modern bar is ~104, not 10.
§4.

**"Shadowbanning drops engagement 10% platform-wide."**
It drops engagement 10% **for the borderline compliers**. The hardcore
spammers (always-takers) and VIP false-positives (never-takers) are outside
the estimand. §6.2.

**"Fuzzy is just sharp RDD with noise."**
No — the non-compliance is *structural*, and ignoring it (running sharp RDD
on a fuzzy design) estimates the ITT, which understates the treatment effect
by the compliance rate. §2.

**"I don't need the density test because it's fuzzy."**
You need every sharp diagnostic *plus* the first-stage F. Manipulation
invalidates fuzzy RDD exactly as it does sharp. §4.
