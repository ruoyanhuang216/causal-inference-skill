# 4.1 — Sharp Regression Discontinuity

> **Part of:** [Lecture 4 — Regression Discontinuity](./README.md)

## 1. The philosophy and the three assumptions

The intuition (README §1): a user who scores **649** on a credit check is
structurally identical to one who scores **650**. The boundary is arbitrary,
so immediately around it, treatment assignment is **as good as random**.

To lean on that mathematically, three assumptions must hold:

1. **Continuity of potential outcomes.** Absent treatment,
   $E[Y(0) \mid X]$ is a smooth, continuous function through the cutoff $c$.
   The *only* thing allowed to jump at $c$ is the treatment.
2. **No precise manipulation (no sorting).** Units — or the humans operating
   the rule — cannot precisely control $X$ to land on their preferred side.
   If the rule is known and gameable, the two sides are no longer
   comparable.
3. **Local randomization.** Very close to the cutoff, observed *and*
   unobserved confounders are balanced, exactly as in an A/B test.

Assumptions 1 and 3 are two views of the same thing; assumption 2 is the one
with teeth, because it's the one you can actually test (§4) — and its
failure is what §5 is about.

---

## 2. The estimand: a LATE at the cutoff

Sharp RDD estimates the vertical jump in the outcome at the cutoff:

```math
\tau_{\text{SRDD}} = \lim_{x \downarrow c} E[Y_i \mid X_i = x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} E[Y_i \mid X_i = x]
```

the right-side limit (treated) minus the left-side limit (control), taken
*at* $c$. As README §3 stressed: this is a **LATE at the cutoff**, not the
ATE. You learn the effect for units at a 650 score and nothing about units
at 750. "Who is this effect for?" — always "the marginal unit at the
threshold."

---

## 3. Implementation: local linear regression

### 3.1 Why not a global polynomial

The tempting move is to fit one high-order polynomial across *all* the data
and read off the jump at $c$. **Don't.** High-order global polynomials give
large weight to observations far from the cutoff and produce erratic fits at
the boundary — **Runge's phenomenon** — which is exactly where you need the
estimate to be stable. Gelman & Imbens (2019) is a whole paper on why this
biases RDD.

*Verified* (code in §6): with a curved underlying $E[Y(0)] = \sin(3X)$ and a
true jump of **1.0**, a global degree-10 polynomial returns **0.960**
(biased at the boundary), while local linear at a sensible bandwidth returns
**0.999**.

### 3.2 The standard: local linear within a bandwidth

Restrict to a narrow window $[c-h, c+h]$ and fit a line with a possibly
different slope on each side:

```math
Y_i = \alpha + \beta_1 (X_i - c) + \tau D_i + \beta_2 D_i (X_i - c) + \epsilon_i
```

where $D_i = \mathbb{1}[X_i \geq c]$. The coefficient $\tau$ is the estimand
— the vertical jump at the cutoff. $\beta_2$ lets the slope differ across the
cutoff (usually it does; forcing equal slopes is a common, avoidable bias).

### 3.3 The `X − c` centering — why we subtract the cutoff

**This is the detail that trips people up, so here is exactly what it does
and why.**

We don't put raw $X_i$ in the regression; we put the **centered** running
variable $X_i - c$. The reason is precise: it makes $\tau$ — the coefficient
on $D_i$ — read off *directly* as the jump at the cutoff.

Walk the limits. With centering, $X_i - c = 0$ exactly at the cutoff, so
every slope term vanishes there:

- Approaching from **below** ($D=0$, $X \to c$): $E[Y] = \alpha$.
  So $\alpha$ is the left limit — the counterfactual outcome at the cutoff.
- Approaching from **above** ($D=1$, $X \to c$): $E[Y] = \alpha + \tau$.
  So the right limit is $\alpha + \tau$.

Subtract: **$\tau = (\alpha + \tau) - \alpha =$ the jump.** The centering is
what collapses all the slope machinery to zero *at the cutoff*, leaving
$\tau$ as a clean vertical gap and $\alpha$ as the baseline value there.

**What goes wrong without it.** Use raw $X_i$ and the coefficient on $D_i$ is
*no longer the jump*. The algebra: the true jump becomes $\tau + \beta_2 c$,
because the slope-interaction term $\beta_2 D_i X_i$ no longer vanishes at
the cutoff (it equals $\beta_2 c$ there). The $D$ coefficient alone is off by
$\beta_2 c$.

*Verified, and the gap is not subtle* (code in §6). True jump **5**, cutoff
$c = 50$, cross-cutoff slope difference $\beta_2 = 1.5$:

```
  CENTERED    coef on D =   5.03   <- equals the jump
  UNCENTERED  coef on D = -69.87   <- off by beta2 * c = 75
              (recover the jump as  D_coef + beta2 * c = 5.03)
```

The uncentered coefficient isn't just imprecise — it has the *wrong sign and
a wildly wrong magnitude*, because $\beta_2 c = 75$ swamps the true effect of
5. Center your running variable. It costs one subtraction and it's the
difference between reading the answer off directly and reading off garbage.

*(This same centering appears in Fuzzy RDD (§4.2) and, as slope-centering, in
RKD (§4.3). Learn it once here.)*

### 3.4 Bandwidth and kernel

**Bandwidth $h$ is the whole ballgame**, and it's a bias-variance tradeoff
made literal:

- **Too wide** → you include units far from the cutoff whose outcome curve
  bends away from the local slope → **bias**.
- **Too narrow** → too few observations → **variance**.

*Verified*: on the $\sin(3X)$ DGP with true jump 1.0, local linear returns
**0.999** at $h=0.1$ but drifts to **1.198** at $h=0.5$ — the wide window
pulls in curvature and biases the estimate up 20%. You can *see* the tradeoff
in a single sweep.

Don't guess $h$. Use the **MSE-optimal bandwidth** (Calonico, Cattaneo &
Titiunik — the `rdrobust` default), which formally balances squared bias
against variance, and pair it with **bias-corrected robust confidence
intervals** (naive CIs at the MSE-optimal $h$ under-cover, because that $h$
deliberately leaves some bias in).

**Kernel:** weight observations inside the window by closeness to the cutoff.
A **triangular kernel** gives full weight at $c$ and decays linearly to zero
at $c \pm h$ — optimal for boundary estimation, and the sensible default. A
uniform kernel (equal weight) is fine but slightly less efficient.

## 4. Diagnostics: the four tests that make it defensible

Present an RDD without these and it gets rejected in review.

### 4.1 Density test — McCrary (2008) / Cattaneo-Jansson-Ma (2020)

**Tests: no manipulation.** Plot the density (histogram) of the running
variable. If units are gaming the cutoff, there's an unnatural **spike just
on the favorable side** and a hole just below. A smooth density through $c$
is the pass; a pile-up is the fail. This is the single most important RDD
diagnostic, because manipulation is the one violation the design can't
survive (§5). Modern version: the Cattaneo-Jansson-Ma local-polynomial
density test (`rddensity`), which needs no pre-binning.

### 4.2 Covariate smoothness

**Tests: only treatment jumps at the cutoff.** Re-run the exact local-linear
RDD with a **pre-treatment covariate** (user age, tenure, prior spend) as the
outcome. Every such $\tau$ **must be statistically zero.** If age jumps at
the cutoff, some confounder moves with treatment and your effect is
contaminated. This is the RDD analogue of a balance table.

### 4.3 Placebo cutoffs

**Tests: the jump is unique to the real rule.** Estimate $\tau$ at *fake*
cutoffs where no rule exists (if the real cutoff is 650, try 600 and 700).
They must all be zero. A nonzero placebo means your specification
manufactures jumps out of ordinary curvature.

### 4.4 Bandwidth sensitivity

**Tests: the result isn't a fragile artifact of one window.** Plot $\hat\tau$
against a range of bandwidths. It should stay stable and significant across
reasonable $h$. A result that only appears at one magic bandwidth is noise.

## 5. Worked probe: the driver bonus

**Setup.** A ride-share company runs a weekend incentive: **"Complete 50
rides this weekend, get a \$100 bonus."** The Ops team wants to know if the
bonus increased retention. You reach for sharp RDD:

- **Running variable $X$:** weekend rides completed.
- **Cutoff $c$:** 50.
- **Treatment $D$:** received the \$100 bonus.
- **Outcome $Y$:** days active next month.

### 5.1 What does the McCrary density test show?

**A massive spike at exactly 50, with a hole at 48–49.** Picture a driver at
48 rides on Sunday night who checks the app, sees they're two away from
\$100, and forces two more rides. The density that *would* have been a smooth
bell curve instead collapses just below 50 and piles up at exactly 50.

Anyone who can affect the running variable and knows the rule will sort. Here
the rule is public, the running variable is fully under the driver's control,
and the reward is immediate and large. Sorting is essentially guaranteed —
**this design fails the density test almost by construction**, and you should
predict that before running it.

### 5.2 Why does the failure invalidate the comparison?

Because it breaks continuity by shifting an **unobserved confounder** exactly
at the cutoff.

RDD needs a driver at 49 to be a clone of a driver at 50. Under sorting they
are not:

- The **50-ride** group is now disproportionately **"hustlers"** —
  full-time, financially driven, high-effort drivers who pushed for the
  payout.
- The **49-ride** group is disproportionately **"casuals"** — part-time,
  didn't care enough to do one more ride.

**Intrinsic motivation — an unobserved confounder — has jumped discretely at
50.** So if the 50-group retains better next month, you cannot tell whether
the \$100 caused it or whether that group is simply full-time hustlers who
would have retained anyway. The treatment and the confounder are perfectly
tangled at the exact point your design relies on being clean.

**The staff-level framing:** *"This isn't a clean RDD. Drivers control the
running variable and know the rule, so they sort at 50 — the density test
will show heavy bunching. The units at 50 are self-selected high-motivation
drivers, and motivation drives retention independently of the bonus. The
discontinuity conflates the \$100 with a discrete jump in who these drivers
are. I'd instead run a randomized bonus, or find a threshold drivers can't
see or control."*

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# panel: X (running variable), Y (outcome). c = cutoff.
c = 50.0
df["Xc"] = df["X"] - c                 # CENTER -- see section 3.3
df["D"] = (df["X"] >= c).astype(int)

# ---- Local linear RDD within bandwidth h. tau = coef on D = the jump. ----
def rdd(df, c, h):
    s = df[np.abs(df["X"] - c) < h].copy()
    s["Xc"] = s["X"] - c
    fit = smf.ols("Y ~ Xc + D + D:Xc", data=s).fit(cov_type="HC1")
    return fit.params["D"], fit.bse["D"]

tau, se = rdd(df, c, h=5)
print(f"tau (jump at cutoff) = {tau:.3f}  (SE {se:.3f})")

# ---- WHY we center: the D coefficient is the jump ONLY when centered ----
cen = smf.ols("Y ~ Xc + D + D:Xc", data=df).fit()
unc = smf.ols("Y ~ X  + D + D:X",  data=df).fit()
print(f"centered   D coef = {cen.params['D']:.2f}   <- the jump")
print(f"uncentered D coef = {unc.params['D']:.2f}   <- off by beta2*c")
print(f"  recover: {unc.params['D'] + unc.params['D:X'] * c:.2f}")
# centered 5.03 ; uncentered -69.87 ; recovered 5.03

# ---- Diagnostic 1: density / manipulation (use rddensity in practice) ----
# from rddensity import rddensity          # Cattaneo-Jansson-Ma test
# rddensity(df["X"].values, c=c)           # look for a spike just above c

# ---- Diagnostic 2: covariate smoothness -- every tau MUST be ~0 ----
for cov in ["age", "tenure", "prior_spend"]:
    t, s = rdd(df.assign(Y=df[cov]), c, h=5)
    flag = "" if abs(t) < 2 * s else "  <-- JUMPS, confound!"
    print(f"  covariate {cov:12s} tau = {t:+.3f} (SE {s:.3f}){flag}")

# ---- Diagnostic 3: placebo cutoffs -- all must be ~0 ----
for fake in [c - 10, c - 5, c + 5, c + 10]:
    t, _ = rdd(df[df.X != c].assign(D=(df.X >= fake).astype(int)), fake, h=5)
    print(f"  placebo cutoff {fake}: tau = {t:+.3f}")

# ---- Diagnostic 4: bandwidth sensitivity -- should be stable ----
for h in [2, 3, 5, 8, 12]:
    t, s = rdd(df, c, h)
    print(f"  h={h:2d}: tau = {t:+.3f} (SE {s:.3f})")

# ---- Production: rdrobust gives MSE-optimal h + bias-corrected CIs ----
# from rdrobust import rdrobust
# rdrobust(df["Y"], df["X"], c=c, kernel="triangular")
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"RDD gives the average treatment effect."**
It gives a **LATE at the cutoff** — the effect for the marginal unit at the
threshold, nobody else. §2.

**"Fit a flexible global polynomial to capture the shape."**
High-order global polynomials cause Runge's phenomenon at the boundary and
bias the jump. Use local linear in a bandwidth. §3.1.

**"Whether I center X doesn't matter, it's just reparametrization."**
It changes what the $D$ coefficient *means*. Uncentered, it's off by
$\beta_2 c$ — in the verified example, −69.87 instead of 5. Center. §3.3.

**"I'll pick a bandwidth that gives a clean result."**
That's specification-shopping. Use the MSE-optimal $h$ and *show the
sensitivity plot* — the result must survive across bandwidths. §3.4, §4.4.

**"The RDD is significant, so it's valid."**
Significance is not identification. If the density test shows sorting (§5),
you have a precisely-estimated confounded number. Run the density and
covariate-smoothness tests *first*.

**"Same slope on both sides is simpler."**
Forcing $\beta_2 = 0$ imposes an equal-slope restriction that biases $\tau$
when the slopes genuinely differ. Always include the interaction. §3.2.

**"The bonus increased retention — the 50-ride group retained better."**
Only if there's no sorting. When drivers game the cutoff, the 50-group is
self-selected hustlers, and you can't separate the bonus from motivation. §5.
