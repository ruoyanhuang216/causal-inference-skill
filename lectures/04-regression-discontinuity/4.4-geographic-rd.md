# 4.4 — Geographic Regression Discontinuity

> **Part of:** [Lecture 4 — Regression Discontinuity](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§4.1](./4.1-sharp-rdd.md) — the identification, diagnostics, and manipulation logic carry over; what changes is the geometry.

---

## 1. The cutoff is a border

Two regions split by an administrative boundary — a state line, a school-
district edge, a tax zone, a server-cluster map. Policy A applies on one
side, Policy B on the other. **The boundary is the cutoff.**

The running variable becomes **distance to the nearest point on the
boundary**:

- On the boundary → $X = 0$.
- In the treated region → $X > 0$.
- In the control region → $X < 0$.

A house 200 meters into the treated county is compared to a house 200 meters
into the control county, and the same intuition as sharp RDD applies: right
at the border, which side you're on is close to arbitrary, so treatment is as
good as random.

---

## 2. Why it's architecturally different

Geographic RD is not just "sharp RDD with distance." Two structural changes:

**1. A 2D boundary, not a 1D point.** Standard RDD has one cutoff value. A
border is a *line*, and every point on it is its own little RDD. You're
running a local regression on a **2D surface** — you move *across* the border
(the running variable) *and along* it (a whole second dimension of
variation). Collapsing to scalar distance throws away *where along the border*
each unit sits.

**2. The border-selection problem.** Not all stretches of a border are
comparable. If a state line runs through a dense city at one end and empty
desert at the other, the treatment effect at the city border can differ
completely from the desert border. You must check for **spatial
heterogeneity** — a single pooled number can average over wildly different
local effects.

---

## 3. The estimand and estimation

The effect is the outcome jump across the border, **averaged along the entire
boundary**:

```math
\tau_{\text{GRD}} = \int_{\text{border}} \left( \lim_{x \downarrow 0} E[Y \mid \text{lat}, \text{lon}] - \lim_{x \uparrow 0} E[Y \mid \text{lat}, \text{lon}] \right) ds
```

where $ds$ is the line element along the border. In practice you don't
integrate analytically — you **bin the border into segments**, run a local
RDD at each segment (on distance-to-boundary), and aggregate:

- **Grid / binning:** chop the border into small pieces, estimate the local
  discontinuity for each, then average (weighting by segment population or
  length).
- **Boundary fixed effects:** include a fixed effect for each border segment,
  so you compare treated-vs-control *within* a segment, absorbing the
  along-border variation of §2.
- **Local polynomial in distance** with the same centering/bandwidth/kernel
  logic as §4.1, plus **spatially-clustered standard errors** (nearby units
  are correlated — clustering on segment or a spatial grid is essential,
  the geographic cousin of the DiD clustering in [§3.1](../03-difference-in-differences/3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#3-inference-the-standard-error-is-where-did-dies)).

---

## 4. Diagnostics: the spatially-aware toolkit

GRD is uniquely prone to **spatial confounds** — the border may coincide with
a river, a mountain range, a historical dividing line that affects the
outcome directly. The standard RDD tests need spatial versions:

### 4.1 Placebo borders

Draw a **fake boundary inside the treated region**, where no policy changes.
Run the GRD. A significant effect at the fake border means your model is
picking up **natural spatial patterns** (population clusters, income
gradients) rather than the policy. This is the geographic placebo-cutoff test
and the most important GRD diagnostic.

### 4.2 Spatial covariate smoothness

Map your covariates. For a tax change, plot **median income** across the
border. It should be a *continuous gradient*, not a cliff. If income drops
sharply right at the state line, you're comparing rich to poor, not measuring
the tax — the border coincides with a pre-existing economic divide.

### 4.3 Distance-based bandwidth sensitivity

Choose the bandwidth $h$ in kilometers. Show the result holds whether you look
at units within 1 km, 3 km, or 5 km of the border. A finding that only appears
at one radius is fragile.

---

## 5. Worked probe: Roblox creator royalties

**Setup.** Roblox tests a **Creator Royalty** model:

- **Region A** (a set of server-clusters): **50%** revenue share.
- **Region B** (neighboring clusters): **30%** share.
- **The border:** clusters assigned by a **server-latency map** — essentially
  a circle around a server hub.

You suspect creators are **clustering near the border** to get into the
higher-paying Region A.

### 5.1 Detecting the gaming — a spatial density test

Run a **McCrary-style density test on distance-to-boundary.** Take every
creator's signed distance to the border ($X < 0$ in Region B, $X > 0$ in
Region A) and plot the density.

- **Clean:** a smooth density through $X = 0$ — creators are distributed by
  where they naturally are, indifferent to the border.
- **Gamed:** a **spike just inside Region A** ($X$ slightly $> 0$) and a
  **hole just inside Region B** — creators relocating (changing their latency
  profile, spoofing location, migrating server affinity) to land on the 50%
  side.

Visually it's a histogram of distance with a discontinuous pile-up at $0^+$;
mathematically it's the same density-continuity test as §4.1, run on the
spatial running variable. A pile-up on the favorable side is manipulation —
and, exactly as in §4.1 and §4.3, it means an unobserved trait (here, the
creators most motivated by revenue, likely the most commercial and
highest-earning) has concentrated right at the border.

### 5.2 Proving the bunching is policy-driven — the placebo border

Bunching alone doesn't prove *the royalty policy* caused it — maybe creators
naturally cluster near server hubs for **latency** reasons (better ping),
independent of royalties.

The **placebo-border test** separates the two:

1. Draw a **fake border** deep **inside Region A** — around a *different*
   server hub where the royalty share is 50% on *both* sides (no policy
   change).
2. Run the same density test on distance to *this* fake border.

- **If there's bunching at the real border but NOT the fake one:** creators
  pile up only where the *money* changes, not where *latency* changes. The
  sorting is **policy-driven** — the royalty gap causes it. Your suspicion is
  confirmed and the RDD is compromised.
- **If there's bunching at BOTH borders:** creators cluster near *every*
  server hub regardless of royalties — it's a **latency preference**, not
  royalty gaming. The density spike is a natural spatial pattern, not
  manipulation of *your* cutoff.

**Staff framing:** *"The real-border density spike could be royalty-gaming or
just latency clustering. The placebo border — a fake boundary inside Region A
with no royalty change — distinguishes them. Bunching at the real border but a
smooth density at the placebo proves creators sort on the revenue gap, not on
latency. That confirms the No-Sorting violation and tells the Product team the
50/30 split itself is driving the relocation."*

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# df: lat, lon, Y (outcome), plus signed distance_to_border (>0 treated side),
# and border_segment id. c = 0 (the border).

df["X"] = df["distance_to_border"]        # signed; 0 on the border
df["D"] = (df["X"] >= 0).astype(int)      # treated side

# ---- Local RDD in distance, with boundary-segment fixed effects and ----
# ---- spatial clustering (nearby units are correlated). ----
def geo_rdd(df, h_km):
    s = df[np.abs(df["X"]) < h_km].copy()
    fit = smf.ols("Y ~ X + D + D:X + C(border_segment)", data=s).fit(
        cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": s["border_segment"]})
    return fit.params["D"], fit.bse["D"]

tau, se = geo_rdd(df, h_km=3)
print(f"tau at border = {tau:.3f} (SE {se:.3f})")

# ---- Spatial density test: bunching just inside the treated side? ----
# Histogram of signed distance; look for a spike at X -> 0+ (the Roblox probe).

# ---- Placebo border: fake boundary INSIDE the treated region ----
def placebo_border(df, fake_center_km):
    d = df.copy()
    d["X"] = d["distance_to_border"] - fake_center_km   # shift the "border"
    d["D"] = (d["X"] >= 0).astype(int)
    return geo_rdd(d, h_km=3)                            # should be ~0 and smooth density

# ---- Spatial covariate smoothness: median income must be a gradient ----
for cov in ["median_income", "population_density"]:
    t, s = geo_rdd(df.assign(Y=df[cov]), h_km=3)
    print(f"  covariate {cov}: jump = {t:+.3g} (SE {s:.3g}; want ~0)")

# ---- Bandwidth sensitivity in km ----
for h in [1, 2, 3, 5]:
    t, s = geo_rdd(df, h)
    print(f"  h={h}km: tau = {t:+.3f} (SE {s:.3f})")
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"Geographic RD is just RDD with distance as the running variable."**
The boundary is 2D — you vary *across* and *along* it. Collapsing to scalar
distance ignores along-border heterogeneity; use boundary-segment fixed
effects. §2, §3.

**"One pooled τ describes the whole border."**
The effect can differ from the city end to the desert end. Check spatial
heterogeneity; a single number may average over opposite local effects. §2.

**"Standard errors are fine as-is."**
Nearby units are spatially correlated. Cluster on border segment (or a
spatial grid), or your SEs are far too small — the same lesson as DiD
clustering. §3.

**"Bunching at the border proves policy-gaming."**
It might be a natural spatial preference (latency, amenities). The **placebo
border** distinguishes policy-driven sorting from natural clustering. §5.2.

**"The border is the cleanest possible cutoff."**
Borders often coincide with rivers, mountains, or historical divides that
affect the outcome directly. Spatial covariate smoothness is essential — a
cliff in income at the line means you're comparing rich to poor, not
measuring the policy. §4.2.
