# 4.3 — Regression Kink Design (RKD)

> **Part of:** [Lecture 4 — Regression Discontinuity](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§4.1](./4.1-sharp-rdd.md) — RKD is RDD with slopes instead of levels; the centering, bandwidth, and diagnostics all carry over.

---

## 1. Level vs. slope

Sharp RDD needs a *jump* in treatment at the cutoff. But many policies don't
switch on and off — they change **intensity** at a smooth-but-kinked rate:

- **RDD world:** score > 650 → a flat \$500 bonus. A **jump**.
- **RKD world:** credit limit rises \$100 for every \$1,000 of income — until
  income hits \$100,000, where it **caps**. Above the cap, every extra dollar
  of income adds \$0 of limit. A **kink**.

Because the treatment amount is a *continuous* function of the running
variable, there is **no vertical jump** at the cutoff. What changes is the
**slope** of the treatment. RKD asks: does the slope of the *outcome* change
sharply at the same point? If the credit formula bends at \$100k and spending
bends at exactly \$100k too, the bend in spending is caused by the bend in
credit.

```
   Policy B (credit limit)          Outcome Y (spend)
      │        ____                     │        ____
      │       /                         │       /
      │      /  ← kink at c             │      /  ← does Y kink here too?
      │     /                           │    _/
      │    /                            │   /
      │   /                             │  /
      └──────────┼──────  X            └──────────┼──────  X
                 c                                 c
      slope changes by gamma2           slope changes by alpha2
```

---

## 2. The estimand: a ratio of slope changes

RKD divides the **change in the outcome's slope** at the cutoff by the
**change in the policy's slope** at the cutoff:

```math
\tau_{\text{RKD}} = \frac{\displaystyle\lim_{x \downarrow c} \frac{\partial E[Y \mid X=x]}{\partial x} - \lim_{x \uparrow c} \frac{\partial E[Y \mid X=x]}{\partial x}}{\displaystyle\lim_{x \downarrow c} \frac{\partial B(x)}{\partial x} - \lim_{x \uparrow c} \frac{\partial B(x)}{\partial x}}
```

- **Numerator:** the change in the *derivative* (slope) of the outcome at $c$.
- **Denominator:** the change in the *derivative* of the policy function at
  $c$ — a known, often exact quantity (you wrote the formula).

It's the RDD skeleton (README §4) with derivatives in place of levels:
sharp RDD is *jump ÷ 1*, fuzzy RDD is *jump ÷ jump-in-probability*, RKD is
*slope-change ÷ slope-change*.

---

## 3. Identifying assumptions

Slightly stronger than sharp RDD, because you're identifying a *derivative*:

1. **Smoothness of the underlying relationship.** Absent the kinked policy,
   $E[Y(0) \mid X]$ must be *continuously differentiable* — no natural kinks
   in the outcome. (Sharp RDD only needs continuity; RKD needs smooth
   slopes.)
2. **No sorting / precise manipulation** — same as RDD (§4.1), and here the
   density test looks for a **kink in the density**, not a jump.
3. **Monotonicity** (for fuzzy RKD) — the slope change pushes everyone the
   same direction.

---

## 4. Implementation: local *quadratic* regression

Because you're estimating **slopes (derivatives), not levels (intercepts)**,
local *linear* regression is often too rigid — a straight line has one slope
and can't flex to capture the true derivative at the boundary. Standard
practice is **local quadratic** within the bandwidth, giving the fit enough
curvature to estimate the slope accurately at the kink.

**Policy first stage** (the known rule, but estimate it to be safe):

```math
B_i = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 (X_i - c) + \gamma_2 D_i (X_i - c) + \text{error}
```

$\gamma_2$ is the **change in the policy's slope** at the cutoff
($D_i = \mathbb{1}[X_i \geq c]$; the centering is §4.1 §3.3 again, now on the
slope terms).

**Outcome reduced form:**

```math
Y_i = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 (X_i - c) + \alpha_2 D_i (X_i - c) + \text{error}
```

$\alpha_2$ is the **change in the outcome's slope**. The causal effect is the
ratio:

```math
\tau_{\text{RKD}} = \frac{\alpha_2}{\gamma_2}
```

*Verified* (code in §6): a credit formula with slope 0.1 that caps at the
cutoff (so the policy slope changes by $\gamma_2 = -0.1$), true effect of
credit on spend $= 0.8$. The fit recovers $\gamma_2 = -0.100$,
$\alpha_2 = -0.079$, and $\tau_{\text{RKD}} = \alpha_2/\gamma_2 = -0.079 /
-0.10 = \mathbf{0.794}$ against a true **0.8**.

**RKD is data-hungry.** Estimating a *change in a slope* is far noisier than
estimating a change in a level, so RKD needs large samples and high density
around the kink. If you have thousands of points, sharp RDD may be feasible
where RKD is hopeless.

## 5. Worked probe: the FinTech credit cap

**Setup.** A FinTech issues corporate cards. The policy: **\$100 of credit
limit per \$1,000 of cash balance**, hard-**capped at \$50,000** once a
startup holds **\$500,000** in cash. You want the causal effect of credit
limit on transaction volume, via RKD at the \$500k kink.

You examine the running variable's density and find **massive bunching at
\$490k–\$500k** — startups artificially parking exactly half a million.

### 5.1 Why does the bunching break the design?

Same logic as §4.1's driver bonus, one level up: **sorting concentrates an
unobserved trait exactly at the kink.**

Ask *why* a startup would park exactly \$500k. Because a CFO reverse-
engineered the credit formula, realized \$501k yields zero extra credit, and
optimized their treasury to extract the maximum \$50k limit while parking the
rest elsewhere. The trait suddenly concentrated at the kink is **financial
sophistication + credit hunger.**

The population is now **bifurcated**:

- **Just at the kink (\$490–500k):** hyper-optimizing, credit-hungry startups.
  Being credit-hungry, their transaction volume $Y$ is high relative to
  balance.
- **Just above (\$510–550k):** startups that *organically* happen to have
  \$500k sitting around — cash-rich, not optimizing, not desperate for credit.

### 5.2 Why the causal read is ruined

RKD needs the *only* thing changing at \$500k to be the slope of the credit
limit. Instead, the **type of user** changes discretely there: hustlers pile
up at the kink, and just past it you're looking at a different, less-active
demographic.

So when the RKD math finds a change in the slope of spending at \$500k, it is
**not** the credit cap causing it — it's the composition shift. You cannot
separate the effect of the *credit cap* from the effect of *who chose to sit
at the cap*. Treatment intensity and an unobserved confounder both bend at
the same point. **The design is invalid**, and the density kink test is what
exposes it.

**Staff framing:** *"The \$500k density bunching means the No-Sorting
assumption fails. Sophisticated, credit-hungry startups optimize onto the cap;
past it the population is cash-rich and passive. The slope change in spending
at \$500k conflates the credit cap with a discrete shift in financial
sophistication. I can't identify the credit effect here — I'd need a cap
startups can't see or optimize against, or exogenous variation in the limit."*

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# df: X (running var, e.g. cash balance), B (policy amount), Y (outcome). c = kink.
c = 500_000.0
h = 100_000.0
s = df[np.abs(df["X"] - c) < h].copy()
s["Xc"] = s["X"] - c
s["D"] = (s["X"] >= c).astype(int)

# ---- Slope change in the POLICY (gamma2) ----
# For RKD prefer local QUADRATIC: add I((Xc)**2) and its interaction.
policy = smf.ols("B ~ Xc + D:Xc + I(Xc**2) + D:I(Xc**2)", data=s).fit()
gamma2 = policy.params["D:Xc"]

# ---- Slope change in the OUTCOME (alpha2) ----
outcome = smf.ols("Y ~ Xc + D:Xc + I(Xc**2) + D:I(Xc**2)", data=s).fit()
alpha2 = outcome.params["D:Xc"]

tau_rkd = alpha2 / gamma2
print(f"gamma2 = {gamma2:.4f}, alpha2 = {alpha2:.4f}, tau_RKD = {tau_rkd:.3f}")
# gamma2 = -0.100, alpha2 = -0.079, tau_RKD = 0.794   (true 0.8)

# ---- Diagnostic: density KINK test (not just a jump) ----
# Look for a change in the SLOPE of the running variable's histogram at c.
# A kink in the density => sorting => design invalid (the FinTech probe).

# ---- Diagnostic: covariate smoothness -- alpha2 on covariates must be ~0 ----
for cov in ["founder_age", "company_age"]:
    a = smf.ols(f"{cov} ~ Xc + D:Xc + I(Xc**2) + D:I(Xc**2)", data=s).fit().params["D:Xc"]
    print(f"  covariate {cov}: slope-change = {a:.4g} (want ~0)")

# ---- Diagnostic: placebo kinks at non-policy points -- must be ~0 ----
# ---- Production: rdrobust(..., deriv=1) estimates the kink (derivative) ----
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"RKD needs a jump like RDD."**
No — RKD has *no* jump. The treatment is continuous; only its **slope**
changes. You compare slope-changes, not levels. §1–2.

**"τ is α₂ times γ₂."**
It's the **ratio** $\alpha_2 / \gamma_2$ — outcome slope-change divided by
policy slope-change. §4.

**"Use local linear, like sharp RDD."**
Estimating a derivative needs more flexibility. Local **quadratic** is
standard for RKD; local linear is too rigid to pin the boundary slope. §4.

**"The density test is the same as RDD."**
For RKD you look for a **kink in the density**, not a jump. The FinTech
startups don't pile onto one exact value — they bend the distribution as they
optimize toward the cap. §3, §5.

**"RKD and RDD need the same sample size."**
RKD is far noisier — a slope-change is harder to estimate than a level-jump.
It needs large samples and dense data at the kink. §4.

**"Bunching at the cap is fine, we still see a slope change."**
The slope change is the *artifact* — sorting bifurcates the population at the
kink, so the bend reflects a composition shift, not the policy. §5.2.
