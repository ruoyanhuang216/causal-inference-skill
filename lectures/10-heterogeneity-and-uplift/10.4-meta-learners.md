# 10.4 — Meta-Learners for CATE

> **Part of:** [Lecture 10 — Heterogeneous Effects & Uplift](./README.md)
> **Prerequisites:** [§10.1 Causal Forests](./10.1-causal-forests.md), [Lecture 8 §8.3–8.4](../08-selection-on-observables/) (IPW, AIPW), [Lecture 9 — DML](../09-double-machine-learning/).

A causal forest ([§10.1](./10.1-causal-forests.md)) forces you to use trees. A
**meta-learner** is the *architectural wrapper* that turns **any** predictor you
already trust — XGBoost, LightGBM, a neural net — into a CATE estimator. It's a
*recipe*: how to split the data, train standard models, and combine their
outputs to estimate $\tau(x)$ instead of $Y$. (**CATE = uplift = heterogeneous
treatment effects** — one object, three names, three teams.)

The five learners are an evolution, each fixing the previous one's flaw.

---

## 1. S-learner — one model

**Recipe:** train a single model on $Y \sim X + D$, with treatment $D$ as *just
another feature*. Score by predicting with $D{=}1$ and $D{=}0$ and subtracting:

```math
\hat\tau(x) = \hat\mu(x, 1) - \hat\mu(x, 0)
```

**Fatal flaw — regularization bias.** If $X$ strongly predicts $Y$ but the
treatment effect is *small*, the learner treats $D$ as noise and **prunes it
out** — splitting never bothers with a feature that barely moves the loss. The
predicted CATE is then **~0 for everyone.** S-learners systematically
*under*-estimate.

*Verified* (code in §7): strong baseline ($4X_0 + 3X_1 + 2X_2$), true uniform
effect **0.50**. The S-learner returns a mean CATE of **0.024** — it dropped $D$
— while a T-learner recovers **0.511**.

**Use:** a baseline only, or when the effect is known large and uniform. In
production, **almost never.**

---

## 2. T-learner — two models

**Recipe:** train $\hat\mu_1$ on the *treated* and $\hat\mu_0$ on the *control*,
separately, then subtract:

```math
\hat\tau(x) = \hat\mu_1(x) - \hat\mu_0(x)
```

This *guarantees* $D$ can't be pruned — it's baked into the data split. But the
two models **can't share information.**

**Fatal flaw — data starvation.** If one arm is tiny (a 1% promo), its model
trains on almost nothing, overfits wildly, and you subtract a high-variance
model from an accurate one → garbage CATE.

**Use:** large, **balanced** experiments (≈50/50) where the arms' baseline
behavior genuinely differs.

---

## 3. X-learner — cross-learner (the imbalanced-data fix)

Künzel et al. (2019) built the X-learner to cure T-learner starvation. Four
steps:

1. **T-learner base:** fit $\hat\mu_1$, $\hat\mu_0$.
2. **Impute counterfactuals.** For each *treated* unit, fill its missing baseline
   with the strong control model: $\tilde D_i = Y_i - \hat\mu_0(X_i)$. Mirror for
   controls: $\tilde D_i = \hat\mu_1(X_i) - Y_i$.
3. **Model the imputed effects.** Fit $\hat\tau_1$ on the *treated* (predicting
   their $\tilde D$) and $\hat\tau_0$ on the *control*.
4. **Combine, weighting toward the reliable arm:**
   ```math
   \hat\tau(x) = \hat e(x)\,\hat\tau_0(x) + \big(1 - \hat e(x)\big)\,\hat\tau_1(x)
   ```

**Why it beats the T-learner when treated is rare** — two mechanisms:

- **Borrow the baseline.** The 9,900 controls build a rock-solid $\hat\mu_0$, so
  the 100 treated need only learn the *delta* $Y - \hat\mu_0$ — smoother and far
  easier than learning absolute revenue from 100 rows.
- **Weight the trustworthy estimator.** When treated is rare, $\hat e$ is small,
  so $(1 - \hat e) \approx 1$ puts almost all weight on **$\hat\tau_1$** — the
  model that learned the clean delta *from the treated* — and away from
  $\hat\tau_0$, whose targets $\hat\mu_1(X)-Y$ are polluted by the weak
  treated-arm model. **Getting this direction right is the whole trick: weight
  $\hat\tau_1$ by $1-\hat e$, not by $\hat e$.** (Flip it and the X-learner is
  *worse* than the T-learner — a real bug I hit building this.)

*Verified* (§7): at **1% treatment** (108 treated of 10,000) with a complex
baseline, the T-learner's CATE MSE is **1.08**; the X-learner's is **0.29** — a
**73% error reduction.**

**Use:** highly imbalanced treatment (a 1% VIP rollout, a rare medical
treatment). The gold standard there.

---

## 4. R-learner and DR-learner — the observational standard

These bring Lectures 8–9 to CATE.

**R-learner (residual)** — **DML ([Lecture 9](../09-double-machine-learning/9.1-dml-core.md)) for uplift.**
Cross-fit-residualize $\tilde Y = Y - \hat E[Y\mid X]$ and $\tilde D = D - \hat
E[D\mid X]$, then fit a final model minimizing the residual-on-residual loss
$(\tilde Y - \tau(x)\tilde D)^2$. It targets $\tau(x)$ directly and inherits
Neyman orthogonality. **Flaw:** sensitive to poorly-tuned nuisances. **Use:**
heavy observational confounding.

**DR-learner (doubly robust)** — **AIPW ([Lecture 8 §8.4](../08-selection-on-observables/8.4-doubly-robust-aipw.md)) turned into a CATE model.**
Compute the AIPW pseudo-outcome per user, then regress it on $X$. Two chances to
be right (outcome *or* propensity model). **Flaw:** the AIPW score's
inverse-propensity term **explodes** under weak overlap (Lecture 8 §8.3).
**Use:** the modern default for observational data *with good common support.*

## 5. Does counterfactual prediction actually give the causal lift? — your two questions

Be skeptical here: meta-learners solve a missing-data problem with prediction,
and prediction can lie. Both failure modes **fail silently** — no error metric
warns you.

### 5.1 The mechanism, and when it's valid

For treated user $i$ you observe $Y_i(1)$; the T-learner imputes the missing
$Y_i(0)$ by passing $X_i$ through $\hat\mu_0$ — "what did similar *control* users
do?" The difference is the CATE. **This is valid only under unconfoundedness**
([Lecture 8 §8.1](../08-selection-on-observables/8.1-propensity-and-unconfoundedness.md)):
once you condition on $X$, treatment is as-good-as-random.

**If $D$ is fully determined by observed $X$, that's *fine*** — it *is*
unconfoundedness holding — provided overlap also holds (§5.2). Strong
$X \to D$ is not the enemy; **hidden** drivers of $D$ are (§5.3).

### 5.2 Question 1 — different X-spaces → extrapolation bias

*"If the treated and control populations differ massively in $X$, isn't asking
$\hat\mu_0$ to predict a treated user's baseline pure guessing?"* **Yes — it's
the overlap violation** $0 < P(D{=}1\mid X) < 1$. Give VIP *only* to companies
over \$1M ARR, and $\hat\mu_0$ **never saw** a \$5M-ARR company — predicting its
counterfactual is blind extrapolation into empty space, driven by the learner's
functional form, not data. Three guardrails:

1. **Propensity trimming.** Fit $\hat e(X)$; drop treated with $\hat e > 0.95$
   and control with $\hat e < 0.05$ — units with no peers in the other arm.
2. **Shift the estimand to the ATO.** Say it: *"we can't estimate the lift for
   everyone — only the **ATO**
   ([Lecture 8 §8.3](../08-selection-on-observables/8.3-inverse-probability-weighting.md#5-taming-the-tails--trimming-overlap-weights-and-the-ato)),
   the effect on the marginal, middle-of-the-pack users."* Trimming *changes what
   you estimate*; honesty about that is the staff move.
3. **Causal forests structurally protect.** A `min_samples_leaf` forcing both
   arms into every leaf ([§10.1 §2](./10.1-causal-forests.md#2-the-causal-split--and-how-it-uses-both-arms))
   *refuses* to isolate a pure-$D{=}1$ region — local overlap by construction,
   where a meta-learner extrapolates blindly.

### 5.3 Question 2 — unobserved confounding → silent, fatal bias

*"What if an unknown $U$ drives both $D$ and $Y$?"* **Then every meta-learner,
causal forest, and DML is fatally biased, and no amount of ML rescues it** — the
Lecture 8 golden rule: *these methods control only for the confounders you feed
them.* If VIP opt-in and next-year spend are both driven by an unlogged
**budget expansion** $U$, $\hat\mu_1$ sees VIPs spending heavily and credits the
*program* for what the *budget* caused — the CATE is massively inflated.

**How biased?** In the linear case, omitted-variable bias is exactly:

```math
\text{Bias} \;\approx\; (\text{effect of } U \text{ on } Y) \times (\text{effect of } U \text{ on } D)
```

A $U$ that strongly raises the outcome *and* strongly skews selection produces a
large inflation; one that drives only one of the two is harmless. Since $U$ is
unobserved you **bound** it with **sensitivity analysis** — most cleanly the
**E-value** (*how strong would $U$ have to be, in association with both $D$ and
$Y$, to erase the effect?*). E-value ≈ 1.2 → a trivial confounder overturns it
(**fragile, don't ship**); ≈ 4 → only an implausibly strong $U$ could (**robust**).
**The full toolkit — E-value, Oster's $\delta$, Cinelli-Hazlett, Rosenbaum
bounds — is [Lecture 11](../11-sensitivity-and-partial-id/).** If
domain knowledge says $U$ is large *and* the E-value is low, **abandon ML and
pivot to a design** — IV (Lecture 5), DiD (Lecture 3), RDD (Lecture 4), or a
randomized experiment. A CATE without a sensitivity number is not a finished
analysis.

## 6. The selection matrix

The interview gives a *constraint* and asks you to pick. This is the cheat
sheet.

| Learner | Architecture | Fatal flaw | Deploy when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S** | one model, $Y \sim X + D$ | **regularization bias** — prunes $D$, $\hat\tau \to 0$ | baseline only; effect known huge & uniform |
| **T** | $\hat\mu_1$, $\hat\mu_0$ separately | **data starvation** — tiny arm overfits | large, **balanced** experiments |
| **X** | T-base → impute → model deltas → propensity-weight | 4 models to tune/maintain | **highly imbalanced** (1% VIP, rare treatment) |
| **R** | DML residual-on-residual | sensitive to nuisance tuning | **heavy observational confounding** |
| **DR** | AIPW scores → regress on $X$ | IPW term **explodes** at weak overlap | **observational, good overlap** (modern default) |

**The decision tree (memorize this):**

1. Heavily **imbalanced** experiment? → **X-learner**
2. **Balanced** experiment? → **T-learner** (or Causal Forest, §10.1)
3. **Observational and messy** (good overlap)? → **DR-learner** (or R-learner if
   overlap is the worry — it doesn't invert the propensity)
4. Ship an **S-learner**? → **almost never.**

## 7. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor, RandomForestClassifier
rf = lambda leaf=10: RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=150, min_samples_leaf=leaf, n_jobs=-1)

# ---- S-learner: one model, D as a feature (watch it prune D -> tau ~ 0) ----
s = rf().fit(np.column_stack([X, D]), Y)
tau_S = (s.predict(np.column_stack([X, np.ones(len(X))]))
         - s.predict(np.column_stack([X, np.zeros(len(X))])))    # mean ~0.024 vs true 0.50

# ---- T-learner ----
m1, m0 = rf().fit(X[D==1], Y[D==1]), rf().fit(X[D==0], Y[D==0])
tau_T = m1.predict(X) - m0.predict(X)

# ---- X-learner (the imbalanced-data fix) ----
imp_t = Y[D==1] - m0.predict(X[D==1])          # treated: actual - imputed baseline
imp_c = m1.predict(X[D==0]) - Y[D==0]          # control: imputed treated - actual
t1 = rf(leaf=3).fit(X[D==1], imp_t)            # delta model on the (few) treated
t0 = rf().fit(X[D==0], imp_c)                  # delta model on the (many) control
e = np.clip(RandomForestClassifier(min_samples_leaf=20).fit(X, D).predict_proba(X)[:,1], .01, .99)
tau_X = e * t0.predict(X) + (1 - e) * t1.predict(X)   # weight t1 by (1-e): dominates when treated rare
# verified at 1% treatment: T-learner MSE 1.08 -> X-learner MSE 0.29 (-73%)

# ---- R / DR: use the DML (Lecture 9) and AIPW (Lecture 8) machinery ----
# Production: econml (SLearner, TLearner, XLearner, DRLearner, CausalForestDML),
#             causalml (Uber). Don't hand-roll for production.
```

## 8. Worked probe: the 1% VIP rollout

**Setup.** 10,000 corporate clients; a VIP concierge went to **100** (1%). Build
an uplift model to expand the program. A junior proposes a **T-learner** —
XGBoost on the 100 VIPs, XGBoost on the 9,900 standard.

**Q1 — what happens to the model on the 100 VIPs, and how does it infect
$\hat\tau$?** It **starves.** 100 rows cannot learn a high-dimensional baseline;
$\hat\mu_1$ overfits, and its predictions are high-variance noise. Subtracting an
accurate 9,900-row control model from a noisy 100-row treated model yields
**garbage CATE** — you can't distinguish a real persuadable VIP from noise.

**Q2 — how does the X-learner use the 9,900 controls to help the 100 VIPs?**

> *"We use the massive control group to build a rock-solid baseline for the VIPs.
> (1) Train $\hat\mu_0$ on the 9,900 — with that much data it accurately predicts
> standard-tier behavior. (2) Pass the 100 VIPs through it: what would they have
> spent in the standard tier? (3) Subtract from their actual VIP spend to get an
> **imputed uplift** per VIP. (4) Train the effect model on the 100 VIPs to
> predict that *delta* — not absolute revenue from 100 rows. The baseline
> economics were handled entirely by the 9,900 controls; the VIP model only
> learns the lift. That's why, with treated scarce, the weighting leans on the
> treated-delta model $\hat\tau_1$ (its targets are clean) — cutting CATE error
> 73% versus the T-learner."*

*Caveat that outranks the estimator choice:* 1% opt-in is a screaming **overlap
and confounding risk.** Which 100 self-selected? If a hidden budget expansion
drove both opt-in and spend (§5.3), *no* meta-learner is valid — trim for
overlap, run a sensitivity analysis, and consider randomizing the next expansion
cohort.

## 9. Common misreadings

**"The S-learner is simplest, so start there in production."**
It reports $\hat\tau \approx 0$ when the effect is small — verified 0.024 vs true
0.50. Baseline only; almost never shipped. §1.

**"The T-learner is unbiased, so it's safe for any split."**
It starves the rare arm. At 1% treatment its CATE is noise (73% worse than X).
§2–3, §8.

**"The X-learner weights the estimator from the bigger group."**
It weights the one with the *clean* target: when treated is rare, weight
$\hat\tau_1$ (the treated-delta model) by $1-\hat e \approx 1$. Flip the
direction and it's *worse* than the T-learner. §3.

**"If $X$ perfectly determines $D$, my model is broken."**
The opposite — that's unconfoundedness *holding*, as long as overlap holds too.
The killer is $D$ determined by an **unobserved** $U$. §5.1, §5.3.

**"ML with enough features controls for confounding."**
Only *observed* confounders. A hidden $U$ inflates the CATE silently; bound it
with a sensitivity analysis or pivot to a design. §5.3.

**"R-learner and DR-learner are just fancier T-learners."**
They're the CATE forms of DML and AIPW — they *residualize* or
*doubly-robust-correct* for confounding the T-learner ignores. Different
machinery, different guarantees. §4.
