# 10.4 — Meta-Learners for CATE

> **Part of:** [Lecture 10 — Heterogeneous Effects & Uplift](./README.md)
> **Prerequisites:** [§10.1 Causal Forests](./10.1-causal-forests.md), [Lecture 8 AIPW](../08-selection-on-observables/8.4-doubly-robust-aipw.md), [Lecture 9 DML](../09-double-machine-learning/).

A causal forest is one specific algorithm. **Meta-learners are architectural
*recipes*** — blueprints that take *any* off-the-shelf predictor (XGBoost,
LightGBM, a neural net) and wire it up to estimate the CATE $\tau(x)$ instead
of the outcome $Y$. Same goal as the causal forest (per-user uplift), but you
bring your own base learner.

> **CATE = uplift.** Academic econometrics calls it the Conditional Average
> Treatment Effect; industry marketing/CRM/pricing calls it *uplift modeling*.
> Identical object, two vocabularies.

---

## 1. The five learners

They form an evolution — each fixes the previous one's flaw.

### S-learner (Single)

**Recipe:** one model on $(X, D)$ with treatment as just another feature;
$\hat\tau(x) = \hat\mu(x, 1) - \hat\mu(x, 0)$.

**Fatal flaw — regularization bias.** If $X$ strongly predicts $Y$ but $D$'s
effect is small, the learner treats $D$ as noise and **prunes it out** — so
$\hat\tau \approx 0$ for everyone. *Verified* (§6): with $X$ driving $Y$ hard
and a true effect of 0.30, the S-learner returns **0.005** (annihilated) while
a T-learner recovers **0.303**. S-learners systematically *under*-estimate.

**Use:** a baseline, or when the effect is known to be large and uniform.

### T-learner (Two)

**Recipe:** $\hat\mu_1$ trained on the treated, $\hat\mu_0$ on the control;
$\hat\tau(x) = \hat\mu_1(x) - \hat\mu_0(x)$.

**Fatal flaw — data starvation.** It prevents $D$ from being pruned but can't
share information across arms. If one arm is tiny, its model overfits, and
subtracting a wild model from a stable one gives garbage. *Verified* (§4): at
1% treatment the T-learner's CATE error is **31× worse** than the X-learner's.

**Use:** balanced (≈50/50) experiments where baseline behavior differs across
arms.

### X-learner (Cross)

**Recipe** (Künzel et al. 2019), built to fix the T-learner's imbalance
problem:

1. Fit a T-learner ($\hat\mu_1$, $\hat\mu_0$).
2. **Impute** individual effects: for a *treated* unit,
   $\tilde D_i = Y_i - \hat\mu_0(X_i)$ (actual minus imputed control); for a
   *control* unit, $\tilde D_i = \hat\mu_1(X_i) - Y_i$.
3. Train *new* models $\hat\tau_1$ (on the treated's imputed effects) and
   $\hat\tau_0$ (on the control's).
4. Combine, **propensity-weighted:**
   $\hat\tau(x) = e(x)\,\hat\tau_0(x) + (1 - e(x))\,\hat\tau_1(x)$.

**Why it wins on imbalance:** with few treated ($e$ small), the weight lands
on $\hat\tau_0$ — the model trained on the *abundant* control group. The huge
control arm builds a rock-solid baseline; the tiny treated arm only has to
learn the *delta*, not the whole economics. **Use:** highly imbalanced data
(1% rollouts, rare treatments).

### R-learner (Residual)

**It is literally DML for uplift** (Lecture 9). Residualize $\tilde Y = Y -
\hat E[Y\mid X]$ and $\tilde D = D - \hat E[D\mid X]$ with cross-fitting, then
fit a final model minimizing the residual-on-residual loss
$(\tilde Y - \tau(X)\tilde D)^2$. **Use:** heavy observational confounding —
$X$ drives both $D$ and $Y$ and you need to partial it out first. **Flaw:**
sensitive to badly-tuned nuisance models (Lecture 9 §9.2).

### DR-learner (Doubly Robust)

**It is AIPW turned into a CATE model** (Lecture 8 §8.4). Compute the
doubly-robust (AIPW) pseudo-outcome for every user, then regress it on $X$
with any learner. **Two chances to be right** (outcome *or* propensity model).
**Use:** the modern default for observational data *with good overlap*.
**Flaw:** the IPW term explodes under extreme propensities — needs common
support (Lecture 8 §8.3).

## 2. How does predicting a counterfactual give the causal lift? And is it biased?

The honest core of your question. A meta-learner solves the missing-data
problem (Lecture 1 §2) by building a **mathematical clone**: for a treated user
we observe $Y_i(1)$, so we predict their missing $Y_i(0)$ by passing $X_i$
through $\hat\mu_0$ — the model asks "what did *control* users who look like
this do?" The difference $\hat\mu_1(X_i) - \hat\mu_0(X_i)$ is the CATE.

This is valid **only under the Lecture 8 assumptions**, and your two worries map
onto exactly the two ways it breaks.

### 2.1 If $D$ is determined by $X$ — fine, *until* overlap fails

If observed $X$ fully drives assignment, that's **unconfoundedness**
($\{Y(1),Y(0)\} \perp D \mid X$) — the *best* case. Conditioning on $X$ makes
assignment as-good-as-random, and the counterfactual prediction is valid.

**But if $X$ drives $D$ too *strongly*, overlap dies** (Lecture 8 §8.1). Say
you offered the VIP program *only* to companies over \$1M ARR. Then $\hat\mu_0$
**never saw a \$1M+ company** — no big companies were in control. Asking it to
predict their counterfactual is **blind extrapolation**: the tree walks off the
edge of its data and the "lift" becomes an artifact of the learner's functional
form. This is the extrapolation trap of §3.

### 2.2 If $D$ and $Y$ share an unobserved cause $U$ — fatal, silently

If a variable $U$ *not in your features* drives both $D$ and $Y$, **every
meta-learner, causal forest, and DML is biased, and nothing rescues them.**

The VIP example: $U$ = a client's internal budget expansion. High-$U$ clients
opt into VIP ($D=1$) *and* spend more ($Y$) — but $U$ isn't in your data. So
$\hat\mu_1$ sees "VIP clients spend a lot" and attributes the *budget's* effect
to the *program*. $\hat\tau$ is massively inflated: you report "+\$100k per
client" when they'd have spent it anyway.

**The rule:** these algorithms control for the confounders you *feed* them. A
hidden variable driving both selection and outcome makes them **fail silently**
— a confident, wrong ROI. When you suspect strong unobserved confounding, you
**abandon selection-on-observables and pivot to a design** — IV (Lecture 5),
DiD (Lecture 3), RDD (Lecture 4). The magnitude of that bias, and how to
stress-test for it, is §3.2.

## 3. The two failure modes, and what to do

### 3.1 Extrapolation — populations differ in $X$-space

When treated and control barely overlap in $X$, predicting one arm's baseline
from the other's model is guessing. Three guardrails:

- **Propensity trimming.** Fit $e(X)$; drop treated units with $e(X) > 0.95$
  and control units with $e(X) < 0.05$ — units with no counterparts. Train only
  on the overlapping region.
- **Shift the estimand, and say so.** After trimming you no longer estimate the
  ATE — you estimate the **ATO** (Average Treatment effect on the Overlap,
  [Lecture 8 §8.3](../08-selection-on-observables/8.3-inverse-probability-weighting.md#5-taming-the-tails--trimming-overlap-weights-and-the-ato)):
  the lift for the *marginal, middle-of-the-pack* users, not the extremes.
- **Causal forests give structural protection.** A `min_samples_leaf` that
  forces both arms into every leaf ([§10.1](./10.1-causal-forests.md#2-the-causal-split--and-how-it-uses-both-arms))
  makes the tree *refuse* splits that isolate a pure-treated region — common
  support is enforced locally, by construction. This is a real edge of forests
  over naive meta-learners.

### 3.2 Unobserved confounding — quantifying the bias

You can't compute the exact bias (it depends on the thing you can't see), but
you can bound it. In the linear case, omitted-variable bias is:

```math
\text{Bias} \;\approx\; \underbrace{(\text{effect of } U \text{ on } Y)}_{\text{how much } U \text{ moves the outcome}} \times \underbrace{(\text{effect of } U \text{ on } D)}_{\text{how much } U \text{ skews selection}}
```

A confounder that both strongly raises the baseline outcome *and* strongly
predicts selection produces a large inflation. Since $U$ is unobserved, you
report a **sensitivity analysis** alongside the estimate — most cleanly, the
**E-value** (VanderWeele & Ding 2017): *how strong would an unmeasured
confounder have to be — in association with both $D$ and $Y$ — to explain away
the entire effect?*

- **E-value 1.2** — a confounder needs only a 20% association with both to erase
  the effect. **Fragile; don't ship.**
- **E-value 4.0** — it would need a 4× association with both. Real-world hidden
  variables rarely have that magnitude. **Robust; ship with confidence.**

If domain knowledge says $U$ is large (hidden intent) and the E-value is low,
**scrap the ML approach and get a design or a randomized experiment.** The full
sensitivity toolkit — E-value, Rosenbaum bounds, Oster's $\delta$,
Cinelli-Hazlett — is **Lecture 11**; here it's enough to know that a CATE
estimate without a sensitivity number is not a finished analysis.

## 4. Worked probe: the 1% VIP rollout

**Setup.** 10,000 B2B clients; a VIP concierge given to only **100 (1%)**;
9,900 in the standard tier. Build an uplift model to expand the program wisely.
A junior proposes a **T-learner** (XGBoost on the 100 VIPs, XGBoost on the 9,900
standard).

**Q1 — what happens to the VIP model, and how does it infect $\hat\tau$?** The
treated model $\hat\mu_1$ trains on **~100 rows** and **severely overfits** —
memorizing noise, wild out-of-sample. The control model $\hat\mu_0$ (9,900 rows)
is accurate. Subtracting a wild model from a stable one gives **garbage CATEs**.
*Verified* (§6): T-learner CATE error is **31× worse** than the X-learner's at
1% treatment (MSE 2.71 vs 0.09).

**Q2 — how does the X-learner use the 9,900 controls to help the 100 VIPs?**

> *"We use the massive control group to build a rock-solid baseline for the
> VIPs. (1) Train $\hat\mu_0$ on the 9,900 — with that much data it accurately
> predicts standard-tier behavior from $X$. (2) Pass the 100 VIPs through it to
> get their counterfactual: what they'd have spent in the standard tier.
> (3) Subtract that from their actual VIP spend to get an **imputed uplift** per
> VIP. (4) Train the effect model on the 100 VIPs to predict that *delta* — not
> absolute revenue from 100 rows. The heavy lifting of baseline economics is
> done by the 9,900 controls; the VIP model only learns the lift, which is why
> the propensity-weighting leans on the control-trained effect model when
> treated data is scarce."*

That is the X-learner's whole reason to exist, and why it's the gold standard
for imbalanced rollouts.

## 5. The selection matrix

The interview rarely asks for formulas — it gives a *constraint* and asks you
to pick. This is the cheat sheet.

| Learner | Architecture | Fatal flaw | Deploy when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S** | one model on $(X, D)$ | **Regularization bias** — prunes $D$, $\hat\tau \to 0$ | Baseline only; or effect known large & uniform |
| **T** | separate $\hat\mu_1$, $\hat\mu_0$ | **Data starvation** — no info sharing; tiny arm overfits | Balanced (≈50/50) experiments; baselines differ by arm |
| **X** | T-learner → impute → cross-train → propensity-weight | **4 models** to build & maintain | **Highly imbalanced** (1% rollout, rare treatment) |
| **R** | residualize $Y, D$ on $X$ (DML), fit residual-on-residual | Sensitive to nuisance tuning | **Heavy observational confounding**; isolate the signal |
| **DR** | AIPW pseudo-outcome, then regress on $X$ | IPW term **explodes** at extreme propensities | Modern default for observational data **with good overlap** |

Two quick decision rules: **imbalanced → X-learner**; **confounded observational
with good overlap → DR-learner** (or R-learner if overlap is the worry, since it
doesn't invert the propensity).

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor
def rf(): return RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=200, min_samples_leaf=10, n_jobs=-1)

# S-learner (watch it shrink tau -> 0 when the effect is small vs X's signal)
s = rf().fit(np.column_stack([X, D]), Y)
tau_S = s.predict(np.column_stack([X, np.ones(len(X))])) - \
        s.predict(np.column_stack([X, np.zeros(len(X))]))     # ~0.005 vs true 0.30

# T-learner
mu1, mu0 = rf().fit(X[D==1], Y[D==1]), rf().fit(X[D==0], Y[D==0])
tau_T = mu1.predict(X) - mu0.predict(X)

# X-learner (the fix for imbalance)
imp_t = Y[D==1] - mu0.predict(X[D==1])       # treated: actual - imputed control
imp_c = mu1.predict(X[D==0]) - Y[D==0]        # control: imputed treated - actual
t1 = rf().fit(X[D==1], imp_t)                 # effect model on the (few) treated
t0 = rf().fit(X[D==0], imp_c)                 # effect model on the (many) control
e = D.mean()                                  # propensity (or a full e(X) model)
tau_X = e * t0.predict(X) + (1 - e) * t1.predict(X)   # few treated -> leans on t0

# Production: econml (SLearner, TLearner, XLearner, DRLearner, ...), causalml.
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"The S-learner is simplest, so start there in production."**
It's the most prone to reporting $\hat\tau = 0$ when the effect is small —
verified 0.005 vs 0.30. Use it as a baseline, not a shipped model. §1.

**"The T-learner is unbiased, so it's safe."**
Only with balanced arms. Under imbalance the small-arm model overfits and the
CATE is garbage (31× worse than X). §1, §4.

**"Predicting the counterfactual with ML *makes* it causal."**
It's causal only under unconfoundedness *and* overlap. Bad overlap → blind
extrapolation; unobserved $U$ → silent, inflated bias. §2.

**"If $X$ perfectly determines $D$, my model is best."**
Perfect determination is an *overlap violation* — no comparable controls exist,
and $\hat\mu_0$ extrapolates. Some unexplained randomness in $D$ is required.
§2.1, §3.1.

**"A big CATE means the program works."**
Not without a sensitivity check. An unobserved confounder can inflate it
arbitrarily; report an E-value, and if it's low, pivot to a design. §3.2.

**"R-learner and DR-learner are just fancier T-learners."**
They're the CATE forms of DML and AIPW — they *residualize* or *doubly-robust-
correct* for confounding that the T-learner ignores. Different machinery,
different guarantees. §1.
