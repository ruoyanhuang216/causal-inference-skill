## 1. Standard forest vs. causal forest

A **random forest** groups users with similar *outcomes* $Y$ — its splits
maximize the difference in average $Y$ between child nodes ("who will churn?").
A **causal forest** (Athey & Wager 2018) groups users with similar *treatment
effects* $\tau$ — its splits maximize the difference in $\tau$ between children
("for whom does the discount work?").

The forest is an ensemble of **causal trees**, and everything interesting
happens in how one tree splits.

---

## 2. The causal split — and how it uses both arms

This is the mechanic that answers the natural objection: *"if you split on how
people respond to treatment, aren't you only looking at the treated? Then how
do you ever estimate the untreated baseline $E[Y \mid X, D=0]$?"*

**The answer: a causal tree keeps both treated and control units in every node,
the whole way down.** It never conditions on $D=1$.

Walk one split. A parent node has 1,000 users from a randomized experiment —
~500 treated, ~500 control. Its within-node effect:

```math
\hat\tau_{\text{parent}} = \operatorname{mean}(Y \mid D=1) - \operatorname{mean}(Y \mid D=0)
```

Say treated averaged \$100, control \$80 → $\hat\tau_{\text{parent}} = \$20$.
Now the algorithm tests a split, say **Age > 30**, and sends *both* treated and
control users down *both* branches:

| Child | Treated mean | Control mean | $\hat\tau$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Age ≤ 30 | \$50 | \$50 | **\$0** |
| Age > 30 | \$150 | \$110 | **\$40** |

The split objective **maximizes the heterogeneity** — the gap between
$\hat\tau_{\text{left}}$ and $\hat\tau_{\text{right}}$ (here \$0 vs \$40, a big
spread), *not* the gap in outcomes. It searches every feature and threshold for
the most treatment-effect-splitting cut and locks it in.

**So the $D=0$ baseline is estimated dynamically inside every leaf**, from the
control users who naturally landed there. A new 35-year-old drops into the
"Age > 30" leaf and gets CATE = \$40, because that leaf's historical controls
made \$110 and its treated made \$150.

### The engineering constraint this forces

Because every leaf computes a treated-minus-control difference, **each leaf
must contain enough of *both* arms.** If a deep split strands a leaf with only
treated users, $\operatorname{mean}(Y \mid D=0)$ is undefined and the math
collapses. Causal forests therefore enforce a stricter `min_samples_leaf` than
standard forests — one that guarantees a valid local A/B test in every bucket.
This is why causal forests are hungrier for data than predictive forests: every
leaf is a mini-experiment, and experiments need sample in both arms.

## 3. Honesty — valid confidence intervals

Search a big dataset for subgroups with large treatment effects and you will
find *noise* that looks like signal — the same overfitting that plagues subgroup
analysis. Athey & Wager's fix is **honesty.**

Each tree splits its training data in two:

1. **Splitting subsample** — used *only* to choose the tree's geometry (that
   Age > 30 and Device = iOS is the right split structure).
2. **Estimating subsample** — a *fresh* half, poured down the already-built
   tree. Each leaf's effect $\hat\tau$ is computed **only** from these unseen
   units.

Decoupling *where to split* from *what the effect is* is what prevents the tree
from both finding a subgroup and inflating its effect on the same data. It's the
[cross-fitting idea of Lecture 9](../09-double-machine-learning/9.1-dml-core.md)
applied within a tree, and it's what lets the forest emit **asymptotically valid
confidence intervals** for every user's CATE — not just point estimates.

## 4. Local centering — and "is the splitting X the controlling X?"

Modern causal forests (EconML, `grf`) bolt on **DML-style local centering**
before the forest is built. Using cross-fitted ML, residualize *out* the
covariates:

```math
\tilde{Y} = Y - \hat{E}[Y \mid X], \qquad \tilde{D} = D - \hat{E}[D \mid X]
```

then grow the causal forest **on the residuals** $(\tilde Y, \tilde D)$. This
strips the baseline variance and any confounding from the signal (Lecture 9
§3), so the forest spends its capacity on *heterogeneity* rather than
re-learning the baseline. It's why local-centered causal forests are far more
sample-efficient.

**Your question: is the $X$ used to *control* (residualize) the same $X$ used to
*split*?** By default, **yes — the same feature set plays two distinct roles:**

1. **As controls (nuisance):** $X$ enters $\hat E[Y\mid X]$ and $\hat E[D\mid
   X]$ to partial out baseline and confounding. Their job is to be *absorbed*.
2. **As effect modifiers (splitting):** $X$ is what the tree splits on to
   discover *where* $\tau$ varies. Their job is to *carve up* the population.

The same variable can do both — "tenure" both predicts baseline renewal (a
control) and modifies the call's effect (a splitter). **But you can, and often
should, use different sets.** Two common reasons:

- **Actionability / interpretability:** control on the *full* rich $X$ (500
  telemetry features) to remove confounding, but restrict *splitting* to a
  handful of **actionable, business-legible** features (tenure, plan, region),
  so the resulting policy is one Ops can deploy (§10.3). You don't want a
  targeting rule that hinges on `p99_query_latency_ms`.
- **Avoiding spurious heterogeneity:** fewer splitting features means fewer
  chances to discover noise-driven subgroups.

So: same $X$ is the default and is fine, but treating the **control set** and
the **splitting set** as separate design choices is the staff-level move —
control on everything, split on what you can act on.

## 5. The output and how to use it

`model.predict(X)` returns **$\hat\tau(x)$ for every user**, not $Y$. That per-
user CATE is the raw material for targeting (§10.2) and policy (§10.3): sort
users by $\hat\tau$, and spend the budget at the top.

```python
# Production: EconML (Microsoft) or grf (R). Sketch of the API:
from econml.grf import CausalForest          # or econml.dml.CausalForestDML for local centering
cf = CausalForest(n_estimators=2000, min_samples_leaf=50, honest=True)
cf.fit(X=X, T=D, y=Y)                          # both arms required
tau_hat = cf.predict(X_new)                    # per-user CATE
lb, ub = cf.predict_interval(X_new, alpha=0.05)  # honest CIs, thanks to section 3

# CausalForestDML adds local centering (section 4): residualize Y, D on X first.

# CausalForestDML(model_y=..., model_t=..., discrete_treatment=True)
```

## 6. Common misreadings

**"To model treatment response, filter to the treated and predict $Y$."**
That estimates $E[Y\mid X, D=1]$ — the *outcome*, not the *effect*. You've
thrown away the control arm and the baseline. It's the §10.2 sure-things trap.
A causal tree keeps both arms in every leaf. §2.

**"A causal forest just predicts who converts."**
It predicts the *lift* $\tau(x)$ — the difference the treatment makes. Ranking
by $\tau$ ≠ ranking by $P(Y)$. §1, §10.2.

**"Deeper trees give sharper subgroups."**
Only until a leaf runs out of one arm — then $\hat\tau$ is undefined. Causal
forests need a strict `min_samples_leaf` guaranteeing both arms per leaf. §2.

**"Honesty is wasteful — why use half the data just to estimate?"**
That waste buys *valid confidence intervals*. Without it, the same data finds
and inflates subgroups, and your CIs are fiction. §3.

**"Local centering and splitting must use the same features."**
Same set is the default, but control-on-all / split-on-actionable is often
better — it removes all confounding while keeping the policy deployable. §4.
