## 1. The sure-things trap — a worked probe

**Setup.** A telecom runs a randomized experiment: 50% of users got a
"Proactive Support Call" ($D=1$), outcome $Y=1$ if they renewed. You want a
targeting model: whom should we call next month to save the most accounts?

A junior DS skips causal methods. They **filter to $D=1$** (only called users),
train a random-forest classifier to predict renewal $Y$ from features $X$, sort
by predicted renewal probability, and hand the call center the **top 10,000**.

### 1.1 Whom did they just target?

**The "sure things" — the most loyal customers.** By modeling $P(\text{renew}
\mid \text{called})$ and taking the top, they grabbed users with ~99% renewal
probability: the happiest, most engaged, least-likely-to-leave accounts.

### 1.2 Why it's a massive waste

Those users **were going to renew anyway.** A standard model cannot distinguish:

- renewed *because of* the call (a real save), from
- renewed *despite* the call (already loyal).

The **causal effect of calling a sure thing is ~zero** — you spent thousands of
agent-hours to save zero incremental accounts.

*Verified* (code in §4): on held-out randomized data, targeting the top 10% by
**predicted renewal** produces an actual uplift of **−0.005** (statistically
zero — the segment's true $\tau$ is 0.00), while targeting the top 10% by
**CATE** produces an actual uplift of **+0.535**. Same budget, same 10% of
users called; one saves nothing, the other saves half a renewal per call.

### 1.3 How a causal forest ranks them differently

A causal forest ranks by $\tau$ — *how much the call changes* renewal — not by
$P(\text{renew})$:

| Type | $P(\text{renew}\mid\text{call})$ | $P(\text{renew}\mid\text{no call})$ | $\tau$ | Causal rank |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sure thing** (junior's pick) | 0.99 | 0.99 | **0.00** | bottom — don't call |
| **Lost cause** | 0.05 | 0.05 | **0.00** | bottom — don't call |
| **Persuadable** | 0.80 | 0.20 | **+0.60** | **top — call!** |

The persuadable is the only one worth a call: likely to churn if ignored,
likely saved if reached. Standard ML targets the top row; causal ML targets the
bottom row. That is the entire difference, and it's the §README quadrant made
numeric.

## 2. From CATE to a targeting policy

### 2.1 The ROI threshold

A positive $\hat\tau$ is not enough — treatment costs money. If a call costs
$c = \$15$ and $\hat\tau_i$ is the incremental value of calling user $i$:

```math
\textbf{Call user } i \iff \hat\tau_i > c
```

Sort users by $\hat\tau_i$ descending, draw the line where predicted
incremental value crosses the cost $c$, and hand the call center everyone above
it. This is the entire targeting policy: **rank by lift, cut at cost.**

### 2.2 Watch the sleeping dogs

A subtlety the ROI rule handles automatically but a naive "treat likely
converters" policy does not: **sleeping dogs** have $\tau < 0$ — the treatment
*backfires* (a retention call reminds a wavering customer to cancel). Because
they have negative CATE, the $\hat\tau > c$ rule excludes them. But an *outcome*
model, or a "treat everyone with positive predicted renewal" heuristic, will
happily call them and *destroy* value. Targeting on $\tau$ is the only policy
that avoids actively harming your own metric.

## 3. Proving it works offline — the Qini curve

The Hiring Manager's question: *"Before production, how do you prove your causal
forest beats a plain churn model?"* You **cannot** use RMSE or AUC — you never
observe both $Y(1)$ and $Y(0)$ for the same user, so there's no ground-truth
$\tau_i$ to score against. The uplift-specific metric is the **Qini curve**
(and its area, **AUUC**):

1. On a **held-out randomized** test set, score users with your model and rank
   by $\hat\tau$ descending.
2. Walk down the ranking (top 10%, 20%, …). At each cut, compute the *actual*
   treated-minus-control difference in outcomes among the users **above** the
   cut — a real within-slice A/B estimate, valid because assignment was random.
3. Plot cumulative incremental outcome vs. fraction targeted.

A model that works **shoots up steeply on the left** (the top-ranked users
deliver most of the total uplift) and flattens. Compare two models by their
**AUUC**: higher area = the ranking concentrates uplift better. *Verified*
(§4): the CATE ranking's AUUC beats the outcome model's — it front-loads the
persuadables, which the outcome model scatters.

The random-targeting baseline is the diagonal; the gap between your curve and
the diagonal is the value your targeting adds over calling people at random.

## 4. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier

# Randomized data: X, D (0/1), Y. Held-out test with true randomization.
# --- Junior: predict renewal from the TREATED only (the trap) ---
junior = RandomForestClassifier(min_samples_leaf=30).fit(Xtr[Dtr==1], Ytr[Dtr==1])
score_outcome = junior.predict_proba(Xte)[:, 1]

# --- Causal: T-learner CATE (or a real causal forest, section 10.1) ---
m1 = RandomForestClassifier(min_samples_leaf=30).fit(Xtr[Dtr==1], Ytr[Dtr==1])
m0 = RandomForestClassifier(min_samples_leaf=30).fit(Xtr[Dtr==0], Ytr[Dtr==0])
score_cate = m1.predict_proba(Xte)[:,1] - m0.predict_proba(Xte)[:,1]

# --- Actual uplift in the top decile (valid because the test set is randomized) ---
def top_uplift(score, frac=0.1):
    top = np.argsort(-score)[:int(frac*len(score))]
    d, y = Dte[top], Yte[top]
    return y[d==1].mean() - y[d==0].mean()
print(f"outcome-ranked top 10%: uplift = {top_uplift(score_outcome):+.3f}")  # -0.005 (sure things!)
print(f"CATE-ranked top 10%:    uplift = {top_uplift(score_cate):+.3f}")     # +0.535 (persuadables)

# --- ROI threshold: call iff predicted incremental value exceeds cost ---
COST = 15.0
to_call = score_cate > COST      # (score in $ terms for a value outcome)

# --- Qini / AUUC: cumulative uplift down the ranking ---
def auuc(score):
    o = np.argsort(-score); d, y = Dte[o], Yte[o]
    ct, cc = np.cumsum(d).clip(1), np.cumsum(1-d).clip(1)
    upl = (np.cumsum(d*y)/ct - np.cumsum((1-d)*y)/cc) * (np.arange(1,len(o)+1)/len(o))
    return np.trapz(upl, np.arange(1,len(o)+1)/len(o))
# Production: causalml.metrics.qini_score / plot_qini, or econml's policy tools.
```

## 5. Common misreadings

**"Target the users most likely to convert."**
That's the outcome model — it targets sure things, whose causal lift is ~0.
Verified: −0.005 uplift. Target by $\tau$, not $P(Y)$. §1.

**"Any user with positive predicted renewal is worth calling."**
Positive *renewal* ≠ positive *lift*. And it will hit sleeping dogs ($\tau<0$),
actively destroying value. Filter on $\hat\tau > c$. §2.

**"Call everyone with $\hat\tau > 0$."**
Only if treatment is free. With a cost $c$, call iff $\hat\tau > c$ — the
incremental value must clear the incremental cost. §2.1.

**"Use AUC to validate the uplift model."**
You can't — there's no per-user ground-truth $\tau$. Use the Qini curve / AUUC
on randomized held-out data. §3.

**"A high Qini means the effect is large."**
Qini measures how well the *ranking* concentrates uplift, not the ATE's size.
A model can rank well even when the average effect is modest. §3.
