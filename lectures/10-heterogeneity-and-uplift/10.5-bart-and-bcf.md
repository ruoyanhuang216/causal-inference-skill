# 10.5 — BART & Bayesian Causal Forests

> **Part of:** [Lecture 10 — Heterogeneous Effects & Uplift](./README.md)
> **Prerequisites:** [§10.1 Causal Forests](./10.1-causal-forests.md), [§10.4 Meta-Learners](./10.4-meta-learners.md) (BART is the Bayesian S-learner and inherits its flaw).

Everything so far gives you a *point estimate* of the CATE. The hardest
question in targeting is the next one: **how confident are you?** If your uplift
model says "call this user, +\$50," is the 95% interval $[+48, +52]$ or
$[-100, +200]$? Frequentist CATE (causal forests, meta-learners) buys that
interval with expensive machinery — honesty (throw away half the data),
bootstrapping (retrain 1,000×). **BART gives you the uncertainty for free**,
because uncertainty is its native output.

---

## 1. What BART is

**Bayesian Additive Regression Trees** — the Bayesian cousin of XGBoost. Both
are tree ensembles; the *construction* is the difference:

- **Boosting (XGBoost):** greedily grows trees to minimize residual error via
  gradient descent → one best set of trees, one prediction.
- **BART:** puts a **Bayesian prior that penalizes deep trees**, forcing every
  tree to be a *weak, shallow* learner (often 1–2 splits). Then it uses **MCMC**
  to *sample* thousands of tree-ensemble configurations consistent with the data
  — not the single "best" one.

**The payoff — a posterior, not a point.** Ask BART to predict $Y$ for user $i$
and it returns not one number but **~1,000 draws** (one per MCMC sample). Those
draws *are* a posterior distribution. Take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles →
a 95% **credible interval**, with no bootstrap, no honest-splitting, no extra
work. That's the whole appeal.

---

## 2. Hill (2011) — BART as the ultimate S-learner

Jennifer Hill's insight: BART is the best possible **S-learner**
([§10.4 §1](./10.4-meta-learners.md#1-s-learner--one-model)) for CATE.

1. Fit one BART on the full data: predict $Y$ from $(X, D)$, with $D$ as a
   feature. Its flexibility captures complex $X$–$D$ interactions automatically.
2. **Counterfactuals:** force $D{=}1$ and draw 1,000 predictions; force $D{=}0$
   and draw 1,000. Subtract the arrays.
3. You now have a **1,000-draw posterior of the causal lift $\tau_i$** for that
   exact user — point estimate *and* credible interval in one shot.

For years this was the go-to flexible CATE estimator, precisely because the
uncertainty came free.

---

## 3. The fatal flaw — regularization-induced confounding

BART is an S-learner, and it **inherits the S-learner's regularization bias**
([§10.4 §1](./10.4-meta-learners.md#1-s-learner--one-model)) — in fact it makes
it *worse*. The Bayesian prior is *explicitly designed to shrink toward zero*.
So if the treatment effect is small relative to the variance $X$ drives in $Y$,
the prior concludes *"$D$ looks like noise — shrink it out,"* and the estimated
$\tau$ collapses to ~0.

This is the same shrinkage verified in [§10.4](./10.4-meta-learners.md#1-s-learner--one-model):
a plain S-learner with a strong baseline and a true effect of **0.50** returned
a mean CATE of **0.024** — the treatment feature was annihilated. BART's prior
adds a *second*, deliberate shrinkage force on top of that. Under **targeted
selection** — where the same $X$ that drives the outcome also drives who gets
treated — this shrinkage leaks confounding into $\tau$, a phenomenon Hahn et al.
named **regularization-induced confounding (RIC)**: the regularization you added
to control variance ends up *biasing the causal parameter.*

---

## 4. The fix — Bayesian Causal Forest (Hahn, Murray & Carvalho 2020)

BCF stops asking one BART to do everything. It **structurally separates** the
model into two dedicated forests:

```math
Y_i = \underbrace{\mu\big(X_i,\ \hat\pi(X_i)\big)}_{\text{baseline forest}} + \underbrace{\tau(X_i)}_{\text{treatment forest}} D_i + \epsilon_i
```

Three moves, each essential:

1. **A baseline forest $\mu$** dedicated to predicting untreated behavior.
2. **A separate treatment forest $\tau$** dedicated to the causal lift. Because
   $\tau$ is its *own* component with its *own* prior, the baseline prior can no
   longer shrink the treatment effect to zero — the §3 flaw is structurally
   removed.
3. **Feed the propensity score $\hat\pi(X)$ into the baseline $\mu$.** This is
   the genius. Under targeted selection, the propensity encodes *which* users
   self-select; by giving $\mu$ the propensity as an input, the baseline forest
   **absorbs all the selection-driven confounding**, leaving the $\tau$ forest
   clean and isolated. RIC is defeated because the confounding now has a
   dedicated home in $\mu$ rather than leaking into $\tau$.

BCF became the gold standard in causal econometrics for observational CATE —
flexible, honest about uncertainty (§5), and robust to targeted selection.

---

## 5. Bayesian vs. frequentist — same estimand, different philosophy

Both a frequentist causal forest (§10.1) and BCF target the **identical**
estimand, $\tau(x)$. The *business* question is the same. The *statistics*
differ:

- **Frequentist:** there is *one true $\tau$*; find the single best point
  estimate.
- **Bayesian:** $\tau$ is a *distribution*; draw the whole curve of what's
  plausible given the data and prior.

That philosophical gap buys BCF three things frequentist ML fights for:

- **Free, native uncertainty.** The posterior *is* the answer, so you instantly
  know whether the model is confident (tight curve at +\$50) or guessing (flat
  curve from −\$100 to +\$200). For high-stakes calls, *the probability of
  losing money* often matters more than the mean — and BCF hands it to you
  directly.
- **Regularization via priors.** The prior declares *"I assume $\tau = 0$ and
  shallow trees; convince me with overwhelming evidence"* — so it doesn't chase
  a lucky 3-person subgroup and report "+\$5,000 for left-handed Ohioans." It
  shrinks noise back toward zero by construction. (This is the *same* force that
  causes the §3 flaw — a prior that helps against overfitting hurts against a
  small true effect, which is exactly why BCF isolates $\tau$ in its own forest.)
- **Small-sample dominance.** With sparse or unbalanced data (200 enterprise
  clients), the prior stabilizes the estimate and lets the data take over as $n$
  grows — where XGBoost/deep nets need millions of rows to find signal.

## 6. Worked probe: the MLOps drawback of BCF

**Setup.** An e-commerce A/B test on a new homepage. You want per-user CATE to
see which demographics loved it. Choose between **Athey's frequentist causal
forest** and **Hahn's BCF** — both state-of-the-art. **From an MLOps
perspective, why might BCF break a pipeline that must score 50M live users in
real time?**

**Answer: inference latency and compute cost.**

- **Frequentist causal forest:** once trained, it's a *static collection of
  IF/THEN rules.* You compile it to optimized C++ — or even a giant SQL `CASE
  WHEN` — and scoring a user is a handful of comparisons: **~2 ms.**
- **BCF:** prediction *is* MCMC. To score one user you evaluate their features
  against *thousands of posterior tree-ensembles* and summarize the resulting
  distribution. Real-time, per user, at 50M users, that's a latency spike that
  **times out the page load.** The very thing that makes BCF valuable — a full
  posterior per user — is what makes it too slow for the serving path.

**The industry compromise:** **BCF offline, frequentist online.** Use BCF for
*batch* decisions where latency doesn't matter (who gets tomorrow's email, with
its risk bounds), and a compiled frequentist causal forest for *real-time*
inference (homepage layout, dynamic pricing). The staff-level answer isn't
"pick one" — it's "different tools for the offline and online paths."

> **The selection rule.** *Frequentist* when you have massive data, need
> millisecond inference, and just need to rank-order users. *BCF* when the
> sample is smaller, the business risk is high, and leadership needs the
> probability bounds on the worst case before shipping.

# Python: pymc-bart (PyMC), or the `bartpy` package.  R: `bcf`, `dbarts`, `BART`.

# ---- Standard BART as an S-learner (Hill 2011), sketch with pymc-bart ----
import pymc as pm, pymc_bart as pmb, numpy as np
with pm.Model() as bart_s:
    X_D = np.column_stack([X, D])                 # treatment as a feature
    mu = pmb.BART("mu", X_D, Y, m=200)            # 200 shallow trees
    sigma = pm.HalfNormal("sigma", 1)
    pm.Normal("y", mu, sigma, observed=Y)
    idata = pm.sample()                           # MCMC -> posterior draws
# CATE posterior: predict with D=1 vs D=0, subtract the DRAW ARRAYS (not the means)
#   tau_draws[i] = mu_posterior(X_i, D=1) - mu_posterior(X_i, D=0)   # ~1000 draws
#   point = tau_draws.mean(); ci = np.percentile(tau_draws, [2.5, 97.5])  # FREE interval

## 8. Common misreadings

**"BART is Bayesian, so it avoids the S-learner's regularization bias."**
The opposite — it's an S-learner *with an extra shrinkage prior*, so it's *more*
prone to collapsing a small $\tau$ to zero. That's why BCF exists. §3.

**"BCF is just BART with two forests."**
The second forest matters, but the *propensity-in-the-baseline* is the real
innovation — it absorbs targeted-selection confounding so $\tau$ stays clean.
§4.

**"Bayesian gives better point estimates than frequentist."**
Same estimand, comparable points. What Bayesian gives is **native uncertainty**
and **prior-based regularization**, not a better mean. §5.

**"Use BCF everywhere — it's the gold standard."**
Not in the real-time serving path. MCMC per user times out at scale; use a
compiled frequentist forest online, BCF offline. §6.

**"The credible interval is free, so BCF is cheaper."**
Free at *inference-of-uncertainty*, expensive at *compute* — MCMC training and
posterior scoring are heavy. The interval is free; the FLOPs are not. §1, §6.

**"Take the mean prediction under D=1 and D=0, then subtract."**
Subtract the **draw arrays**, not the means — you need the posterior of the
*difference* to get the CATE's credible interval. Subtracting means throws the
uncertainty away. §7.
