# 10.3 — Policy Learning: From Scores to Rules

> **Part of:** [Lecture 10 — Heterogeneous Effects & Uplift](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§10.1](./10.1-causal-forests.md) (per-user CATE) and [§10.2](./10.2-uplift-and-targeting.md) (targeting).

A causal forest gives you a precise $\hat\tau_i$ for every user — but a list of
10,000 user IDs is not something an Ops team can act on. They need a
**rule**: *"if the borrower is over 30 and hasn't paid in 15 days, route to the
elite agent."* This chapter turns the black-box score into a deployable policy.

---

## 1. Why the forest itself can't be the policy

A causal forest averages *thousands* of deep trees. It's highly accurate but a
**black box**: it tells you User A has $\hat\tau = \$85$, but not *why*, and not
in a form you can hardcode. Two problems for deployment:

- **Interpretability.** PMs and Ops can't consume "user 8,412,905 → 0.73." They
  need human-legible logic they can reason about, audit, and defend.
- **Serving cost.** Putting a 2,000-tree forest in the real-time path means
  feature-serving latency, model-drift monitoring, and infra you may not want.

You want the forest's *intelligence* distilled into a *simple, static rule.*

---

## 2. Approach 1 — a single honest causal tree

Instead of a forest, constrain the algorithm to **one shallow honest causal
tree** (max depth 3).

- **Mechanics:** it searches for the single most treatment-effect-splitting cut,
  then the next, building ≤ 8 leaves. It might split on `Tenure < 1yr`, then
  `Balance > $5000`.
- **Output:** interpretable subgroups with honest effects — *"borrowers with
  tenure < 1yr and balance > \$5,000 have a proven causal lift of +\$85."*
  Honesty (§10.1 §3) keeps each leaf's CI valid.
- **Drawback:** a single tree has **high variance.** It's readable, but its
  $\hat\tau$ estimates are far noisier than a full forest's — you traded
  accuracy for interpretability in one step.

---

## 3. Approach 2 — the surrogate policy tree (the standard)

The gold standard gets **both** the forest's accuracy *and* a shallow tree's
interpretability, in a two-step pipeline:

**Step 1 — the black box (accuracy).** Train a full causal forest. Score your
whole dataset to get an accurate $\hat\tau_i$ for every user.

**Step 2 — the surrogate (interpretability).** *Throw away $D$ and $Y$.* Train a
plain, shallow **`DecisionTreeRegressor(max_depth=3)`** with:

- **features** = the original covariates $X$,
- **target** = the forest's predicted lift $\hat\tau_i$.

You are forcing a simple tree to *learn the rules of the complex forest.* It
automatically finds the feature splits in $X$ that best separate high-$\hat\tau$
users from low-$\hat\tau$ users — a human-readable approximation of the forest's
targeting logic.

*Verified* (code in §5): on the §10.2 telecom data, where the true persuadables
are the moderate-loyalty segment, a depth-2 surrogate tree recovers exactly:

```
  loyalty X0 <= -1.00  -> lift ~0.00   (lost causes)
  -1.00 < X0 <= 1.00   -> lift  0.50   (PERSUADABLES)
  X0 > 1.00            -> lift ~0.01   (sure things)
```

The surrogate discovered the persuadable rule (`|loyalty| <= 1 → target`) on its
own, from the forest's scores alone — no treatment or outcome column needed in
Step 2.

---

## 4. Deployment — the policy handoff

The surrogate's high-lift leaves become a **treatment policy.** Say it reveals
the highest lift lives in `credit_score < 650 AND app_logins_last_week > 3`. You
hand Engineering the boolean logic, and they hardcode a rule:

```python
if user.credit_score < 650 and user.app_logins_last_week > 3:
    route_to_elite_agent(user)
else:
    route_to_standard_queue(user)
```

**The payoff:** no ML model in the serving path. No feature-serving latency, no
inference-time drift monitoring, no model infra. You ship a lightweight IF/ELSE
that an offline causal forest *proved* captures the highest-ROI segment. The
heavy causal machinery runs offline, in analysis; production runs a rule.

**Restrict the splitting features to actionable ones** (§10.1 §4): if the
surrogate is allowed to split on `p99_query_latency_ms`, the resulting "policy"
isn't one Ops can operate. Control the forest on the full $X$, but let the
surrogate split only on features the business can act on.

> **Caveat — re-validate the policy, don't trust the surrogate's numbers.** The
> surrogate approximates the forest and inherits its assumptions plus its own
> approximation error. Before rollout, estimate the policy's value the honest
> way: on a fresh randomized (or held-out) set, compare outcomes for users the
> rule *would* treat vs. not — ideally, A/B test the policy itself. The
> surrogate finds the rule; the experiment confirms it.

For the fully principled version — directly optimizing the policy for value
rather than distilling scores — see **Athey & Wager (2021)** policy trees and
the `policytree` package, which solve for the depth-$k$ rule that maximizes
expected outcome.

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.tree import DecisionTreeRegressor, export_text

# tau_hat: per-user CATE from the causal forest (section 10.1)
# X: covariates -- restrict to ACTIONABLE features for a deployable policy
surrogate = DecisionTreeRegressor(max_depth=3, min_samples_leaf=500)
surrogate.fit(X_actionable, tau_hat)          # target is the forest's lift, NOT Y

print(export_text(surrogate, feature_names=actionable_names))
# The high-value leaves ARE your policy. Read them off as IF/ELSE rules:
#   -1 < loyalty <= 1  -> lift 0.50  -> TARGET

# Then VALIDATE the policy on fresh randomized data before shipping:
treat = surrogate.predict(X_test) > COST      # the rule's treat/skip decision
policy_value = (Y_test[(D_test==1) & treat].mean()
                - Y_test[(D_test==0) & treat].mean())   # realized lift among targeted
# Production: econml.policy.PolicyTree, or the R `policytree` package.
```

## 6. Common misreadings

**"Deploy the causal forest to production."**
It's a black box with serving cost. Distill it into a surrogate policy tree and
ship an IF/ELSE. §1, §3.

**"A single shallow causal tree is good enough."**
It's interpretable but high-variance — noisy effect estimates. The
forest-then-surrogate pipeline keeps the forest's accuracy. §2 vs §3.

**"Train the surrogate on Y."**
No — the surrogate's target is the forest's **$\hat\tau$**, not the outcome.
Training on $Y$ rebuilds a churn model and re-creates the sure-things trap
(§10.2). §3.

**"The surrogate tree's leaf lifts are the truth."**
They're an approximation of an approximation. Re-validate the policy on fresh
randomized data — ideally A/B test the rule — before trusting its ROI. §4.

**"Let the surrogate split on any feature for the best fit."**
Then the policy may hinge on non-actionable telemetry Ops can't operate on.
Restrict splitting to actionable features. §4.
