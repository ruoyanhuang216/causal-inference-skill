# 2.6 — Ramp-Up, Epoch-Conditioning & Bandits

> **Part of:** [Lecture 2 — Experimentation](./README.md)
> **Read when:** your experiment's traffic allocation changed mid-flight — which is almost always.

---

## 1. The setup nobody thinks is dangerous

Every launch ramps. You start at 1% to check for crashes, go to 5%, then
25%, then 50%. This is responsible engineering, and it is also, under one
extra condition, a mechanism for producing **wildly** biased estimates
from a perfectly randomized experiment.

The extra condition is a **time-based confounder**: users who arrive at
different times are *different kinds of users*, and that difference
predicts the outcome.

This is not exotic. It's the default:

- **Frequent users arrive early.** Your daily-actives hit the app within
  hours of a release; occasional users show up over the following weeks.
  So early-arriving traffic is systematically heavier-using and
  higher-value.
- **Time zones.** A ramp that starts at 9 a.m. Pacific enrolls
  US-west-coast users first.
- **Weekday/weekend.** A Friday ramp enrolls different people than a
  Tuesday one.

Combine a time confounder with a **changing treatment:control ratio**,
and you get Simpson's paradox — with a sign that can be off by 400%.

---

## 2. The two kinds of ramp

This is the distinction that decides everything, and it is not the one
most people draw.

### 2.1 Partial-traffic ramp — SAFE ✅

You increase the fraction of traffic *enrolled in the experiment*, but
**within the enrolled slice, the split stays 50/50**.

```
  Day 1:   2% of traffic enrolled  →  1% T / 1% C     (50/50 within)
  Day 3:  10% of traffic enrolled  →  5% T / 5% C     (50/50 within)
  Day 7:  50% of traffic enrolled  → 25% T / 25% C    (50/50 within)
```

**This is safe.** Each epoch contributes users to T and C **in the same
proportion**, so the epoch composition of the treatment arm exactly
matches the control arm. Time confounding cancels in the difference.

### 2.2 Full-traffic ramp — BIASED ❌

100% of traffic is enrolled from day one, and you change the **T:C
weights**:

```
  Day 1:  100% enrolled  →  10% T / 90% C   (cautious launch)
  Day 5:  100% enrolled  →  50% T / 50% C   (confident now)
```

**This is broken.** The treatment arm is now composed disproportionately
of *late*-arriving users, while control is disproportionately *early*
arrivals. If arrival time predicts the outcome, the arms differ on a
confounder — and randomization did not protect you, because the
randomization *probability itself* changed with time.

### 2.3 The golden rule

> **Change the total enrolled traffic freely. Never change the
> treatment:control ratio.**

Ramp 1% → 5% → 25% → 100% of traffic, all day long, at any speed. Just
keep the split *within* the enrolled slice fixed. This costs nothing and
eliminates the entire problem.

## 3. Why it breaks: a worked Simpson's paradox

**Setup.** Two epochs. Heavy users arrive in epoch 1, light users in
epoch 2. **The true treatment effect is +1.0 in both epochs** — perfectly
homogeneous, no heterogeneity anywhere.

| Epoch | Users | Arrivals | $E[Y(0)]$ | True $\tau$ | T:C weights |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 100,000 | heavy users | 10 | **+1.0** | **90 / 10** |
| 2 | 100,000 | light users | 2 | **+1.0** | **50 / 50** |

**Naive pooled estimate:**

$$\bar{Y}_T = \frac{90{,}000 \times 11 + 50{,}000 \times 3}{140{,}000} = \frac{1{,}140{,}000}{140{,}000} = 8.143$$

$$\bar{Y}_C = \frac{10{,}000 \times 10 + 50{,}000 \times 2}{60{,}000} = \frac{200{,}000}{60{,}000} = 3.333$$

$$\hat{\tau}_{\text{naive}} = 8.143 - 3.333 = \mathbf{4.810}$$

**The truth is +1.0. The naive estimate is +4.81 — a 381% overstatement,
from a correctly randomized experiment with zero effect heterogeneity.**

The mechanism is pure composition. The treatment arm is 64% heavy users
($90{,}000/140{,}000$); the control arm is only 17% heavy users
($10{,}000/60{,}000$). Heavy users score ~10, light users ~2. **Almost
the entire "effect" is the arms being made of different people.** Nothing
about the assignment was non-random *within* an epoch — but pooling
across epochs with different weights is not a randomized comparison.

Note it works in reverse, too: had the ramp gone 90/10 → 50/50 with
heavy users arriving *late*, you'd have measured a large negative effect
and killed a good feature.

### 3.1 The fix: epoch-conditioning

Randomization is valid **within** each epoch. So estimate within-epoch
and recombine with the weights you actually want:

$$\boxed{\;\hat{\theta}_{\text{adj}} = \sum_j W_j\left(\bar{y}_{1,j} - \bar{y}_{0,j}\right), \qquad W_j = \frac{N_j}{N_{\text{total}}}\;}$$

Applied:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{adj}} = 0.5 \times (11 - 10) + 0.5 \times (3 - 2) = \mathbf{1.000} \;\;✓$$

**Exactly right.** Each within-epoch difference is a clean randomized
contrast (the weights were constant *within* the epoch); the weights
$W_j$ then aggregate to the population you care about.

**$W_j$ is a choice, and you should make it deliberately.** Weighting by
*total traffic* in epoch $j$ gives the ATE over all traffic. Weighting by
*enrolled* users gives the effect over the enrolled — different, and
usually not what you want. This is a Lecture 1 §4 question wearing an
engineering hat: **which population does the decision concern?**

### 3.2 Verifying the safe case

Same confounder, partial-traffic ramp (2% → 10% enrolled, always 50/50
within):

$$\bar{Y}_T = \frac{1{,}000 \times 11 + 5{,}000 \times 3}{6{,}000} = 4.333, \qquad \bar{Y}_C = \frac{1{,}000 \times 10 + 5{,}000 \times 2}{6{,}000} = 3.333$$

$$\hat{\tau}_{\text{naive}} = 4.333 - 3.333 = \mathbf{1.000} \;\;✓$$

**Unbiased with no adjustment at all**, because both arms have identical
epoch composition (1,000 heavy / 5,000 light each). This is §2.3 in
arithmetic.

**One caveat:** naive pooling here weights epochs by *enrolled* counts
(1:5), not total traffic. With a homogeneous effect that doesn't matter.
With heterogeneous effects it does — you'd get an enrollment-weighted
average, not the ATE. **If effects vary by epoch, epoch-condition even
when the ramp was safe.** It costs nothing.

## 4. Diagnosing it in the wild

You inherited an experiment. Was it ramped safely?

1. **Plot the T:C ratio by day.** If it's flat, you're fine. If it
   moves, you have work to do. This is a one-line query and it should be
   the first thing you run on any experiment you didn't design.
2. **Test for a time confounder.** Regress a *pre-treatment* covariate
   (tenure, historical activity) on arrival date. Significant → the
   confounder is real and the ratio drift matters.
3. **Compare naive vs. epoch-conditioned.** If they differ materially,
   report the adjusted one and say why.
4. **Check the arms' composition.** Mean pre-period covariates by arm,
   by epoch. Under a broken ramp, the pooled means diverge while each
   epoch's match — that pattern *is* the diagnosis.

**A balance table on pooled data will flag this**, and this is one of the
few cases where a failing balance table is a real finding rather than
a bad draw (§2.1 §3.4). The covariates genuinely differ across arms,
because assignment probability was time-varying. The fix isn't to
re-randomize — it's to condition on the epoch.

## 5. Bandits: an automated ramp that never stops

### 5.1 The lock-in

A multi-armed bandit — Thompson sampling, UCB — **continuously adjusts
the assignment ratio based on observed performance.** By §2.2, that is a
full-traffic ramp with weights changing every few minutes. Under a time
confounder, the whole of §3 applies, continuously.

Worse, the bandit doesn't just mis-*estimate*. It mis-*routes*.

Suppose heavy users arrive in the morning and arm A suits them; light
users arrive in the evening and arm B suits them. The bandit:

1. Morning: heavy users arrive. A performs well. Bandit shifts traffic to A.
2. Afternoon: allocation is now 80/20 toward A.
3. Evening: light users arrive, and would prefer B — but only 20% of
   them ever see B, so B's evidence accumulates slowly.
4. The bandit has **locked in** on the arm that suited the early
   population.

**A bandit can converge on the globally worse arm and lose to a static
50/50 A/B test.** The exploration schedule got captured by a
time-varying population, and the bandit has no concept that "best arm"
might be a function of *when*.

This is the counterintuitive result worth carrying: **the adaptive
algorithm, whose entire purpose is to find the better arm, can be
strictly worse at that than the dumb fixed split** — precisely because
adaptivity plus a time confounder is a bias-generating machine.

### 5.2 Two separate fixes

The failure has two faces, and they need different repairs:

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| **Inference** — the estimate is biased | Integrate over the continuous assignment-time. Epoch-condition on fine time slices; or use **inverse propensity weighting with the *known, logged* assignment probability** — the bandit recorded $\pi_t(a)$ at every step, so you have exact propensities. Adaptively-weighted estimators (Hadad et al. 2021) do this properly. |
| **Routing** — the bandit picked the wrong arm | Make the bandit aware of time. A **contextual bandit** with time-of-day / day-of-week in the context, or a **restless bandit** that models non-stationary arm values. |

**Log the propensities.** A bandit that doesn't record $\pi_t(a)$ at
assignment time is unanalyzable after the fact — you cannot reconstruct
the weights, and no amount of cleverness recovers them. This is a
one-line engineering requirement that determines whether the experiment
can ever be evaluated, and it is routinely skipped.

### 5.3 When to use a bandit at all

Bandits optimize **cumulative reward**. Experiments optimize
**knowledge**. These are different objectives and the choice follows
from which you need:

| Use a bandit | Use a fixed A/B |
|---|---|
| Short-lived decision (headline, promo) | You need a reliable effect estimate |
| Many arms, cheap to evaluate | You'll make a durable decision from it |
| Regret during the test is the real cost | You need clean inference for a launch memo |
| Stationary environment | **There's a time confounder** |

**"Bandits are strictly better because they don't waste traffic on the
losing arm" is wrong.** They trade inferential clarity for regret
reduction. If you need to *know* the effect — to write the launch memo,
to feed a downstream forecast, to defend it to finance — the fixed split
is the right tool, and its "wasted" traffic is what buys the answer.

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

# ---- 1. THE diagnostic: did the T:C ratio move? Run this first. ----
def ratio_by_day(df):
    r = df.groupby(df["assigned_at"].dt.date)["D"].mean()
    print(r)
    if r.max() - r.min() > 0.02:
        print(f"WARNING: T:C ratio moved {r.min():.2f} -> {r.max():.2f}")
        print("  -> full-traffic ramp. Naive pooling is BIASED. Epoch-condition.")
    return r

# ---- 2. Epoch-conditioned estimator ----
def epoch_conditioned(df, epoch_col="epoch", weight="total_traffic"):
    """theta_adj = sum_j W_j * (ybar_1j - ybar_0j)

    Randomization is valid WITHIN an epoch (weights were constant there).
    W_j chooses the population you're estimating for — make it deliberately.
    """
    out = []
    for ep, g in df.groupby(epoch_col):
        t, c = g.loc[g.D == 1, "Y"], g.loc[g.D == 0, "Y"]
        if len(t) == 0 or len(c) == 0:
            continue
        out.append({
            "epoch": ep,
            "diff": t.mean() - c.mean(),
            "var": t.var(ddof=1) / len(t) + c.var(ddof=1) / len(c),
            "N": g[weight].iloc[0] if weight in g else len(g),
        })
    e = pd.DataFrame(out)
    W = e["N"] / e["N"].sum()
    theta = (W * e["diff"]).sum()
    se = np.sqrt((W**2 * e["var"]).sum())     # epochs independent
    return theta, se, e

# ---- 3. The Simpson's paradox, reproduced ----
def simulate(weights, y0s, tau=1.0, n=100_000, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    rows = []
    for j, (w_t, y0) in enumerate(zip(weights, y0s)):
        D = (rng.random(n) < w_t).astype(int)
        rows.append(pd.DataFrame({
            "epoch": j, "D": D,
            "Y": y0 + tau * D + rng.normal(0, 1, n),
            "total_traffic": n,
        }))
    return pd.concat(rows, ignore_index=True)

# FULL-traffic ramp: weights change 90/10 -> 50/50. BIASED.
bad = simulate(weights=[0.9, 0.5], y0s=[10, 2])
naive = bad.loc[bad.D == 1, "Y"].mean() - bad.loc[bad.D == 0, "Y"].mean()
theta, se, _ = epoch_conditioned(bad)
print(f"full-traffic ramp: naive={naive:.3f}  adjusted={theta:.3f} (true=1.0)")
# full-traffic ramp: naive=4.817  adjusted=1.004 (true=1.0)

# PARTIAL-traffic ramp: enrollment grows, split stays 50/50. SAFE.
good = simulate(weights=[0.5, 0.5], y0s=[10, 2], n=100_000)
naive = good.loc[good.D == 1, "Y"].mean() - good.loc[good.D == 0, "Y"].mean()
print(f"partial-traffic ramp: naive={naive:.3f} (true=1.0) -- safe, no adjustment")
# partial-traffic ramp: naive=0.994 (true=1.0) -- safe, no adjustment

# These are Monte-Carlo draws, so they land near — not exactly on — the
# analytic values in §3 (4.810 / 1.000 / 1.000). The §3 arithmetic is exact;
# this simulation adds sampling noise on top of it.

# ---- 4. Bandit: IPW with the LOGGED propensities ----
# Your bandit KNOWS pi_t(a) at assignment time. Log it or the experiment
# is unanalyzable forever after.
def ipw_bandit(df):
    """df needs: D, Y, pi (propensity AT ASSIGNMENT TIME, logged live)."""
    assert "pi" in df, "no logged propensities -> not analyzable, full stop"
    w1 = df.D / df.pi
    w0 = (1 - df.D) / (1 - df.pi)
    return (w1 * df.Y).sum() / w1.sum() - (w0 * df.Y).sum() / w0.sum()
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"We ramped carefully from 10% to 50%, so we were conservative."**
If that was the *T:C ratio* on full traffic, you were biased, not
conservative. §2.2. If it was the *enrolled fraction* at a fixed split,
you were fine. §2.1. **These sound identical in a standup and differ by
381%.**

**"Randomization was valid at every moment, so the pooled estimate is fine."**
Valid *within* each epoch. Pooling across epochs with different
assignment probabilities is not a randomized comparison. §3.

**"There's no effect heterogeneity, so composition can't matter."**
The §3 example has a perfectly homogeneous +1.0 effect and the naive
estimate is +4.81. The bias comes from **baseline** differences across
epochs, not from effect heterogeneity. This is the part that surprises
people.

**"Bandits are strictly better — they don't waste traffic on losers."**
Bandits minimize regret; experiments produce knowledge. Under a time
confounder a bandit can lock onto the wrong arm and lose to a static
50/50. §5.1.

**"We'll reconstruct the propensities later."**
You can't. Log $\pi_t(a)$ live or the data is permanently unanalyzable.
§5.2.

**"The balance table is off, so let's re-randomize."**
If the ramp was full-traffic, imbalance is the *expected symptom* of
time-varying assignment probability. The fix is epoch-conditioning, not
re-randomizing. §4.
