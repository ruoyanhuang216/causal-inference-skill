# 2.4 — Geo Experiments & iROAS

> **Part of:** [Lecture 2 — Experimentation](./README.md)
> **Use when:** you cannot randomize users because the treatment is delivered to *places* — or because the marketplace pools supply across users.

---

## 1. Why geo

Two independent reasons, often conflated:

**1. You physically cannot randomize users.** A TV spot, a billboard, a
radio ad, a regional price change. There is no user-level assignment to
make. The treatment arrives at a *place*.

**2. User-level identity is broken.** Multi-device users, cookie churn,
ITP/ATT restrictions, offline conversions, and long lags between ad
exposure and purchase. You *can* randomize cookies, but the cookie is
not the person, so your control group contains treated humans. That's
SUTVA failure by measurement error.

**3. Supply is pooled.** In a marketplace, treating some users consumes
couriers/drivers/inventory that control users needed (README §1). The
market, not the user, is the isolated unit.

The geo unit of choice in the US is the **DMA** (Nielsen Designated
Market Area, ~210 of them). DMAs are constructed from *media market
reach and commuting patterns*, which is exactly the right property: they
approximate the boundary across which ad exposure does not leak. A zip
code is a worse unit not because it's smaller but because ads and
commuters cross it constantly.

## 2. iROAS is a Wald ratio

The headline metric:

$$\text{iROAS} = \frac{\text{incremental revenue}}{\text{incremental spend}}$$

Write it out over geos:

$$\widehat{\text{iROAS}} = \frac{\sum_{g \in T}\Delta Y_g - \sum_{g \in C}\Delta Y_g}{\sum_{g \in T}\Delta S_g - \sum_{g \in C}\Delta S_g} = \frac{\widehat{\text{ITT}}_Y}{\widehat{\text{ITT}}_S}$$

**Look at that denominator.** This is the Wald estimator from Lecture 1
§4, exactly:

| Lecture 1 | Geo experiment |
|---|---|
| $Z$ — randomized assignment | Geo assigned to treatment |
| $D$ — treatment received | **Ad spend delivered** |
| $Y$ — outcome | Revenue |
| LATE = $\text{ITT}_Y / \text{ITT}_D$ | **iROAS = $\text{ITT}_Y / \text{ITT}_S$** |

**iROAS is a LATE.** The geo assignment instruments for ad spend. This
isn't an analogy — it's the same estimator, and it inherits every caveat
from Lecture 1:

- **Exclusion restriction.** Geo assignment must affect revenue *only*
  through ad spend. Violated if the campaign launch coincides with
  regional PR, a sales push, or retailer stocking decisions in the
  treated markets. Untestable, and the bias is amplified by
  $1/\text{ITT}_S$.
- **Monotonicity.** Assignment doesn't *reduce* spend anywhere. Usually
  fine by construction.
- **It is LOCAL.** This is the big one, and §2.1 is entirely about it.

Recognizing iROAS as a Wald ratio also tells you how to get standard
errors right: **2SLS**, not the delta method by hand. The denominator is
estimated, and pretending otherwise understates uncertainty — the same
error flagged in Lecture 1 §7.

### 2.1 iROAS is a derivative, not an average

The single most expensive misreading in marketing measurement:

> "iROAS is 3.2, so every dollar we spend returns \$3.20. Let's 10× the
> budget."

**No.** The ad response curve is **concave**:

```
  Revenue
     ▲
     │                        ╭────────────  saturation
     │                   ╭────╯
     │              ╭────╯   ← iROAS at 10x spend is much lower
     │         ╭────╯
     │     ╭───╯  ← your test measured the slope HERE
     │   ╭─╯         (between control spend and treated spend)
     │ ╭─╯
     │╭╯
     └──────────────────────────────────────▶  Ad spend
      ↑         ↑
   control   treated
    spend     spend
```

Your experiment perturbed spend from $S_C$ to $S_T$ and measured the
**average slope over that interval**. That is a local derivative:

$$\text{iROAS} \approx \frac{dY}{dS}\bigg|_{S \in [S_C,\, S_T]}$$

Extrapolating it to a 10× budget assumes the curve is a straight line
through the origin. It is not — that's what "diminishing returns" means.
**This is exactly Lecture 1's LATE-vs-ATE error**, in different
clothing: you measured an effect local to the compliers (here, the
spend range you actually tested) and multiplied it by a population
(here, a budget) it doesn't describe.

**The fix is design, not analysis.** If you need the *curve*, randomize
geos to **multiple spend levels** (0%, 50%, 100%, 150% of current) and
fit the response curve. One treated/control contrast gives you one
point's slope, and no amount of post-hoc modeling recovers curvature
you didn't randomize.

### 2.2 Why not just use attribution?

Last-click / MTA answers *"which touchpoint preceded the conversion?"*
That is a **correlational** question. It over-credits ads shown to
people who were going to buy anyway — retargeting is the extreme case,
where you pay to show ads to users already holding the item in their
cart, then take credit for their purchase.

iROAS answers *"how much revenue would not have existed?"* Attribution
sums to 100% of conversions by construction; incrementality routinely
finds that a large share of "attributed" revenue is not incremental.
**The gap between attributed ROAS and iROAS is the number that matters,
and it is usually large.**

## 3. Design

### 3.1 The three phases

```
  ├──── PRETEST (4–8 wk) ────┼──── TEST (3–5 wk) ────┼── COOLDOWN (1–2 wk) ──┤
   Learn the control→treated       Run the campaign      Catch lagged
   mapping. Check parallel                               conversions and
   trends. Fit weights.                                  post-campaign decay.
```

- **Pretest** is where identification is earned. You are learning the
  historical relationship between treated and control geos. Too short
  and you fit noise; too long and old data misrepresents the current
  market. 4–8 weeks is the working range.
- **Test** must exceed the **conversion lag**. If purchases follow
  exposure by 2 weeks, a 3-week test measures one week of steady state.
- **Cooldown** is not optional for considered purchases. Ads have
  memory; ending measurement at campaign end truncates the effect and
  biases iROAS **down**.

### 3.2 The power problem: N = 210, and really N ≈ 13

**Geo experiments are not user-count problems.** You may have 120M
users, but you have ~210 randomization units. §2.3's rule applies with
full force: power comes from the **number of clusters**.

Worse, DMAs are savagely skewed. A Zipf-like rank-size fit to US DMAs:

```
DMAs = 210   mean = 571,429   median = 132,553   max/min = 468x   CV = 3.94
top 10 DMAs = 60.2% of the population
```

If you weight geos by population (which you must, for a revenue
estimate), the **Kish effective sample size** is:

$$N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\left(\sum_g w_g\right)^2}{\sum_g w_g^2} = \frac{n}{1 + \text{CV}^2} = \frac{210}{1 + 3.94^2} = \mathbf{12.7}$$

**A population-weighted geo experiment across all 210 DMAs has the
effective sample size of about 13 independent units.** New York alone
can decide your result. This is why geo experiments feel so noisy, and
why every serious geo methodology is fundamentally an attack on this
number.

**Mitigations, in order of value:**

1. **Matched pairs.** Pair geos on pretest outcome trajectories, then
   randomize *within pair*. Removes between-pair variance — the
   dominant term. This is the highest-leverage design choice available.
2. **Stratify by size.** At minimum, block on population deciles so the
   giants can't all land in one arm.
3. **Trimmed Match** (Google). Paired design + trim the most extreme
   pairs before averaging. Bounded-influence, so one anomalous metro
   can't dominate. Purpose-built for exactly the CV problem above.
4. **Pull the outliers out.** New York isn't a cluster; it's a market
   (§2.3 §6.3). Give it an N=1 synthetic-control read.
5. **Synthetic control** rather than a raw control average — build a
   weighted donor blend per treated geo (Lecture 5).

### 3.3 Bleed — the geo version of SUTVA

Ads and people cross borders. A commuter lives in the control DMA and
works in the treated one. **Bleed contaminates control toward treatment
→ attenuation.** Same direction as network spillover (§2.3), opposite
to marketplace cannibalization.

| Mitigation | What it does | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **DMAs as units** | Built from media reach + commuting; the borders are *designed* to be low-leak | Only ~210 of them |
| **Buffer "donuts"** | Drop geos in a ring between arms; measure only in the sealed interior | Loses users, and the dropped ring is disproportionately urban |
| **Graph-partitioned allocation** | Assign *adjacent* geos to the same arm → fewer shared borders | Reduces effective randomization; must model the induced correlation |

**Graph-partitioned allocation deserves a caution.** Assigning
neighboring geos to the same arm minimizes border length, but it also
makes assignment *spatially correlated* — you're no longer running
independent geo randomization, and naive SEs will be wrong. Handle it
with randomization inference over the actual (restricted) assignment
distribution, not by pretending it was simple random assignment.

### 3.4 Virtual DMAs

For borderless digital platforms (social, gaming), geography isn't the
interference boundary — the **network** is. Construct "virtual DMAs" by
partitioning the interaction graph:

- **Signals:** social graph, co-play, session overlap, temporal cohorts.
- **Algorithm:** Louvain / **Leiden** (§2.3 §2.3 — prefer Leiden;
  Louvain can emit disconnected "communities").
- **Objective:** minimize cross-cluster information flow — i.e. cut
  ratio on the **interference** graph (§2.3 §2.1).

**Validate before you spend, cheapest first:**

1. **Modularity / edge-density ratio.** Structural sanity check.
2. **SIR contagion simulation.** Simulate spread on the real graph under
   your partition. What fraction escapes its cluster? This is a direct
   estimate of residual bias, *before* you commit budget.
3. **Geo A/A test.** Full pipeline, sham arms. Measured lift must hug
   zero **and** the variance estimator must be calibrated. With ~13
   effective units, this is where you discover your CIs are fiction.

## 4. Estimation

Three families, increasing sophistication:

| Method | Idea | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **TBR** (time-based regression) | Regress treated-geo outcome on control-geo outcome using pretest; project the counterfactual | Simple, transparent, well-behaved |
| **Synthetic control / CausalImpact** | Weighted donor blend; BSTS forecasts the counterfactual with a prior | Few treated geos, strong pretest fit |
| **Trimmed Match** | Paired design, trim extreme pairs, bounded influence | Skewed geos (i.e. always) |

**CausalImpact** (Brodersen et al. 2015) fits a Bayesian structural time
series on the pretest, forecasts the treated geo's counterfactual, and
integrates the gap. Its credible intervals are honest *only if* the
pretest fit is genuinely good — check the pretest residuals and run
placebo-in-time tests. A BSTS that fits the pretest by overfitting
seasonal terms will produce a confident, wrong counterfactual.

**GeoLift** (Meta) wraps synthetic control with power simulation and
market selection. Its real value is the **pre-test power analysis**:
given your geos and history, it simulates what lift you could detect
*before* you spend. Given §3.2, that's the step that saves budgets.

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.api as sm
from linearmodels.iv import IV2SLS

# panel: geo, date, revenue, spend, treat (geo-level), post (time)

# ---- iROAS as a Wald ratio: geo assignment INSTRUMENTS for spend ----
# Do NOT hand-roll revenue_lift / spend_lift and call it done — the
# denominator is estimated, and 2SLS gets the SE right for free.
# (Lecture 1 §7 makes the same point about LATE.)
panel["z"] = panel["treat"] * panel["post"]

first = sm.OLS(panel["spend"], sm.add_constant(panel[["z"]])).fit(
    cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": panel["geo"]})
print(f"first stage (incremental spend): {first.params['z']:,.0f}  F={first.tvalues['z']**2:.1f}")

iroas = IV2SLS(
    dependent=panel["revenue"],
    exog=sm.add_constant(panel[["z"]])[["const"]],
    endog=panel["spend"],
    instruments=panel["z"],
).fit(cov_type="clustered", clusters=panel["geo"])
print(f"iROAS = {iroas.params['spend']:.2f}  95% CI {iroas.conf_int().loc['spend'].values}")

# ---- Kish effective N: the number that explains why geo tests are noisy ----
w = panel.groupby("geo")["population"].first().values
n_eff = w.sum() ** 2 / (w**2).sum()
print(f"{len(w)} geos -> Kish N_eff = {n_eff:.1f} (CV={w.std()/w.mean():.2f})")
# 210 geos -> Kish N_eff = 12.7   <- THIS is your sample size

# ---- Matched pairs: kill the between-pair variance ----
def match_pairs(pretest):           # pretest: geo x date matrix of outcomes
    from scipy.spatial.distance import pdist, squareform
    X = pretest.div(pretest.mean(axis=1), axis=0)      # scale-free trajectories
    D = squareform(pdist(X.values, metric="correlation"))
    np.fill_diagonal(D, np.inf)
    pairs, used = [], set()
    for i in np.argsort(X.mean(axis=1).values)[::-1]:  # big geos first
        if i in used: continue
        cand = [j for j in np.argsort(D[i]) if j not in used and j != i]
        if cand:
            pairs.append((X.index[i], X.index[cand[0]])); used |= {i, cand[0]}
    return pairs

# ---- Trimmed Match: bounded influence, because CV=3.9 ----
def trimmed_match_iroas(pairs_df, trim=0.10):
    """pairs_df: one row per pair, cols d_revenue, d_spend (treated - control)."""
    ratios = pairs_df["d_revenue"] / pairs_df["d_spend"]
    lo, hi = ratios.quantile([trim, 1 - trim])
    keep = pairs_df[(ratios >= lo) & (ratios <= hi)]
    return keep["d_revenue"].sum() / keep["d_spend"].sum()

# ---- Randomization inference: with N_eff~13, do not trust the t-stat ----
def ri_pvalue(panel, observed, n_perm=10_000, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    geos = panel["geo"].unique()
    null = []
    for _ in range(n_perm):
        sham = dict(zip(geos, rng.permutation(panel.groupby("geo")["treat"].first())))
        p = panel.assign(t=panel["geo"].map(sham))
        null.append(estimate_iroas(p))          # your estimator, re-run
    return (np.abs(null) >= abs(observed)).mean()
```

## 6. Common misreadings

**"iROAS is 3.2, so 10× the budget returns 3.2× on every new dollar."**
iROAS is the **slope over the spend range you tested**, not the average
return of all spend. The curve is concave. This is Lecture 1's
LATE-vs-ATE error with a media plan attached. Want the curve? Randomize
multiple spend levels. §2.1.

**"We have 120M users, the geo test is well-powered."**
You have **210 units**, and population-weighted, an effective **13**.
§3.2.

**"Attributed ROAS is 4.1, so the channel is profitable."**
Attribution is correlational and sums to 100% by construction.
Retargeting attributes revenue from people already buying. iROAS is the
counterfactual question. §2.2.

**"We used DMAs, so bleed is handled."**
DMAs are *low*-leak, not *no*-leak. Commuters exist. Add buffer donuts
if the borders are dense, and remember the donut you dropped was mostly
urban — you changed the estimand.

**"Graph-partitioned allocation solved our bleed."**
It also made assignment spatially correlated. Your SEs are now wrong
unless you do randomization inference over the *restricted* assignment
distribution you actually used. §3.3.

**"CausalImpact gave a tight credible interval, so we're confident."**
Those intervals are conditional on the BSTS being right. Check pretest
residuals and run placebo-in-time tests before believing them. §4.

**"The campaign ended, so measurement ends."**
Ad effects have memory. Truncating at campaign end biases iROAS
**down**. Budget a cooldown. §3.1.
