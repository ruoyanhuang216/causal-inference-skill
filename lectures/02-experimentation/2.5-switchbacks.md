# 2.5 — Switchback Experiments

> **Part of:** [Lecture 2 — Experimentation](./README.md)
> **Use when:** shared-resource (bipartite) interference makes user-level randomization meaningless, and you can't afford geo-level power.

---

## 1. The problem switchbacks solve

You want to test a new courier-dispatch algorithm in Austin. User-level
randomization is hopeless: **there is one courier pool.** If treated
orders grab couriers faster, control orders wait longer. The measured
effect is inflated by exactly the amount you stole from control:

$$\hat{\tau}_{\text{naive}} = \underbrace{\tau}_{\text{real}} + \underbrace{(\text{what treatment took from control})}_{\text{pure artifact}}$$

**This is the dangerous direction** (README §1). A dispatch change that
merely *reallocates* couriers — zero total value — reads as a large
win. Ship it, and city-wide metrics don't move. That post-mortem is the
most common one in marketplace DS.

Geo randomization (§2.4) would fix it: treat Austin, control Dallas. But
now $N = 2$, cities differ, and you need months. **The switchback keeps
the market whole and randomizes *time* instead.** Austin runs algorithm
A from 12:00–14:00, B from 14:00–16:00, and so on. The unit is the
**(market, time-block)**.

You've traded a spatial interference problem for a **temporal** one:
blocks are adjacent in time, and the system remembers.

---

## 2. Carryover — the whole difficulty

At 14:00 you switch A → B. But the courier pool at 14:00 — where
couriers are, what they're committed to, which orders are mid-flight —
was **produced by 45 minutes of A's decisions.** B's early performance
is measured on a system A built.

Formally, SUTVA now fails across *time*: the outcome in block $k$
depends on the assignment of block $k-1$.

$$Y_k = f(D_k, \underbrace{D_{k-1}, D_{k-2}, \ldots}_{\text{carryover}})$$

The fix is a **washout**: discard outcomes from the start of each block,
long enough for the system to forget.

### 2.1 How long must the washout be?

The washout must exceed the **system's memory** — how long a decision
made under the old algorithm continues to constrain the system. For food
delivery, a courier dispatched under A is committed for roughly the
delivery duration, so:

$$w \;\gtrsim\; \text{delivery time} \;\approx\; 45\text{ min}$$

This is a **lower bound**, not a target. It's the time for A's *last*
dispatch to complete; full convergence to B's steady state can take
longer if the algorithms differ in how they position idle couriers.
Measure it rather than assume it (§4.1).

### 2.2 The constraint that follows

$$\boxed{\;T > w\;}$$

**Your block must be longer than your washout.** Otherwise you drop
100% of the data — and worse, you never reach steady state at all,
because you switch again before the system has flushed. This is the
crux of §5.

---

## 3. Attribution: which block does an order belong to?

Before any washout math, settle this — it's the question the PM's
objection really turns on.

**The treatment is the *dispatch decision*, and it happens once, at
order placement.** So:

> **An order belongs to the block in which it was placed.** Its outcome
> is attributed there, however long the delivery runs.

An order placed at 13:59 under A and delivered at 14:44 is an **A
order**. It does not "span two algorithms." No splitting, no
proportional allocation, no weighting by minutes-in-block. Those are all
answers to a question that isn't being asked.

### 3.1 The exception that changes everything

**Unless your system re-optimizes mid-delivery.** If the dispatcher
reassigns couriers to in-flight orders, then an order placed at 13:59
genuinely *is* handled by A and then B, and the PM's objection is
literally correct.

**Ask the engineer this before designing anything:**

> *"Once an order is dispatched, does any later decision touch it? Batching, re-routing, reassignment?"*

| Answer | Implication |
|---|---|
| **One-shot dispatch** | Order → placement block. Carryover is *system state* only. Washout ≈ delivery time. |
| **Continuous re-optimization** | Order is genuinely multi-treated. Placement-block attribution is **wrong**. You need a longer washout *and* to drop orders whose *delivery* crosses a boundary — or to reframe the unit as the market-minute. |

This distinction is invisible in the data and decides whether your
design is valid. It is the single highest-value question in the room,
and it's an engineering question, not a statistical one.

---

## 4. Choosing the window: derive it, don't assert it

Here is the tension. Let:

- $T$ — block length (minutes)
- $w$ — washout (minutes)
- $r$ — order rate (orders/minute)
- $D$ — total experiment duration (minutes)
- $\sigma_b^2$ — **between-block** variance of the block mean
- $\sigma_w^2$ — **within-block** per-order variance

Blocks: $K = D/T$. Clean orders per block: $n_k = r(T - w)$.

With blocks randomized 50/50, the difference in means across blocks has:

$$\text{Var}(\hat\tau) \approx \frac{4}{K}\left(\sigma_b^2 + \frac{\sigma_w^2}{n_k}\right) = \frac{4T}{D}\left(\sigma_b^2 + \frac{\sigma_w^2}{r(T-w)}\right)$$

**Both directions hurt.** Longer $T$ → fewer blocks $K$ (first factor
grows), but more clean orders per block (second term shrinks). There's
an interior optimum. Setting $\partial\text{Var}/\partial T = 0$:

$$r\sigma_b^2(T-w)^2 + \sigma_w^2(T-w) - T\sigma_w^2 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; r\sigma_b^2 (T-w)^2 = w\sigma_w^2$$

$$\boxed{\;T^{*} = w + \sqrt{\dfrac{w\,\sigma_w^2}{r\,\sigma_b^2}}\;}$$

*(Verified against a numerical optimizer — exact match.)*

### 4.1 What it says

With $w = 45$ min, $r = 5$ orders/min, $\sigma_w = 12$ min:

| $\sigma_b$ | $T^*$ | Data kept | Blocks/day | Var at $T{=}120$ vs. optimum |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0.5 min | 117 min | 61% | 12.3 | 1.00× |
| 1.0 min | 81 min | 44% | 17.8 | 1.14× |
| 2.0 min | 63 min | 29% | 22.9 | 1.49× |
| **3.0 min** | **57 min** | **21%** | **25.3** | **1.73×** |
| 5.0 min | 52 min | 14% | 27.6 | 2.01× |

Three things fall out, and they're all counterintuitive:

**1. The optimum is usually ~1 hour, not 2.** A 2-hour window is optimal
only if block-to-block variation is *tiny* ($\sigma_b \approx 0.5$ min).
Real markets have rush hours, weather, and events; $\sigma_b$ of 2–3
minutes in mean delivery time is realistic, and there **2 hours costs
you 73% more variance than the optimum.** If you're going to name a
number in a design review, derive it from your own $\sigma_b$.

**2. The optimal design throws away most of your data.** At
$\sigma_b = 3$, you discard **79%** of orders to washout — and that's
*optimal*. Because power comes from the **number of blocks**, not the
number of orders. Which is the third lesson, and the one that ties this
whole lecture together:

**3. It's the same lesson as §2.3 and §2.4.**

| Chapter | Power comes from | Not from |
|---|---|---|
| §2.3 Clusters | number of **clusters** ($N_{\text{eff}} \to K/\rho$) | number of users |
| §2.4 Geo | number of **geos** (Kish $N_{\text{eff}} \approx 13$) | number of users |
| §2.5 Switchback | number of **blocks** | number of orders |

**$N$ is the number of randomization units. Everything else is
decoration.** Once you believe that, "discard 79% of the data to double
the block count" stops being shocking and becomes obvious.

**Estimate $\sigma_b$ and $\sigma_w$ from historical data before
designing.** They're a `groupby` on last month's orders (§6).

### 4.2 Block on time-of-day

Time-of-day is the dominant driver of $\sigma_b^2$ — a lunch block and a
3 a.m. block are not exchangeable. Randomizing blocks independently
balances time-of-day *in expectation* while paying its full variance.

**Better: stratify.** Pair each time-of-day slot across days and
randomize within slot (slot 12:00–14:00 gets A on some days, B on
others). This removes time-of-day from $\sigma_b^2$ entirely.

Note the feedback into §4: **blocking shrinks $\sigma_b$, which
*raises* $T^*$.** At $\sigma_b = 3 \to 1$, $T^*$ goes 57 → 81 min. If
your blocks are near-exchangeable, there's little value in having many
of them, so buy cleaner ones instead. Design the stratification first,
then solve for the window.

## 5. Worked example: the DoorDash PM

**Setup.** Switchback in Austin, alternating the dispatch algorithm
every **30 minutes**. Average delivery: **45 minutes**.

**PM:** *"30 minutes is too short! Our average delivery takes 45 minutes.
Almost every order will overlap two different algorithms. The data will
be completely contaminated."*

### 5.1 Is the PM right?

**Right conclusion. Wrong mechanism. And the mechanism is what fixes
it.**

**Where the PM is wrong.** An order does not "overlap two algorithms."
Assuming one-shot dispatch (§3.1 — *verify this*), the algorithm makes
its decision once, at placement. An order placed at 13:59 is dispatched
by A and remains an A order through delivery at 14:44. **Attribution:
the block in which it was placed.** So the specific question — *"how do
you handle an order placed at minute 25 and delivered at minute 70,
spanning three blocks?"* — has a boring answer:

> **It's a block-1 order. Full outcome, attributed to block 1, no
> splitting.** Randomization assigned block 1 to an algorithm; that
> algorithm made the decision; the outcome is the consequence. Blocks 2
> and 3 are irrelevant to it. Splitting the order across blocks would be
> attributing an outcome to a treatment that never touched it.

**Where the PM is right.** There *is* contamination, but it flows the
other way — not the order leaking into later blocks, but **earlier
blocks leaking into the order**. At 14:00 the courier pool's positions
and commitments are the residue of 45 minutes of A. An order placed at
14:05 under B is dispatched by B *into a world A built*. **That is
carryover, and it's about system state, not order duration.**

The distinction matters because it determines the fix. "Orders overlap
blocks" suggests splitting outcomes — which is wrong and would do
nothing. "The system remembers" gives you the washout, which is right.

### 5.2 Why 30 minutes actually fails

The washout must exceed the system's memory ≈ **45 min** (§2.1). So:

$$w = 45 > T = 30$$

**You'd drop every order.** Every order in a 30-minute block is placed
within 45 minutes of a switch, because *the whole block* is within 45
minutes of a switch. Worse, the system never reaches steady state — you
switch again before it has flushed, so blocks are permanently in
transient. There is no analysis that rescues a 30-minute window; there
is no clean data to analyze.

**The PM is right that 30 minutes is broken. They just diagnosed it as
an attribution problem when it's a washout-feasibility problem.**

### 5.3 What to actually propose

Don't assert a number — derive it. Estimate $\sigma_b$ and $\sigma_w$
from last month's Austin orders, then apply §4:

$$T^{*} = w + \sqrt{\frac{w\,\sigma_w^2}{r\,\sigma_b^2}}$$

At $w=45$, $r=5$, $\sigma_w=12$, $\sigma_b=3$: $T^{*} \approx 57$ min.

> *"You're right that 30 minutes is broken, though not for the reason
> you think — orders don't straddle algorithms, since dispatch is
> decided once at placement. The real problem is that our washout has to
> be at least as long as a delivery (~45 min), because that's how long
> A's couriers stay committed. A 30-minute block is shorter than its own
> washout, so we'd throw away 100% of the data and never reach steady
> state.*
>
> *From last month's Austin data, between-block sd is ~3 min and
> within-block sd is ~12 min at 5 orders/min. That puts the optimal
> block at **~60 minutes**: drop the first 45 minutes, keep the last 15,
> and we get **24 blocks/day**. Over 14 days that's ~336 blocks — enough
> for randomization inference.*
>
> *Yes, we discard 75% of orders. That's the right trade: power comes
> from the number of blocks, not the number of orders. And we'll
> stratify by time-of-day so lunch rush lands in both arms — that shrinks
> between-block variance, and if it drops to ~1 min I'll re-solve and
> come back with a longer window."*

Note what this does: it concedes the PM's conclusion immediately, corrects
the mechanism (which they'll need in order to reason about the next
design), and replaces a number-argument with a formula they can check.

### 5.4 If dispatch *is* continuously re-optimized

Then the PM's literal claim is true and §5.1 is wrong. An order placed
at 13:59 whose courier gets reassigned at 14:10 was genuinely handled by
both. Placement-block attribution breaks. Options:

- **Restrict to orders that complete within their block's clean window**
  — but that conditions on delivery duration, which is *post-treatment*
  and correlated with the outcome. **This is a collider** (README §3.4).
  Do not do it, however tempting.
- **Redefine the unit** as the market-minute and model carryover
  explicitly (Bojinov et al. 2023).
- **Lengthen $T$** so boundary-crossers are a small share — costs blocks.
- **Freeze in-flight orders** at their dispatching algorithm. An
  engineering fix that restores the clean design. Usually cheaper than
  the statistical workarounds, and nobody thinks to ask.

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

# ---- 1. Estimate the variance components from HISTORY, before designing ----
def variance_components(hist, block_min, outcome="delivery_min"):
    """hist: orders with placed_at (datetime) and the outcome."""
    b = hist.assign(block=hist["placed_at"].dt.floor(f"{block_min}min"))
    g = b.groupby("block")[outcome]
    return dict(
        sigma_b=g.mean().std(),                       # between-block sd
        sigma_w=np.sqrt(g.var().mean()),              # within-block sd
        rate=len(b) / ((b.placed_at.max() - b.placed_at.min()).total_seconds() / 60),
    )

# ---- 2. Measure the washout instead of guessing it ----
def washout_from_data(hist, outcome="delivery_min"):
    """How long does a dispatch decision constrain the system?
    p95 of delivery duration is a defensible floor for system memory."""
    return hist[outcome].quantile(0.95)

# ---- 3. Solve for the optimal window ----
def optimal_window(w, rate, sigma_w, sigma_b):
    """T* = w + sqrt(w * sigma_w^2 / (rate * sigma_b^2))"""
    return w + np.sqrt(w * sigma_w**2 / (rate * sigma_b**2))

def var_tau(T, w, rate, sigma_w, sigma_b, duration_min):
    if T <= w:
        return np.inf                     # washout >= window: no clean data
    K = duration_min / T
    n_k = rate * (T - w)
    return (4 / K) * (sigma_b**2 + sigma_w**2 / n_k)

vc = dict(sigma_b=3.0, sigma_w=12.0, rate=5.0)
w = 45.0
T_star = optimal_window(w, **vc)
print(f"optimal window = {T_star:.0f} min | keep {100*(T_star-w)/T_star:.0f}% "
      f"| {24*60/T_star:.0f} blocks/day")
print(f"vs T=120: variance x{var_tau(120,w,**vc,duration_min=14*24*60) / var_tau(T_star,w,**vc,duration_min=14*24*60):.2f}")
# optimal window = 57 min | keep 21% | 25 blocks/day
# vs T=120: variance x1.73

# ---- 4. Assign orders to blocks; apply washout ----
def prepare(orders, block_min, washout_min, assignment):
    """assignment: {block_start -> 0/1}. Orders belong to the block they
    were PLACED in — dispatch is decided once, at placement."""
    o = orders.assign(block=orders["placed_at"].dt.floor(f"{block_min}min"))
    o["minutes_into_block"] = (o["placed_at"] - o["block"]).dt.total_seconds() / 60
    o["D"] = o["block"].map(assignment)
    # Drop by PLACEMENT time (pre-treatment). Never by delivery duration —
    # that's post-treatment and a collider.
    return o[o["minutes_into_block"] >= washout_min]

# ---- 5. Stratify by time-of-day: removes the biggest chunk of sigma_b ----
def assign_stratified(blocks, seed=0):
    """Randomize WITHIN each time-of-day slot, across days."""
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    df = pd.DataFrame({"block": blocks})
    df["slot"] = df["block"].dt.hour * 60 + df["block"].dt.minute
    out = {}
    for _, grp in df.groupby("slot"):
        n = len(grp)
        d = np.array([0, 1] * (n // 2 + 1))[:n]
        out.update(dict(zip(grp["block"], rng.permutation(d))))
    return out

# ---- 6. Inference: block-level, and use randomization inference ----
# The unit is the BLOCK. Aggregate first, then compare. With ~300 blocks
# and a known design, RI beats asymptotics.
def ri_pvalue(block_means, assignment, n_perm=10_000, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    d = np.array([assignment[b] for b in block_means.index])
    y = block_means.values
    obs = y[d == 1].mean() - y[d == 0].mean()
    null = np.empty(n_perm)
    for i in range(n_perm):
        p = rng.permutation(d)
        null[i] = y[p == 1].mean() - y[p == 0].mean()
    return obs, (np.abs(null) >= abs(obs)).mean()
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"The order spans three blocks, so split its outcome across them."**
No. Dispatch is decided once, at placement. It's a placement-block
order, whole. §3.

**"Use a 30-minute window; deliveries are only 45 minutes."**
Washout (45) > window (30) → 100% data loss and permanent transient.
Window must exceed washout. §5.2.

**"Drop orders whose delivery crosses a block boundary."**
Delivery duration is **post-treatment** and correlates with the
outcome — that's a collider (README §3.4). Drop by *placement* time,
which is pre-treatment. §5.4.

**"Use 2-hour blocks, that's the standard."**
Optimal only at $\sigma_b \approx 0.5$ min. At realistic $\sigma_b$ it
costs 73% more variance than ~1 hour. Estimate your own variance
components. §4.1.

**"We're throwing away 75% of the data, that can't be right."**
It is right. Power comes from **blocks**, not orders. §4.1.

**"We have 500,000 orders, we're well-powered."**
You have ~300 blocks. That's your $N$. Same lesson as clusters (§2.3)
and geos (§2.4).

**"Switchbacks handle interference, so SUTVA is fine."**
They handle *cross-user* interference within a market. They introduce
*cross-time* interference. That's the whole chapter.
