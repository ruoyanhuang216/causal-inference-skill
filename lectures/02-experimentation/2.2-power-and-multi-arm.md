# 2.2 — Power Analysis & Multi-Arm Experiments

> **Part of:** [Lecture 2 — Experimentation](./README.md)

An unbiased estimate you can't resolve is not an answer. This chapter is
about the arithmetic that decides, before you launch, whether the
experiment can possibly settle the question — and about the two ways
adding arms quietly triples your runtime.

---

## 1. The four levers

Sample size per arm is determined by exactly four inputs. Everything
else is commentary.

| Lever | Symbol | Typical | What it is |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Significance** | $\alpha$ | 0.05 | P(Type I error) — shipping a nothing |
| **Power** | $1-\beta$ | 0.80 | P(detecting a real effect) — 1 − P(missing it) |
| **Minimum detectable effect** | $\delta$ (MDE) | business-set | Smallest effect worth detecting |
| **Variance** | $\sigma^2$ | data-set | Metric noise; $p(1-p)$ for a rate |

Only two of these are yours. **$\sigma^2$ is a fact about your metric**,
and **MDE is a business decision** — it is the effect below which you
would not act. That last point gets skipped constantly: the MDE is not
"the effect I expect," it is "the effect I would change my mind over."
If a 0.5% lift wouldn't change the roadmap, don't power for it.

---

## 2. The two-arm formula

For a difference in means with equal arms:

$$n \approx \frac{2\sigma^2\left(Z_{1-\alpha/2} + Z_{1-\beta}\right)^2}{\delta^2}$$

At the standard settings, $Z_{0.975} = 1.96$ and $Z_{0.80} = 0.8416$, so:

$$\left(1.96 + 0.8416\right)^2 = 7.849 \approx 8$$

giving the back-of-envelope every DS should have memorized:

$$\boxed{\;n \approx \frac{16\sigma^2}{\delta^2}\;}\quad\text{per arm, at }\alpha=0.05,\;\text{power}=0.8$$

For a proportion with baseline rate $p$ and **relative** lift $r$:

$$n \approx \frac{16\,p(1-p)}{(pr)^2} = \frac{16(1-p)}{p\,r^2}$$

*Example:* $p = 5\%$ conversion, want to detect a 2% relative lift
($r = 0.02$):

$$n \approx \frac{16 \times 0.95}{0.05 \times 0.0004} = 760{,}000 \text{ per arm}$$

This is why conversion-rate tests on low-baseline metrics are brutal.
Note $(1-p)/p$: **the lower the baseline, the worse it gets.**

### 2.1 The inverse-square law

$\delta$ is **squared** in the denominator. This single fact drives more
experimentation strategy than everything else in this chapter:

$$n \propto \frac{1}{\delta^2}$$

| PM asks for | Sample multiplier | 10-day test becomes |
|---|---|---|
| Detect 4% instead of 2% | **0.25×** | 2.5 days |
| Detect 2% (baseline) | 1× | 10 days |
| Detect 1% instead of 2% | **4×** | 40 days |
| Detect 0.5% instead of 2% | **16×** | 160 days |

**Halving the MDE quadruples the runtime.** When a PM says "can we also
detect small effects?", they are asking for a quarter of a year, and
they don't know it. Your job is to translate.

This also explains why **variance reduction is so valuable**: $n \propto
\sigma^2$ is *linear*. CUPED at $\rho = 0.7$ halves $\sigma^2$ (§2.1
[here](./2.1-rct-and-assumptions.md#64-cuped--the-variance-reduction-that-actually-matters)),
which halves $n$ — a free doubling of your sample. There's no
corresponding trick for $\delta$.

### 2.2 Solving for what you actually control

You rarely get to pick $n$; traffic is what it is. Invert instead.

**MDE given a fixed $n$:**

$$\delta = \left(Z_{1-\alpha/2} + Z_{1-\beta}\right)\sqrt{\frac{2\sigma^2}{n}} \;\propto\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$$

**Power given fixed $n$ and $\delta$:**

$$1 - \beta = \Phi\!\left(\frac{\delta}{\sigma}\sqrt{\frac{n}{2}} - Z_{1-\alpha/2}\right)$$

**Report MDE, not power.** "This test can detect a 2.4% lift" is a
sentence a PM can act on. "This test has 62% power" is not — it invites
the response "62% sounds fine," which is exactly wrong.

## 3. Multi-arm: the multiplicity problem

Test 4 new UIs against 1 control. You now make 4 comparisons, each at
$\alpha = 0.05$. The chance of **at least one** false positive — the
**family-wise error rate** — is no longer 5%.

### 3.1 The formula everyone quotes, and why it's wrong here

The standard line:

$$\text{FWER} = 1 - (1-\alpha)^k$$

For $k=3$: $1 - 0.95^3 = 14.3\%$. For $k=4$: $18.6\%$.

**This formula assumes the comparisons are independent. Yours aren't.**
Every treatment is compared against the *same control arm*, so the test
statistics share a common term and correlate at $\rho = 0.5$ (for equal
arm sizes). The true FWER is lower:

| $k$ | Independent formula | **True FWER (shared control)** | Overstated by |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 9.8% | **9.1%** | 7.5% |
| 3 | 14.3% | **12.5%** | 13.8% |
| 4 | 18.6% | **15.6%** | 19.1% |

*(Computed by simulating $\max_j |Z_j|$ under $H_0$ with the induced
$\rho = 0.5$ correlation — see §6.)*

The direction is unchanged and the problem is real: **15.6% is still
three times your nominal 5%.** But quoting 18.6% is quoting the wrong
model, and in a design review someone will know. The reason the shared
control helps: if the control arm happens to draw low, *all* treatments
look good simultaneously — the errors come in bunches rather than
independently, which makes "at least one" less likely than independence
implies.

### 3.2 Bonferroni

Divide $\alpha$ by the number of comparisons:

$$\alpha_{\text{adj}} = \frac{\alpha}{k}$$

For $k=4$: $\alpha_{\text{adj}} = 0.0125$, so $Z_{1-\alpha_{\text{adj}}/2}
= 2.498$ (up from 1.96). Guarantees $\text{FWER} \le \alpha$ **regardless
of correlation**, which is why it's the default: it needs no assumptions
about the dependence structure.

### 3.3 Dunnett — and why Bonferroni is fine anyway

Bonferroni is conservative *because* it ignores the $\rho = 0.5$
correlation. **Dunnett's test** is built for exactly this design (many
treatments, one shared control) and uses the exact joint distribution:

| Method | Critical value | Achieved FWER | $n$/arm (MDE 2%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unadjusted | 1.960 | **15.6%** ❌ | 100,000 |
| **Bonferroni** | 2.498 | 4.3% | **142,073** |
| **Dunnett** | 2.441 | 5.0% ✓ | **137,282** |

**Dunnett saves 3.4%.** That's it.

This is worth internalizing because the folklore says "Bonferroni is way
too conservative." In the many-vs-one design it barely is — the
correlation is only 0.5, so there's little slack to reclaim. Bonferroni
costs you 3% more sample than the exact method and takes ten seconds to
explain. **Use Bonferroni.** Save the sophistication for a problem where
it buys more than three percent.

(Bonferroni *is* badly conservative when you have many highly-correlated
tests — 50 metrics that all measure engagement. Different problem. There,
reach for **Holm** (uniformly more powerful than Bonferroni, same
assumptions, no reason not to) or control the **FDR** via
Benjamini-Hochberg if you're screening rather than confirming.)

### 3.4 The FWER/FDR choice is about what the family *is*

Before correcting, answer: **what decision does this family support?**

- **Ship exactly one of 4 UIs.** One decision, 4 comparisons → control
  FWER. A false positive means shipping a dud.
- **Screen 200 metrics for anything interesting.** Not a decision, a
  hunt → control **FDR**. Bonferroni at $k=200$ would need $\alpha =
  0.00025$ and detect nothing.
- **1 primary metric, 12 guardrails.** These are *different families*.
  Don't correct the primary metric for the guardrails — the primary
  decision was pre-specified. Guardrails get their own (usually looser)
  treatment, because there you *want* sensitivity to harm.

**Corollary: pre-registering a single primary metric is a power
optimization, not just hygiene.** It shrinks $k$ to 1.

## 4. Worked example: the VP wants four arms

**Setup.** A 28.2% conversion baseline, where a 2% relative MDE needs
**100,000 per arm** at $\alpha=0.05$, power 0.8, in a standard A/B.
Traffic: **20,000 users/day**. Current plan: 1 treatment, 10 days.

The VP: *"The design agency gave us four concepts. Test all four. It's
the same experiment — should still be 10 days, right?"*

**No. It's 35 days.** Two multiplicative penalties compound:

### Penalty 1 — Z-score inflation

Bonferroni with $k=4$ → $\alpha_{\text{adj}} = 0.0125$ → $Z = 2.498$:

$$\frac{n_{k=4}}{n_{k=1}} = \frac{(2.498 + 0.8416)^2}{(1.960 + 0.8416)^2} = \frac{11.151}{7.849} = 1.421$$

$$100{,}000 \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbf{142{,}073} \text{ per arm}$$

### Penalty 2 — traffic dilution

You need 142,073 per arm across **5 arms** (4 treatments + 1 control):

$$142{,}073 \times 5 = \mathbf{710{,}363} \text{ total}$$

$$\frac{710{,}363}{20{,}000} = \mathbf{35.5 \text{ days}}$$

**10 days → 35.5 days.** The 3.5× is 1.42 (multiplicity) × 2.5
(arms). Penalty 2 is the bigger one and the one people forget,
because it isn't statistical — it's arithmetic. Every arm you add
divides your traffic *and* raises the bar each arm must clear.

### The concession

VP: *"You have 10 days. Ship it."*

Then $n$ is locked at $200{,}000 / 5 = \mathbf{40{,}000}$ per arm, and
$\alpha$ is locked by Bonferroni. The math forces a concession — pick
one:

**Option A — concede MDE.** With 40,000/arm:

$$\delta_{\min} = 2.0\% \times \sqrt{\frac{142{,}073}{40{,}000}} = \mathbf{3.77\%}$$

> *"At 10 days we can only detect a 3.8% lift. If the agency's designs
> deliver 3% — a genuinely good outcome — the test returns flat and we
> kill a winner."*

**Option B — concede power.** Still hunting the 2% lift:

$$1-\beta = \Phi\!\left(\sqrt{40{,}000}\times 0.00886 - 2.498\right) = \Phi(-0.726) = \mathbf{23.4\%}$$

> *"Power collapses from 80% to 23%. If the effect is real, we miss it
> **three times out of four.** We'd be paying for a 10-day test to
> generate a coin flip that lands wrong most of the time."*

**Option C — the answer that actually gets you the win.** Cut the arms.

> *"Pick your best two concepts. With $k=2$: 121,000/arm × 3 arms =
> 363,000 → **18 days**. Or pick one: 10 days, as originally scoped. The
> agency's four concepts aren't four hypotheses, they're four
> renderings of maybe two ideas. Which two do you actually believe?"*

**This is the staff-level move.** A and B are correct and get you
labeled the person who says no. C reframes the constraint as a
prioritization question and hands it back to the person who owns
prioritization. The formula didn't tell you to do that — but it told you
that "four arms" costs 25 days, which is the fact that makes the
conversation possible.

## 5. Sequential testing: the multiplicity you didn't know you had

§3 is about multiplicity across **arms**. There's a second, sneakier
kind: multiplicity across **time**.

If you check the p-value daily for 14 days and stop when it drops below
0.05, your true Type I error is **not 5% — it's roughly 25–40%.** You
made 14 correlated comparisons and reported the best one. Every
dashboard with a live p-value is a false-positive generator, and unlike
the multi-arm case, nobody counted the comparisons.

Three legitimate fixes:

| Approach | Idea | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed horizon** | Compute $n$, don't look until you hit it | Default; simplest |
| **Group sequential** (O'Brien-Fleming, Pocock) | Pre-specify $K$ looks, spend $\alpha$ across them | You need interim safety reads |
| **Always-valid inference** (mSPRT, confidence sequences) | Anytime-valid $p$ / CI, look continuously | Dashboards, continuous monitoring |

**Always-valid inference is not free**: it costs roughly 20–40% more
sample than a fixed horizon for the same power. You're buying the right
to peek, and the price is real. The trade is usually worth it — the
alternative is a dashboard everyone peeks at anyway while pretending
they don't.

**One asymmetry worth knowing:** stopping early for *harm* is much less
problematic than stopping early for *success*. Guardrail-triggered
shutdowns are fine — you're not making a shipping claim, you're avoiding
damage, and the cost of a false alarm is a rerun.

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm
from scipy.optimize import brentq

# ---------- two-arm ----------
def n_per_arm(mde_rel, base_rate, power=0.8, alpha=0.05, k=1):
    """Sample size per arm. k = number of TREATMENT arms (Bonferroni)."""
    z_a = norm.ppf(1 - (alpha / k) / 2)
    z_b = norm.ppf(power)
    delta = base_rate * mde_rel
    var = base_rate * (1 - base_rate)
    return 2 * var * (z_a + z_b) ** 2 / delta**2

def mde(n, base_rate, power=0.8, alpha=0.05, k=1):
    z_a = norm.ppf(1 - (alpha / k) / 2)
    z_b = norm.ppf(power)
    var = base_rate * (1 - base_rate)
    return (z_a + z_b) * np.sqrt(2 * var / n) / base_rate

def power_of(n, mde_rel, base_rate, alpha=0.05, k=1):
    z_a = norm.ppf(1 - (alpha / k) / 2)
    var = base_rate * (1 - base_rate)
    delta = base_rate * mde_rel
    return norm.cdf(delta * np.sqrt(n / (2 * var)) - z_a)

# ---------- the VP scenario ----------
# 28.2% baseline conversion is what makes a 2% relative MDE cost exactly
# 100,000/arm in a standard A/B — the premise of §4.
BASE, DAILY = 0.282, 20_000
for k in [1, 2, 4]:
    n = n_per_arm(0.02, BASE, k=k)
    total = n * (k + 1)          # k treatments + 1 control
    print(f"k={k}: {n:8,.0f}/arm x {k+1} arms = {total:9,.0f} -> {total/DAILY:5.1f} days")

# k=1:  100,000/arm x 2 arms =   200,000 ->  10.0 days
# k=2:  121,101/arm x 3 arms =   363,302 ->  18.2 days
# k=4:  142,073/arm x 5 arms =   710,363 ->  35.5 days

print(f"\nforced to 40k/arm at k=4:")
print(f"  MDE inflates to {mde(40_000, BASE, k=4):.2%}")
print(f"  power at 2% MDE  {power_of(40_000, 0.02, BASE, k=4):.1%}")
#   MDE inflates to 3.77%
#   power at 2% MDE  23.4%

# ---------- true FWER with a SHARED control (rho = 0.5) ----------
# The 1-(1-a)^k formula assumes independence. Comparisons against a
# common control are correlated, so it OVERSTATES the FWER.
def fwer_shared_control(k, crit, n_sim=2_000_000, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    cov = np.full((k, k), 0.5)
    np.fill_diagonal(cov, 1.0)
    Z = rng.multivariate_normal(np.zeros(k), cov, size=n_sim)
    return (np.abs(Z).max(axis=1) > crit).mean()

for k in [2, 3, 4]:
    print(f"k={k}: independent={1-0.95**k:.4f}  true={fwer_shared_control(k, 1.96):.4f}")
# k=2: independent=0.0975  true=0.0907
# k=3: independent=0.1426  true=0.1254
# k=4: independent=0.1855  true=0.1558

# ---------- Dunnett vs Bonferroni: is the sophistication worth it? ----------
k = 4
c_dunnett = brentq(lambda c: fwer_shared_control(k, c) - 0.05, 1.5, 4.0)
c_bonf = norm.ppf(1 - 0.05 / (2 * k))
zb = norm.ppf(0.8)
saving = 1 - (c_dunnett + zb) ** 2 / (c_bonf + zb) ** 2
print(f"\nBonferroni crit {c_bonf:.3f} | Dunnett crit {c_dunnett:.3f}")
print(f"Dunnett saves {saving:.1%} of sample -> not worth the explanation")
# Bonferroni crit 2.498 | Dunnett crit 2.441
# Dunnett saves 3.4% of sample -> not worth the explanation
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"We got p = 0.06, so let's run it a few more days."**
That's sequential testing without the correction (§5). You've already
peeked; extending conditional on the result inflates Type I error badly.
Either pre-commit to a horizon or use always-valid inference.

**"Power is 62%, that's close enough to 80%."**
62% power means a **38% chance of missing a real effect.** Reframe as
MDE — "we can detect 2.6%, not 2.0%" — and let the business decide
whether that's acceptable. Numbers near a threshold sound fine; the
failure probability doesn't.

**"Adding a fifth arm is basically free, we have the traffic."**
It divides traffic by 6 instead of 5 (−17%) *and* raises every arm's
bar via $\alpha/5$. Both compound. §4.

**"MDE is our best guess at the effect size."**
MDE is the smallest effect you'd **act on**. If you'd ship at +1% but
power for 3%, you built a test that can't answer your question. If you'd
only ship at +5%, powering for 1% wastes a month.

**"FWER = 1 − (1−α)^k."**
Only under independence. With a shared control, $\rho = 0.5$ and the
true FWER is meaningfully lower (§3.1). Real problem, wrong magnitude.

**"Bonferroni is too conservative, we need something fancier."**
In many-vs-one, Dunnett buys **3.4%** (§3.3). Bonferroni is the right
default. Fancier methods pay off when tests are numerous and highly
correlated — a different design.

**"We powered the primary metric, so we're fine."**
If you also read 12 guardrails and 8 segments, you're making 21
comparisons. Pre-specify which family drives the decision.
