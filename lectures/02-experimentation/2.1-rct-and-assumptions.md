## 1. Why randomization works

Lecture 1 §3.2 gave the decomposition that defines the problem:

$$
\underbrace{E[Y \mid D=1] - E[Y \mid D=0]}_{\text{what you compute}}
= \underbrace{E[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid D=1]}_{\text{ATT}}
+ \underbrace{E[Y(0) \mid D=1] - E[Y(0) \mid D=0]}_{\text{selection bias}}
$$

Randomization kills the second term, and it's worth being precise about
*how*, because the mechanism is not "the groups are the same."

Random assignment makes $D$ **statistically independent of the potential
outcomes**:

$$\{Y_i(1), Y_i(0)\} \perp\!\!\!\perp D_i$$

This is a statement about the *joint distribution*, not about any
realized sample. It gives:

$$E[Y(0) \mid D=1] = E[Y(0) \mid D=0] = E[Y(0)]$$

so selection bias is exactly zero, and:

$$E[Y \mid D=1] - E[Y \mid D=0] = E[Y(1)] - E[Y(0)] = \text{ATE}$$

**Independence is the assumption we usually have to argue for.
Randomization is the one case where we manufacture it.** That is the
entire value proposition of an experiment, and it's why the estimate
needs no defense of unconfoundedness, no DAG, no sensitivity analysis
for omitted variables. You bought identification with the design.

### What randomization does *not* give you

The single most common conceptual error, and the one that generates bad
balance-table arguments:

> Randomization does **not** make your two groups identical. It makes
> them identical **in expectation**, and it makes the *distribution of
> the imbalance* known.

In any realized sample the arms differ on countless covariates. That's
not a failure — it's sampling. What randomization guarantees is that the
imbalance is *random*, so it enters your estimator as **variance, not
bias**, and your standard errors already price it in.

This distinction decides what you do when a balance table shows a
significant difference. See §3.

## 2. The assumption stack

An RCT needs far less than an observational study, but "less" is not
"none." Four things must hold, and only the first is bought by the coin
flip.

| # | Assumption | Bought by randomization? | How it fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Independence** — $\{Y(1),Y(0)\} \perp D$ | ✅ Yes, by design | Broken randomization (§3.3), SRM |
| 2 | **SUTVA** — no interference, no hidden treatment variants | ❌ **No** | Networks, marketplaces (§2.3–2.5) |
| 3 | **No differential attrition** | ❌ **No** | Treatment changes who you observe (§4) |
| 4 | **Full compliance** (only if you want ATE) | ❌ **No** | Non-compliance → ITT/LATE (Lecture 1) |

**Assumption 2 is the one that kills you**, and it deserves the emphasis
because it's invisible. A broken randomization announces itself via SRM.
Attrition announces itself in the data. Non-compliance shows up in the
logs. **Interference is silent** — the experiment runs cleanly, the
balance table is beautiful, the p-value is small, and the number is
meaningless because the estimand was never defined. Lecture 1 §2.3: it's
a definition error, not an estimation error.

The rest of this chapter covers assumptions 1, 3, and 4. Assumption 2 is
the subject of every other chapter in Lecture 2.

## 3. Balance tables — and how to read them correctly

### 3.1 What they're for

Compare pre-treatment covariate means across arms:

| Covariate | Control | Treatment | Diff | Std. diff | p |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tenure (days) | 412.3 | 409.8 | −2.5 | −0.011 | 0.42 |
| Prior 30d revenue | \$11.98 | \$12.04 | 0.06 | 0.008 | 0.61 |
| % mobile | 63.1% | 63.4% | 0.3pp | 0.006 | 0.55 |
| % enterprise | 8.2% | 8.9% | 0.7pp | 0.025 | **0.03** |

The purpose is **not** to prove the groups are the same — §1 explained
why they never are. It is to **detect a broken randomizer**. A balance
table is a debugging tool masquerading as a statistical test.

### 3.2 The mistake everyone makes

> "% enterprise is imbalanced at p = 0.03. The randomization failed. Let
> me re-randomize / control for it / throw out the experiment."

**No.** You tested ~12 covariates. Under a *correctly working*
randomizer, the probability that at least one shows p < 0.05 is:

$$1 - 0.95^{12} = 46\%$$

Finding one significant imbalance in twelve is not evidence of failure.
It is the *expected* outcome. This is the same multiplicity problem as
§2.2, applied to your sanity check, and treating each row as an
independent verdict is how teams talk themselves into discarding valid
experiments.

**Re-randomizing because you didn't like the draw is a serious error** —
it conditions the assignment on the realized covariates and destroys the
independence in §1. You are no longer running the experiment you think
you are, and your SEs are now wrong in a direction you cannot compute.
(There is a legitimate version of this — *rerandomization* as a
pre-specified design where you re-draw until a balance criterion is met
and then use the correct, restricted randomization distribution for
inference. That is a design decision made *before* launch, not a
reaction to a bad table.)

### 3.3 What to actually do

**Test the joint hypothesis, not 12 marginal ones.** Regress assignment
on all covariates and run an F-test / omnibus test:

$$D_i = \alpha + \boldsymbol{\beta}'\mathbf{X}_i + \varepsilon_i \quad\Rightarrow\quad H_0: \boldsymbol{\beta} = \mathbf{0}$$

If this rejects, the randomizer is broken and you have a real bug. If it
doesn't, you have a normal draw. **One omnibus test, one verdict.**

Then read the **standardized differences**, not the p-values. The
standardized difference

$$d = \frac{\bar{X}_T - \bar{X}_C}{\sqrt{(s_T^2 + s_C^2)/2}}$$

doesn't grow more damning as $n$ grows. At $n = 1$M, a trivially small
imbalance will have $p < 0.001$ and $d = 0.004$; the p-value is telling
you about your sample size, not your randomization. **Rule of thumb:
$|d| < 0.1$ is noise.** In the table above, every $|d| < 0.03$ —
including the "significant" one. The randomizer is fine.

### 3.4 When imbalance *is* a red flag

Not all imbalance is benign. Escalate when:

- **The omnibus test rejects.** Real bug.
- **SRM** (§README 3.2). Arms aren't the right size → assignment is
  non-random. Debug, don't analyze.
- **Imbalance on a variable that can't differ.** If "account created
  before experiment start" differs, that's logically impossible under
  valid assignment — you have a leak.
- **Imbalance correlated with the treatment mechanism.** If the
  treatment is a redirect and your treatment arm has fewer slow-network
  users, the randomizer is fine but the *implementation* is dropping
  users differentially. This is attrition, not imbalance (§4).

## 4. Attrition — the assumption that quietly destroys RCTs

### 4.1 Why it's worse than it looks

You randomized 50k/50k. You observe outcomes for 47k treated and 49k
control. You have lost independence — **not because assignment was
biased, but because observation was.**

Formally, you now estimate:

$$E[Y \mid D=1, R=1] - E[Y \mid D=0, R=1]$$

where $R_i$ is a response/retention indicator. Conditioning on $R$ is
conditioning on a **post-treatment variable** (README §3.4), so it opens
exactly the collider path that randomization was supposed to close. The
experiment is now observational among survivors, and the design bought
you nothing.

**Differential attrition is the killer**, not attrition per se. If 6% of
both arms churn for reasons unrelated to treatment, you're estimating
the effect on a smaller but still-randomized population. If the
*treatment causes* the churn, then the treated survivors are a selected
group.

And the selection is usually adverse in the worst way: **a treatment
that annoys marginal users drives them out, leaving a treated arm of
enthusiasts.** Your metric goes up. The feature is bad. This is not a
subtle bias — it's a sign flip, and it's the mechanism behind a large
share of "we shipped it and the metric didn't move in production" post-mortems.

### 4.2 Diagnostics

1. **Compare attrition rates by arm.** A difference here is a finding,
   not a nuisance — report it as an outcome.
2. **Compare *baseline covariates of attriters* by arm.** Equal rates
   can still hide differential composition: if control loses random
   users and treatment loses its low-engagement users, rates match and
   the bias is fully present. This check catches what rate comparison
   misses, and almost nobody runs it.
3. **Lee bounds.** Trim the arm with less attrition to match the other's
   retention rate, at both tails of the outcome distribution. Gives a
   *worst-case interval* under a monotonicity assumption (treatment
   doesn't cause anyone to stay who'd otherwise leave). If the Lee bounds
   still exclude zero, your result survives even adversarial attrition —
   which is a far stronger claim than "attrition looks balanced."
4. **Bound the extreme.** Impute the worst case for treated attriters
   and the best case for control attriters. If the sign holds, stop
   worrying.

### 4.3 The best fix is design

Measure outcomes for everyone randomized, not just the engaged.
**Server-side outcomes (revenue, retention) beat client-side outcomes
(clicks, surveys)** precisely because they don't attrit. If your outcome
requires the user to show up, you have built attrition into your
measurement.

## 5. Compliance

Covered in depth in [Lecture 1](../01-foundations-and-estimands.md) §4–6.
The one-paragraph version:

If take-up is partial, you have three quantities and they are not
interchangeable. **ITT** ($E[Y|Z{=}1]-E[Y|Z{=}0]$) needs nothing but
randomization and answers *"what does launching do."* **LATE**
(ITT ÷ compliance rate) needs exclusion and monotonicity and answers
*"what does using do."* **ATE** is **not identified** — never-takers
never reveal $Y(1)$.

Always report the take-up rate. An ITT of zero with 3% take-up is not a
failed feature; it's a failed *rollout*, and the fix is distribution,
not product. An ITT of zero with 90% take-up is a failed feature.
Reporting ITT without the take-up rate makes those two indistinguishable.

## 6. Estimation: use covariates, but use them correctly

### 6.1 Difference in means is already unbiased

$$\hat{\tau} = \bar{Y}_T - \bar{Y}_C$$

Under §1, this is unbiased for the ATE. Full stop. Everything below is
about **precision**, not bias — you are not fixing anything, you are
buying narrower confidence intervals.

### 6.2 Regression adjustment and the Freedman critique

The obvious upgrade:

$$Y_i = \alpha + \tau D_i + \boldsymbol{\beta}'\mathbf{X}_i + \varepsilon_i$$

Including pre-treatment covariates soaks up variance in $Y$, shrinking
$\text{SE}(\hat\tau)$. Covariates that predict the outcome help a lot;
covariates that don't, don't.

**But Freedman (2008) showed this can go wrong.** With treatment effect
heterogeneity, the additive-covariate OLS above is *biased* in finite
samples and can be **less** precise than the raw difference in means.
The mechanism: the model constrains treated and control to share a
single $\boldsymbol{\beta}$, and when the true covariate-outcome
relationship differs by arm, that misspecification bites.

### 6.3 Lin (2013): the fix

**Interact the treatment with the (demeaned) covariates:**

$$Y_i = \alpha + \tau D_i + \boldsymbol{\beta}'\tilde{\mathbf{X}}_i + \boldsymbol{\gamma}'(D_i \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{X}}_i) + \varepsilon_i, \qquad \tilde{\mathbf{X}}_i = \mathbf{X}_i - \bar{\mathbf{X}}$$

This is equivalent to fitting **separate regressions per arm** and
averaging the predictions. It:

- is **consistent** for the ATE regardless of heterogeneity,
- is **never less precise** asymptotically than the difference in means,
- requires **demeaning** — without it, $\tau$ is the effect at
  $\mathbf{X} = 0$, which is usually a meaningless point, not the ATE.

**The demeaning is the part people skip, and it silently changes the
estimand.** Use HC2 robust standard errors (Lin's asymptotics assume
them).

**Rule: never run additive-covariate OLS on an RCT. Either report the
difference in means, or run Lin's interacted estimator.** The middle
option is the one with no advantages.

### 6.4 CUPED — the variance reduction that actually matters

When you have **pre-experiment values of the outcome itself**, use them:

$$Y_i^{\text{CUPED}} = Y_i - \theta(X_i - \bar{X}), \qquad \theta = \frac{\text{Cov}(Y, X)}{\text{Var}(X)}$$

where $X_i$ is the same metric measured *before* the experiment. Then
run the difference in means on $Y^{\text{CUPED}}$.

Variance reduction is exactly:

$$\frac{\text{Var}(Y^{\text{CUPED}})}{\text{Var}(Y)} = 1 - \rho^2$$

where $\rho = \text{Corr}(Y, X)$. Pre-period revenue typically correlates
$\rho \approx 0.7$ with in-period revenue, giving $1 - 0.49 = 51\%$
variance reduction — **equivalent to doubling your sample size, for
free**. This is the highest-ROI line of code in most experimentation
platforms.

$\theta$ is estimated on the pooled data; because $X$ is pre-treatment,
it is independent of assignment, so this is unbiased. **The catch: it
only works for users with pre-period data.** New users have no $X$, and
they're often the population you most care about.

## 7. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.api as sm
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf
from scipy import stats

# df: D (assigned), Y (outcome), plus pre-treatment covariates

# ---- 0. SRM check. Run this FIRST. If it fails, stop. ----
obs = df["D"].value_counts().sort_index().values
chi2, p_srm = stats.chisquare(obs, f_exp=[obs.sum() / 2] * 2)
assert p_srm > 0.001, f"SRM! p={p_srm:.2e} — assignment is broken, do not analyze"

# ---- 1. Balance: ONE omnibus test, not one test per covariate ----
covs = ["tenure", "prior_rev", "is_mobile", "is_enterprise"]
bal = sm.OLS(df["D"], sm.add_constant(df[covs])).fit()
print(f"omnibus balance F-test p = {bal.f_pvalue:.3f}")   # >0.05 => randomizer OK

# Standardized differences — read these, not the marginal p-values.
for c in covs:
    t, ctl = df.loc[df.D == 1, c], df.loc[df.D == 0, c]
    d = (t.mean() - ctl.mean()) / np.sqrt((t.var() + ctl.var()) / 2)
    print(f"  {c:15s} std diff = {d:+.3f} {'<-- CHECK' if abs(d) > 0.1 else ''}")

# ---- 2. Attrition ----
if "observed" in df:
    rate = df.groupby("D")["observed"].mean()
    print(f"attrition: control {1-rate[0]:.1%}, treatment {1-rate[1]:.1%}")
    # Equal rates can still hide differential COMPOSITION. Check who leaves.
    print(df[df.observed == 0].groupby("D")[covs].mean())

# ---- 3a. Difference in means — unbiased, no assumptions beyond design ----
dim = sm.OLS(df["Y"], sm.add_constant(df[["D"]])).fit(cov_type="HC2")
print(f"diff-in-means: {dim.params['D']:.4f} (SE {dim.bse['D']:.4f})")

# ---- 3b. Lin (2013): interact D with DEMEANED covariates. ----
# Demeaning is not optional — without it the D coefficient is the effect
# at X=0, not the ATE.
for c in covs:
    df[f"{c}_c"] = df[c] - df[c].mean()
lin = smf.ols(
    "Y ~ D * (" + " + ".join(f"{c}_c" for c in covs) + ")", data=df
).fit(cov_type="HC2")
print(f"Lin ATE:       {lin.params['D']:.4f} (SE {lin.bse['D']:.4f})")

# NEVER this — additive covariates, no interaction (Freedman 2008):
# smf.ols("Y ~ D + tenure + prior_rev", data=df).fit()

# ---- 4. CUPED: use the pre-period outcome. Usually the biggest win. ----
theta = np.cov(df["Y"], df["Y_pre"])[0, 1] / np.var(df["Y_pre"])
df["Y_cuped"] = df["Y"] - theta * (df["Y_pre"] - df["Y_pre"].mean())
cuped = sm.OLS(df["Y_cuped"], sm.add_constant(df[["D"]])).fit(cov_type="HC2")
rho = np.corrcoef(df["Y"], df["Y_pre"])[0, 1]
print(f"CUPED ATE:     {cuped.params['D']:.4f} (SE {cuped.bse['D']:.4f})")
print(f"  rho={rho:.2f} -> variance x{1-rho**2:.2f}, "
      f"SE x{np.sqrt(1-rho**2):.2f} (like {1/(1-rho**2):.1f}x the sample)")
```

## 8. Common misreadings

**"The balance table has a significant covariate, so randomization failed."**
With 12 covariates you expect a 46% chance of at least one. Run the
omnibus test and read standardized differences. See §3.2.

**"Let me re-randomize until the table looks clean."**
This conditions assignment on realized covariates and destroys the
independence in §1. Pre-specified rerandomization is a real technique
with its own inference; reacting to an ugly table is not it.

**"Randomization makes the groups identical."**
In expectation only. Realized imbalance is variance, and your SEs
already account for it. This is why "controlling for" an imbalanced
covariate to "fix" the draw is unnecessary — and why adding covariates
for *precision* is still a good idea.

**"Adding covariates fixes bias."**
There is no bias to fix. Covariates buy precision. If they change your
point estimate materially, that's a signal something is wrong with the
design (or you ran additive OLS with heterogeneity — see Freedman).

**"Attrition is balanced at 6% vs 6%, so we're fine."**
Equal *rates* with different *composition* is fully biased. Compare the
covariates of attriters, not just the counts. §4.2.

**"We got a null, so the feature doesn't work."**
Check take-up first. ITT ≈ 0 with 3% take-up is a distribution failure,
not a product failure.

**"It's an RCT, so it's the gold standard."**
An RCT buys assumption 1 of four. SUTVA, attrition, and compliance are
all still yours to defend — and SUTVA fails silently.
