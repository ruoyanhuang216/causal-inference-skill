# 2.3 — Cluster Randomization

> **Part of:** [Lecture 2 — Experimentation](./README.md)
> **Use when:** direct network interference — treating A changes what B sees.

---

## 1. The problem

Your feature makes treated users post more. Their friends — including
friends in the **control** group — see those posts and engage more.
Control is contaminated. $Y_i(0)$ now depends on other units'
assignments, so the potential outcome isn't well-defined (Lecture 1
§2.3), and:

$$E[Y \mid D=1] - E[Y \mid D=0] \;<\; \text{ATE}$$

The bias is **attenuation**: control drifts toward treatment, shrinking
the measured gap. **A user-level A/B on a social feature systematically
under-reports the effect**, which means your null results are the
suspicious ones. That asymmetry is worth holding onto — it's the
opposite of the marketplace case (§2.4, §2.5), where interference
*inflates* the estimate.

**The fix:** make the randomization unit large enough to contain the
spillover. Randomize *clusters*, and interference becomes
*within*-cluster — where it's harmless, because both the source and the
recipient of the spillover share an assignment.

---

## 2. Defining clusters

### 2.1 The only rule that matters

> **Cluster on the graph along which the treatment interferes — not on
> the graph that is most convenient, most modular, or most available.**

Almost every real cluster-randomization failure is a violation of this
one sentence. Teams reach for the graph they have (messaging history,
follows, co-purchases) instead of the graph the *treatment* travels
along. A beautifully modular partition of the wrong graph provides
**zero** protection. See §6.

### 2.2 Natural / heuristic clusters

Use boundaries the product already has:

| Domain | Cluster |
|---|---|
| EdTech | Classroom, school |
| Gaming | Guild, server, lobby |
| B2B SaaS | Workspace, account, tenant |
| Geo | Zip, city, DMA (→ §2.4) |
| Professional | **Company** |

**Pro:** trivial to implement, interpretable, stable over time, and
survives a lawyer's questions.
**Con:** users cross boundaries. A Slack user in three workspaces breaks
the partition; a person with two "current companies" breaks §6's answer.
You need a tie-break rule, and it must be **pre-treatment**.

### 2.3 Algorithmic / graph-partitioning clusters

When no natural boundary exists, build the interaction graph — users as
nodes, interactions as weighted edges — and cut it.

**Algorithms:** Louvain / Leiden (modularity maximization), spectral
clustering, METIS (balanced $k$-way partition), normalized cuts.

**Prefer Leiden over Louvain.** Louvain has a documented defect: it can
produce **internally disconnected communities** — a "cluster" that isn't
even connected. For experimentation this is silently fatal, since your
randomization unit isn't a coherent neighborhood at all. Leiden
guarantees connected communities and is a drop-in replacement.

**The metric — cut ratio.** The fraction of edge weight crossing cluster
boundaries:

$$\text{cut ratio} = \frac{\sum_{(i,j) \in E:\; c(i) \neq c(j)} w_{ij}}{\sum_{(i,j) \in E} w_{ij}}$$

Target < 5%. **But cut ratio must be computed on the interference graph**
(§2.1), not on whatever graph you clustered. If those differ, your cut
ratio is measuring nothing.

**Modularity** ($Q$) is what Louvain/Leiden optimize:

$$Q = \frac{1}{2m}\sum_{ij}\left[w_{ij} - \frac{k_i k_j}{2m}\right]\delta(c_i, c_j)$$

$Q > 0.3$ suggests real community structure. **Modularity is a
clustering-quality metric, not an experiment-validity metric.** High $Q$
on the wrong graph is a well-partitioned irrelevance.

### 2.4 Validate before you spend

Ordered by cost, cheapest first:

1. **Cut ratio / modularity** on the *interference* graph. Fails here → stop.
2. **Contagion simulation.** Simulate an SIR-style spread on the real
   graph under your partition. What fraction of "infection" crosses
   clusters? Directly estimates residual bias before you spend a user.
3. **Geo/cluster A/A test.** Assign clusters to sham arms and run the
   full pipeline. Measured lift must hug zero **and** the variance
   estimator must be calibrated. This catches the failure in §3.4.

## 3. The cost: intra-cluster correlation and the design effect

### 3.1 ICC

Users in a cluster resemble each other. Decompose:

$$Y_{ij} = \mu + u_j + \varepsilon_{ij}, \qquad u_j \sim (0, \sigma_b^2), \quad \varepsilon_{ij} \sim (0, \sigma_w^2)$$

The **intra-cluster correlation** is the share of variance that lives
*between* clusters:

$$\rho = \frac{\sigma_b^2}{\sigma_b^2 + \sigma_w^2}$$

| Setting | Typical $\rho$ |
|---|---|
| Geo (DMA) revenue | 0.01 – 0.10 |
| Social graph engagement | 0.02 – 0.10 |
| Classroom test scores | 0.10 – 0.25 |
| Gaming guild spend | 0.05 – 0.20 |

$\rho$ looks small. It isn't — it gets multiplied by cluster size.

### 3.2 The design effect

$$\boxed{\;\text{Deff} = 1 + (m-1)\rho\;}\qquad N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{N}{\text{Deff}}$$

with $m$ = cluster size. Your $N$ users have the power of
$N_{\text{eff}}$ independent ones. Since $n \propto \sigma^2$ (§2.2),
**Deff multiplies your required sample directly.**

At $N = 100{,}000$, $\rho = 0.05$:

| $m$ | Deff | $N_{\text{eff}}$ | You kept |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10 | 1.45 | 68,966 | 69% |
| 100 | 5.95 | 16,807 | 17% |
| 1,000 | 50.95 | **1,963** | **2%** |

**At $m = 1{,}000$ and $\rho = 0.05$ — a mild ICC — 100,000 users
have the power of 1,963.** You did not lose some power. You lost
essentially all of it.

The asymptotics explain why. As $m \to \infty$ with $N$ fixed:

$$N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{N}{1+(m-1)\rho} \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{N}{m\rho} = \frac{K}{\rho}$$

where $K = N/m$ is the **number of clusters**. **Effective sample size
is governed by the number of clusters, not the number of users.**
Adding users to existing clusters buys almost nothing; adding clusters
is everything. This is the single most important fact in cluster
experimentation, and it's why "we have 100M users" is not an answer to
"are you powered?"

### 3.3 Unequal cluster sizes — the hidden killer

The textbook formula assumes equal $m$. Real clusters are wildly skewed,
and the correct formula is:

$$\text{Deff} = 1 + \left[(\text{CV}^2 + 1)\bar{m} - 1\right]\rho$$

where CV is the coefficient of variation of cluster sizes. At
$\bar{m} = 100$, $\rho = 0.05$:

| CV | Deff | $N_{\text{eff}}$ (of 100k) |
|---|---|---|
| 0 (equal) | 5.95 | 16,807 |
| 1 | 10.95 | 9,132 |
| 3 | 50.95 | 1,963 |

**CV enters squared.** A few giant clusters among many small ones
destroys the design even when $\bar{m}$ looks harmless. This is the
mechanism behind §6, and it's why reporting mean cluster size without
the CV is close to meaningless.

### 3.4 Inference: the number of clusters, again

Cluster-robust standard errors are **consistent in the number of
clusters**, not observations. With few clusters they are biased *down* —
your CIs are too narrow and you over-reject. This is the failure an A/A
test catches (§README 3.1): everything looks fine, and your false
positive rate is 15%.

| $K$ (clusters) | What to do |
|---|---|
| $K > 50$ | CRVE fine |
| $20 < K \le 50$ | CRVE + **wild cluster bootstrap** (Cameron-Gelbach-Miller 2008) |
| $K \le 20$ | **Randomization inference**; do not trust CRVE |

**Rule of thumb: 40+ clusters.** Below that, the asymptotics that
justify your standard error simply don't hold, and no amount of user
volume rescues them.

### 3.5 The trade-off, stated plainly

| | **Large clusters** | **Small clusters** |
|---|---|---|
| **SUTVA** | ✅ Near-perfect isolation | ❌ Heavy bleed |
| **Power** | ❌ Deff explodes, $K$ tiny | ✅ Deff ≈ 1 |
| **Inference** | ❌ CRVE invalid | ✅ Fine |
| **Fails as** | Underpowered, outlier-driven | Biased toward zero |

**Prefer many small clusters to few large ones — *conditional on the
small clusters sealing the interference.*** The conditional is the whole
sentence. Small clusters that leak give you a precise estimate of the
wrong thing, and §6 is a scenario engineered to make you feel the
difference.

## 4. Handling edge users: exposure mapping

No partition is perfect. Some users have strong ties outside their
cluster. What do you do with them?

### 4.1 The tempting wrong answer

> "Drop users with cut edges. Keep the clean interior."

This is **not** post-treatment conditioning — network position is
pre-treatment, so it doesn't open a collider (§README 3.4). It is
nonetheless usually wrong, for a subtler reason:

**It changes the estimand, and in the worst possible direction.** Users
with few cross-cluster edges are the *least connected* users. For a
social feature, connectivity is the primary effect moderator. So you'd
be estimating:

$$E[\tau_i \mid i \text{ is socially isolated}]$$

and reporting it as the ATE. **You've measured the effect of a social
feature on the least social users.** The estimate is internally valid
and answers a question nobody asked — the Lecture 1 §4 failure mode,
wearing a lab coat.

### 4.2 The right answer: model exposure

Following **Aronow & Samii (2017)**, replace binary treatment with an
**exposure mapping**. For user $i$ with interference-graph neighbors
$\mathcal{N}(i)$, define exposure as the treated share of neighbors:

$$\varepsilon_i = \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} w_{ij} D_j}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} w_{ij}} \;\in [0,1]$$

Then define exposure *conditions*, e.g.:

| Condition | $D_i$ | $\varepsilon_i$ |
|---|---|---|
| Pure treatment | 1 | $\ge 0.9$ |
| Pure control | 0 | $\le 0.1$ |
| Mixed | either | otherwise |

The key move: **you designed the randomization, so you know
$\Pr(\text{exposure condition } k \mid \text{design})$ exactly** — or can
compute it by Monte Carlo, re-running your assignment 10,000 times over
the fixed graph. That gives a **Horvitz-Thompson** estimator with
design-based (not model-based) probabilities:

$$\hat{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \frac{Y_i \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{cond}_i = k]}{\Pr(\text{cond}_i = k)}$$

$$\widehat{\text{direct}} = \hat{\mu}_{\text{pure treat}} - \hat{\mu}_{\text{pure control}}$$

Nothing is dropped, nothing is assumed about the outcome model, and the
probabilities come from the design you control.

### 4.3 The bonus: spillover becomes a result

Regress on own-treatment **and** neighbor exposure:

$$Y_i = \alpha + \tau D_i + \gamma \varepsilon_i + \eta (D_i \cdot \varepsilon_i) + \epsilon_i$$

- $\tau$ — **direct effect**
- $\gamma$ — **spillover effect**: what happens to *you* when your
  neighbors are treated

$\gamma$ is not a nuisance. **It is the number that decides the
rollout.** If $\gamma > 0$, the feature has positive network
externalities, and the full-launch effect **exceeds** anything your
experiment measured — because at 100% rollout everyone is at
$\varepsilon = 1$, a cell your 50/50 experiment barely populated.
Total effect at full launch $\approx \tau + \gamma$, not $\tau$.

**This flips the interpretation.** The interference you were treating as
contamination is, for a social product, the *mechanism*. Measuring it
turns your biggest methodological threat into your most valuable
finding. (Identifying $\gamma$ well generally wants **two-stage
randomization** — randomize clusters to *saturation levels* (e.g. 25% /
50% / 75% treated), then randomize within. Hudgens & Halloran (2008);
Baird et al. (2018) for the power analysis.)

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.api as sm

# ---------- design effect ----------
def deff(m_bar, rho, cv=0.0):
    """Design effect. cv = coefficient of variation of CLUSTER SIZES.
    cv enters squared — a few giant clusters dominate everything."""
    return 1 + ((cv**2 + 1) * m_bar - 1) * rho

def n_eff(N, m_bar, rho, cv=0.0):
    return N / deff(m_bar, rho, cv)

for m in [10, 100, 1000]:
    print(f"m={m:5d} rho=0.05 -> Deff={deff(m, .05):6.2f}  N_eff={n_eff(1e5, m, .05):9,.0f}")

# The number of CLUSTERS is what powers the experiment, not users:
#   N_eff -> K/rho  as m grows.  Adding users to existing clusters is ~free
#   power; adding clusters is everything.

# ---------- required clusters ----------
def clusters_needed(mde, sigma, m, rho, power=0.8, alpha=0.05):
    from scipy.stats import norm
    z = norm.ppf(1 - alpha / 2) + norm.ppf(power)
    n_ind = 2 * sigma**2 * z**2 / mde**2      # per arm, if independent
    return np.ceil(n_ind * deff(m, rho) / m) * 2   # x2 arms

print(clusters_needed(mde=0.02, sigma=1.0, m=100, rho=0.05), "clusters needed")

# ---------- ICC from data (one-way random effects) ----------
def icc(df, cluster, y):
    g = df.groupby(cluster)[y]
    k, n = g.ngroups, len(df)
    m0 = (n - (g.count()**2).sum() / n) / (k - 1)   # unequal-size correction
    msb = g.count().mul((g.mean() - df[y].mean())**2).sum() / (k - 1)
    msw = ((df[y] - g.transform("mean"))**2).sum() / (n - k)
    return max(0.0, (msb - msw) / (msb + (m0 - 1) * msw))

# ---------- inference: it's about K, not N ----------
K = df["cluster_id"].nunique()
fit = sm.OLS(df["Y"], sm.add_constant(df[["D"]])).fit(
    cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["cluster_id"]}
)
if K < 40:
    print(f"WARNING: K={K} clusters. CRVE is anti-conservative here.")
    print("  -> wild cluster bootstrap (K in 20-50) or randomization inference (K<=20)")

# ---------- exposure mapping (Aronow & Samii 2017) ----------
# A: adjacency of the INTERFERENCE graph (not whatever graph you clustered on)
def exposure(A, D):
    w = np.asarray(A.sum(axis=1)).ravel()
    return np.divide(np.asarray(A @ D).ravel(), w, out=np.zeros_like(w, float), where=w > 0)

def exposure_probs(A, cluster_ids, n_sim=10_000, seed=0):
    """Monte-Carlo the exposure distribution over YOUR OWN design.
    You know these probabilities exactly because you chose the randomization."""
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    clusters = np.unique(cluster_ids)
    idx = np.searchsorted(clusters, cluster_ids)
    counts = {"pure_t": np.zeros(len(cluster_ids)), "pure_c": np.zeros(len(cluster_ids))}
    for _ in range(n_sim):
        Dc = rng.integers(0, 2, size=len(clusters))
        D = Dc[idx]
        e = exposure(A, D)
        counts["pure_t"] += (D == 1) & (e >= 0.9)
        counts["pure_c"] += (D == 0) & (e <= 0.1)
    return {k: v / n_sim for k, v in counts.items()}

# Horvitz-Thompson: nothing dropped, design-based probabilities
probs = exposure_probs(A, df["cluster_id"].values)
e = exposure(A, df["D"].values)
pure_t = (df.D == 1) & (e >= 0.9)
pure_c = (df.D == 0) & (e <= 0.1)
mu_t = (df.Y[pure_t] / probs["pure_t"][pure_t]).sum() / len(df)
mu_c = (df.Y[pure_c] / probs["pure_c"][pure_c]).sum() / len(df)
print(f"HT direct effect = {mu_t - mu_c:.4f}")

# ---------- spillover as a RESULT, not a nuisance ----------
df["exposure"] = e
spill = sm.OLS(
    df["Y"], sm.add_constant(df[["D", "exposure"]])
).fit(cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["cluster_id"]})
print(f"direct   tau   = {spill.params['D']:.4f}")
print(f"spillover gamma = {spill.params['exposure']:.4f}")
print(f"full-launch effect ~ {spill.params['D'] + spill.params['exposure']:.4f}")
# If gamma > 0, the launch beats anything the 50/50 experiment could measure.
```

## 6. Worked example: the LinkedIn feed

**Setup.** New ranking algorithm that **heavily prioritizes posts from
people at your current company**. Network effects are obvious, so you
must cluster. Two proposals:

- **Option A** — cluster by **company**. Microsoft's 200,000 employees
  in one cluster; Stripe's 50,000 in another.
- **Option B** — **Louvain** on **direct-messaging history**, clusters
  capped at exactly **100 users**.

### 6.1 Which one? — Option A, and it isn't close

The question is engineered to make B look right. B has a Deff of 5.95
against A's 483 (below), it has millions of clusters, and it uses a real
algorithm. **B is a catastrophe, and the reason is §2.1.**

**Identify the interference graph.** The feature promotes *same-company*
posts. So treating user $i$ → $i$ sees more colleague content → $i$
engages more with colleagues → colleague $j$'s post gets engagement →
$j$ posts more. **Interference flows along company co-membership edges.**
The interference graph is a union of cliques, one per company.

**Now score each option on the graph that matters:**

| | Clusters on | Cut ratio on **interference** graph |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Company | **≈ 0** — company edges never cross clusters, by construction |
| **B** | DM history | **catastrophic** — Microsoft is split into 2,000 independently randomized clusters |

Option B forcibly shatters every company larger than 100 people. Treated
Microsoft employees post more; their control-group colleagues see it.
Contamination is *maximal* precisely where the feature is most active.

**Option B's beautiful modularity is on the DM graph, and the DM graph
is not the interference graph.** Colleagues who never DM each other still
interfere — the feature routes content by *employer*, not by
conversation. B optimizes a quantity that has nothing to do with the
validity of the experiment. A high-$Q$ partition of an irrelevant graph
is an irrelevant partition.

**Verdict: A. SUTVA is not tradeable against power.** B gives a precise
estimate of an undefined quantity; A gives an imprecise estimate of the
right one. Imprecision you can fix with time and money. An undefined
estimand you cannot fix at all.

### 6.2 Now pay A's bill — and it's steeper than $\bar{m}$ suggests

A simulated LinkedIn-like company distribution (lognormal + a handful of
megacorps), $\rho = 0.05$:

```
companies = 182,399   users = 11,413,321
mean size  = 62.6     median size = 9      CV = 12.38
```

Mean cluster size of 62 sounds benign. It is not — **CV = 12.4**, and CV
enters squared (§3.3):

$$\text{Deff} = 1 + \left[(12.38^2 + 1)(62.6) - 1\right](0.05) = 483.3$$

$$N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{11{,}413{,}321}{483.3} = \mathbf{23{,}613}$$

**11.4 million users have the statistical power of 23,613.** Not because
the average company is big — the median company has *nine people* — but
because five megacorps dominate the variance.

### 6.3 The fix: trim the tail, and say so

Exclude the giants from the randomized experiment:

| Design | Companies | Users | $\bar{m}$ | CV | Deff | $N_{\text{eff}}$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| All companies | 182,399 | 11.4M | 62.6 | 12.38 | 483.3 | **23,613** |
| Cap at 10,000 | 182,366 | 10.3M | 56.7 | 4.29 | 56.0 | **184,807** |
| **Cap at 1,000** | 180,965 | 7.3M | 40.6 | 2.36 | 14.3 | **512,897** |

**Dropping 36% of users buys 22× the effective sample.** That is the
most counterintuitive and most useful number in this chapter: you throw
away a third of the data and the experiment gets twenty-two times more
powerful, because you removed the variance, not the information.

Then be honest about what you did — the estimand is now:

$$E[\tau_i \mid i \text{ at a company with} < 1{,}000 \text{ employees}]$$

**Handle the megacorps separately, not by ignoring them.** Microsoft at
200k users isn't a cluster — it's a *market*. Give it a within-company
design: a switchback (§2.5), or a synthetic-control case study (Lecture
1 §5's logic — treat it as N=1). This is a feature of the analysis, not
an apology for it: mega-employers are where the feature matters most, so
they deserve a purpose-built read rather than being averaged into a
number they'd dominate anyway.

**Also stratify by company size** within the randomized population.
Doesn't reduce Deff, but prevents the draw where treatment gets all the
900-person companies — with CV = 2.36 remaining, that's a live risk.

### 6.4 The second question: edge users

*"Under Option B, some users have strong ties outside their cluster. How
do you handle them?"*

The question presupposes B — but answer it on the merits, because the
same issue arises under A (contractors, dual affiliations, recent job
changers whose "current company" is stale).

**Don't drop them.** §4.1: dropping the cut-edge users is legal
(pre-treatment) but redefines the estimand to *the effect on
poorly-connected users*, and for a feature about professional networks
that's precisely the wrong subpopulation. You'd measure a social
feature's effect on the least social people and call it the ATE.

**Do this instead** (§4.2–4.3):

1. **Exposure mapping.** $\varepsilon_i$ = treated share of $i$'s
   *company* neighbors. Not DM neighbors — the interference graph again.
2. **Horvitz-Thompson with design-based probabilities.** Monte-Carlo
   your own randomization 10,000× over the fixed graph to get
   $\Pr(\text{cond}_i = k)$ exactly. Nothing dropped, no outcome model.
3. **Report the spillover $\gamma$ as a headline result.** For this
   feature $\gamma > 0$ is the expected finding, and it means the
   full-launch effect **exceeds** the experiment's estimate — at 100%
   rollout every user sits at $\varepsilon = 1$, a cell a 50/50 design
   barely populates. Reporting $\tau$ alone *understates the launch*.
4. **Bound it.** If the cut ratio on the interference graph is $c$, the
   attenuation is roughly proportional to $c$; report $\hat\tau$ and
   $\hat\tau/(1-c)$ as a sensitivity band.

**The meta-point:** the interference you designed around is, for a social
product, the mechanism you're trying to build. Under A the spillover is
inside the cluster and *invisible* — which is exactly what makes the
estimate clean, and also what makes $\gamma$ unmeasurable. If you want
both the clean ATE *and* $\gamma$, you need saturation randomization
(§4.3), not a better partition.

## 7. Common misreadings

**"Our Louvain partition has modularity 0.7 and a 3% cut ratio, so we're clean."**
On *which* graph? If it isn't the graph the treatment travels along,
those numbers are decoration. §6.1.

**"We have 100M users, we're fine on power."**
$N_{\text{eff}} \to K/\rho$. **Clusters** power the experiment. 100M
users in 50 clusters has the power of $50/\rho$. §3.2.

**"Mean cluster size is 62, that's manageable."**
Not with CV = 12.4. Deff = 483. Report the CV or you've reported
nothing. §3.3, §6.2.

**"Clustering is conservative — if we find an effect, it's real."**
Backwards. Clustering costs *power*, so it inflates false **negatives**.
It's the *unclustered* test on network data that's anti-conservative:
under-estimated SEs → over-rejection.

**"Just drop the users with cut edges."**
Legal, but it silently redefines the estimand to the effect on isolated
users. §4.1.

**"Spillover is contamination to be minimized."**
For a social product it's the mechanism, and $\gamma > 0$ means your
launch beats your experiment. §4.3.

**"We used cluster-robust SEs, so inference is handled."**
CRVE is consistent in $K$, not $N$. Under 40 clusters it's biased down
and your A/A test will show 15% false positives. §3.4.
