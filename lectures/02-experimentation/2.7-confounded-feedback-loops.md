# 2.7 — Confounded Feedback Loops & Alternating Optimization

> **Part of:** [Lecture 2 — Experimentation](./README.md)
> **Connects to:** [Lecture 8 — Selection on Observables](../08-selection-on-observables/) (the confounding) and [Lecture 9 — DML](../09-double-machine-learning/) (de-confounding with ML). The fix is an *experiment* — a tiny randomized slice — anchoring a giant logged ML system.

Every other chapter in this lecture randomizes to *measure* an effect. This one
randomizes to *keep a production ML system honest.* Rankers and recommenders
create a confounding loop in their own logs, and the only escape is a small,
permanent randomized holdback that acts as a **causal sensor.** It's the most
important experimentation-ops idea most teams don't run.

---

## 1. The problem — the recommender filter bubble

In a feed or search system, whether a user clicks an item depends on two things:

1. **Quality $X$** — how genuinely good/relevant the item is.
2. **Placement $\pi$** — where it's ranked (Rank 1 vs. Rank 10).

Here's the loop: **the system is *designed* to put high-quality items in high
placements.** So in your historical logs, quality and placement are **almost
perfectly correlated** — the good items are *also* the well-placed items. There
is no independent variation to tell them apart.

Train an XGBoost on those logs to predict clicks from $X$ and $\pi$, and it
**cannot separate the two.** It will decide "Rank 1 is magic" — overstating the
causal effect of placement and understating true quality — because in the data,
being at Rank 1 and being good are the same thing.

*Verified* (code in §8): with a true placement effect of **2.0** and a true
quality effect of **1.0**, a naive model on the confounded logs estimates the
placement effect at **3.0** — it soaked up quality *and* placement into one
inflated number.

---

## 2. The quarantine architecture

You cannot randomize 100% of traffic to get clean data — scrambling every user's
ranking would torch the experience and revenue. Instead, split the stream:

```
   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │  Stream A — OBSERVATIONAL (99%)                               │
   │  Production ranker runs normally.                             │
   │  Massive, high-dimensional... and heavily CONFOUNDED.         │
   │  Job: learn item Quality X (millions of features).           │
   ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
   │  Stream B — RANDOMIZED HOLDBACK (1%)                          │
   │  Ranking is SHUFFLED — good items low, bad items high.        │
   │  Tiny... but PURE, unconfounded causal signal for placement.  │
   │  Job: learn the placement effect beta_pi, cleanly.           │
   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

- **Stream A (99%)** gives you the scale to fight the curse of dimensionality —
  learning quality scores over millions of items — but it's confounded (§1).
- **Stream B (1%)** shuffles placement so that **placement ⊥ quality** by
  construction. It's small, but it contains the one thing Stream A can't
  provide: *independent variation in placement.* This is an RCT (§2.1) embedded
  in production. *Verified* (§8): estimating the placement effect on the
  randomized 1% recovers the true **2.0**, where the logs said 3.0.

---

## 3. Alternating optimization

You want to fit, without the two terms poisoning each other:

```math
\text{Click} = \underbrace{\text{Quality}(X)}_{\text{learn on the 99\%}} + \underbrace{\beta_\pi \cdot \pi}_{\text{learn on the 1\%}}
```

The trick (an EM-like alternation) is to learn each term where it's
*identified*, holding the other fixed:

- **Step 1 — fix quality, update placement.** On the **1% randomized** holdback,
  placement was shuffled, so quality is irrelevant to placement assignment.
  Estimate the causal placement effect $\beta_\pi$ **strictly on this clean
  data.**
- **Step 2 — fix placement, update quality.** On the **99% observational**
  stream, plug the just-learned $\beta_\pi$ into the quality model as a **fixed
  offset** — a hardcoded constant the model must accept. The neural net is
  forced to treat position bias as a law of physics, freeing 100% of its
  capacity to learn *true quality*, un-poisoned by position.
- **Step 3 — iterate** until convergence.

The offset is the whole mechanism: by subtracting the *cleanly-estimated*
placement effect before the quality model looks at the data, you **quarantine**
the confounding. The 99% handles dimensionality; the 1% is the **causal anchor**
that stops the model from lying to itself about *why* things get clicked.

**HTE extension:** replace the scalar $\beta_\pi$ with a function
$\tau(X_{\text{user}})$ — the placement effect can itself vary by user (some
users read to Rank 10, some never scroll), estimated on the randomized slice.

---

## 4. Monitoring — the holdback as a causal sensor

The randomized slice isn't just for training; it's a **live diagnostic**:

- **Drift on the quality score** — track **PSI / KL divergence** of the quality
  distribution over time. A sudden shift means the model is learning something
  new (or leaking bias, §7).
- **Rolling holdback ATE — the "causal canary."** Continuously re-estimate
  $\beta_\pi$ on the fresh 1% randomized data. If the *true* placement effect
  drifts (a UI change, §7) the canary catches it immediately, while a frozen
  constant would silently rot.

## 5. Worked probe: the VP who wants to kill the holdback

**Setup.** You're tech lead for the YouTube homepage ranker, running this exact
architecture. Six months in, a new VP of Engineering: *"Why are we deliberately
showing bad recommendations to 1% of users? That holdout costs \$5M/year in lost
ad revenue. Turn it off — we learned the placement effect months ago, freeze
that constant and train quality on 100% of the stream."*

### 5.1 What happens to "Quality" over the next year?

**The quality score silently rots into a popularity score — position bias leaks
back in.** Freeze $\beta_\pi$ and kill the holdback, and you've destroyed the
quarantine. The UI is *not* static: enlarge the Rank-1 thumbnail, change
autoplay, shift user behavior — and the *true* placement effect changes. But
your frozen constant doesn't know.

Now the quality model sees huge click-through on Rank-1 videos, subtracts the
*stale, too-small* placement penalty, and finds **excess clicks left over.**
Desperate to minimize its loss, it attributes those leftover clicks to **item
quality.** Within a year, "quality" no longer measures relevance — it measures
*"was this recently placed at Rank 1?"* The confounded loop is fully
re-established.

*Verified* (§8): with the frozen $\beta_\pi = 2.0$ but the true effect drifted
to 3.0, the quality coefficient **inflates to 1.99** against a true value of
**1.0** — the un-subtracted placement clicks get mislabeled as quality, exactly
this leakage.

### 5.2 The business consequence — the death of the creator ecosystem

This is **"the rich get richer" (popularity bias)**, and for a UGC platform it's
fatal. Trace a brand-new video (the cold-start problem):

1. **The stale top slots.** Old popular videos have had their quality scores
   inflated by leaked position bias — they're glued to the top.
2. **Penalty of obscurity.** The new video starts at Rank 50, gets few clicks
   because it's buried.
3. **False demotion.** The algorithm sees the low clicks, applies the frozen
   penalty, and assigns the new video a *terrible* quality score.

New videos can never break out. The feed goes stale, engagement drops, and — the
real damage — **new creators leave**, because the algorithm structurally refuses
to distribute their content. To save \$5M in holdback cost, the VP set a
\$500M+ platform death spiral in motion.

### 5.3 The defense

> *"The 1% holdout is not a cost — it's a **causal sensor.** We pay for server
> monitoring so hardware doesn't crash silently; we pay for this holdout so our
> \$10B recommendation engine doesn't drift into a popularity-bias death spiral.
> The placement effect is not a constant we learned once — it moves every time
> we touch the UI, and the only thing that catches the drift is fresh randomized
> data. Turning it off doesn't save \$5M; it blinds the system to its own
> confounding."*

The staff-level reframe: a randomized holdback is **causal observability
infrastructure**, in the same budget category as logging and monitoring — not a
line item to optimize away.

---

## 6. Why this is the right tool

It combines the two halves of this whole series: the **99% observational**
stream fights the curse of dimensionality (Lectures 8–9 territory — millions of
item features), while the **1% experiment** (this lecture) supplies the clean
identifying variation that observational data structurally cannot. Neither alone
works: pure observational is confounded (§1); pure randomization is too
expensive at 100%. The architecture is the marriage.

## 7. Python

```python
import numpy as np

# Logs: q (item quality features), pi (placement boost), click. A 1% subset is
# the RANDOMIZED holdback where pi was shuffled independent of quality.

def alternating_optimization(qA, piA, clickA, qB, piB, clickB, n_iter=5):
    """Stream A = 99% observational (confounded). Stream B = 1% randomized."""
    beta_pi = 0.0
    for _ in range(n_iter):
        # Step 1: learn placement effect ONLY on the randomized holdback (clean)
        X = np.column_stack([qB, piB, np.ones(len(qB))])   # pi _|_ q here
        beta_pi = np.linalg.lstsq(X, clickB, rcond=None)[0][1]
        # Step 2: learn quality on the 99%, with beta_pi as a FIXED OFFSET
        resid = clickA - beta_pi * piA                     # subtract clean placement effect
        quality_model = fit_quality(qA, resid)             # NN/GBM on the residual
    return beta_pi, quality_model

# Naive (do NOT do this): regress click on pi over the confounded logs ->
#   beta_pi ~ 3.0 vs true 2.0 (soaks up quality). The holdback recovers 2.0.

# Monitoring:
#   psi(quality_scores_today, quality_scores_baseline)   # drift alarm
#   rolling_beta_pi = estimate on the latest 1% randomized batch  # causal canary
```

## 8. Common misreadings

**"Just put quality and placement in one big model — it'll sort them out."**
It can't: in the logs they're collinear (good items are well-placed), so the
model attributes clicks to placement arbitrarily. Verified: 3.0 vs true 2.0. §1.

**"We learned the placement effect once; freeze it."**
The placement effect *drifts* with every UI and behavior change. A frozen
constant leaks the drift into the quality score (verified: quality inflates to
1.99 vs true 1.0), re-confounding the system. §5.

**"The 1% holdback is a cost to minimize."**
It's causal observability infrastructure — a sensor, like monitoring. Killing it
saves a little revenue and blinds the system to a popularity death spiral. §5.3.

**"Randomize more than 1% for a better estimate."**
1% is usually enough for a low-dimensional $\beta_\pi$, and more scrambles more
users' experience. Scale the holdback to the dimensionality of what you're
identifying (bigger for $\tau(X)$ HTE), not bigger for its own sake. §3.

**"This is an observational method."**
Its identifying power comes from the *randomized* 1% — an embedded RCT (§2.1).
The observational 99% only supplies dimensionality; without the experiment,
there's no clean $\beta_\pi$. §2, §6.
