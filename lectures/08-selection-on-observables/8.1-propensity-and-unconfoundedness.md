# 8.1 — The Propensity Score & Unconfoundedness

> **Part of:** [Lecture 8 — Selection on Observables](./README.md)

---

## 1. The curse of dimensionality, and the theorem that beats it

To compare a treated user to a control "twin," you'd want them identical on
every covariate — age, tenure, spend, geo, device, 50 of them. In high
dimensions this is hopeless: partition 50 covariates into bins and you have
more cells than users, so **exact matching finds no twins.**

**Rosenbaum & Rubin (1983)** collapse the problem. You don't need to match on
all 50 covariates $X$ — only on the scalar **propensity score**:

```math
e(X) = P(D = 1 \mid X)
```

Their theorem: **if unconfoundedness holds given $X$, it holds given $e(X)$.**
Two users with the same propensity — both 75% likely to be treated — but one
treated and one not, are comparable as a local coin flip. The propensity score
is a *balancing score*: conditioning on it makes the covariate distributions of
the two arms identical. Fifty dimensions become one.

---

## 2. The two assumptions (recap with teeth)

- **Unconfoundedness:** $\{Y(1), Y(0)\} \perp\!\!\!\perp D \mid X$ — you
  measured *every* confounder. Untestable. §3–5 are about why this is the whole
  ballgame.
- **Overlap:** $0 < e(X) < 1$ — everyone could have gone either way. §4 is
  about why this is where the method dies.

---

## 3. The golden rule: what PSM does and doesn't fix

Here is the single sentence to carry out of this entire lecture:

> **Propensity score matching does not solve selection bias; it only solves the
> curse of dimensionality for the variables we happen to have measured.**

The propensity score is a *dimension-reduction device*. It turns "match on 50
things" into "match on 1 thing." That is genuinely useful. But it operates
*entirely within the covariates you collected.* If a confounder isn't in $X$,
no propensity model — however sophisticated — can balance it, because it isn't
there to balance. Unconfoundedness is an assumption about the **world**, not a
property you can achieve with better code.

---

## 4. Overlap, and the R² / AUC illusion

A natural instinct: *"If my propensity model has high explanatory power (high
AUC / R²), I've captured the important features, so I've ruled out
confounders."* **This is wrong, and it's the most dangerous confusion between
prediction and causation.**

**High predictive power tells you nothing about unobserved confounders.** A high
AUC means your model predicts *treatment assignment* well **using the observed
$X$ you gave it.** It says nothing about the unobserved $U$ missing from your
table. The ice-cream/shark-attack paradox makes it concrete: you can predict
ice-cream sales with R² = 0.95 from "month of year," but if the true confounder
(**temperature**, driving both ice cream *and* swimming) is missing, your great
predictive model is causally meaningless.

**And "too high" is actively bad.** If the propensity model separates the
groups *perfectly* (AUC → 1), then treated and control users occupy disjoint
regions of covariate space — **no control looks like any treated unit.** That
is exactly an **overlap violation**: the matcher can't find twins and drops
your data. In PSM, a propensity AUC around **0.75 is the sweet spot**; an AUC
of 0.99 means your design is dead, not excellent. A perfect predictive model is
a broken causal one.

## 5. Worked probe: the Spotify opt-in

**Setup.** Spotify launches **"Social Listening Lounges"** — strictly opt-in
(click a banner). Six months later, measure the effect of joining on 3-month
retention. You build a 100-feature XGBoost propensity model, caliper-match, and
the **Love plot is flawless** — all 100 covariates balanced at SMD < 0.05. The
result: **+15% retention** for joiners. The PM wants to auto-enroll everyone.

You refuse to validate it. Why?

### 5.1 The unobserved confounder

**Instantaneous intent — the user's intrinsic social motivation at the moment
they saw the banner.** Two users with identical age, tier, and listening hours
both get a 90% propensity. Both see the banner. User A clicks; User B ignores
it. *Why did B ignore it?* Studying for finals, no friends on the app, quietly
churning. User A was feeling social and engaged **at that exact second.**

The +15% is not the causal effect of the Lounge. It's massively inflated by
User A's **pre-existing, unobservable motivation** — which drives *both* the
click *and* the retention. Classic omitted-variable bias, and the omitted
variable is a psychological state you never logged.

### 5.2 Why the flawless Love plot is a trap

A perfect Love plot proves you balanced the data **that was in your SQL
table.** It proves you're a competent programmer. It proves **nothing** about
causal validity, because it is structurally blind to the unmeasured confounder.
The balance you achieved is balance on the *observed* $X$; the bias lives in the
*unobserved* $U$, which no diagnostic on $X$ can see. **The better the Love plot
looks, the more confident — and more wrong — you become.**

This is the golden rule (§3) in action: matching balanced the curse of
dimensionality, not the selection bias.

## 6. Getting closer to "identical twins" — the playbook

Your instinct is exactly right: PSM tries to build statistical twins, and it
only works if you've captured *every* covariate driving both opt-in and
retention. But **you cannot fix unobserved confounders statistically** — no
XGBoost tuning balances a variable that isn't in the data. Three honest moves:

### 6.1 Ruthless proxy feature engineering

If you know the confounder is "instantaneous social intent," force it into the
observed space with behavioral proxies from raw logs:
`playlist_shares_last_24h`, `friend_profiles_viewed_today`,
`seconds_looking_at_the_banner_before_closing_it`. You can't measure motivation
directly, but you can proxy the *behavioral trace* it leaves. This narrows the
gap; it never closes it.

### 6.2 Encouragement design (pivot to IV)

If the confounding is too severe, **abandon PSM and randomize a nudge.** You
can't force people to join an opt-in feature — but you *can* randomly send 50%
of users a **push notification** to join. Then use "received the nudge" as an
**instrument** $Z$ for "actually joined" $D$ (Lecture 5). This recovers the LATE
and **bypasses the unobserved motivation entirely**, because the nudge is
randomized.

> **"If I'm already running an A/B test, why do I need IV?"** Because you're not
> A/B-testing the *feature* — you're A/B-testing the *nudge*. Comparing nudged
> vs. not-nudged gives the **ITT**: "the ROI of sending a push notification."
> That is *not* "the causal effect of joining a Lounge," because joining is
> still a self-selected choice infected by motivation. To get the effect of the
> feature itself, you divide the ITT of the outcome by the ITT of adoption — the
> Wald ratio, which is exactly what 2SLS does (Lecture 5 §1). The A/B test gives
> you a *valid instrument*, not the treatment effect directly.

### 6.3 Two-way fixed effects (pivot to panel)

If you have longitudinal data, you don't match User A to User B — **you match
User A to User A in the past** (a within-subject design):

```math
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \tau D_{it} + \epsilon_{it}
```

- **$\alpha_i$ (user fixed effects)** create a dummy for *every user*,
  absorbing **all time-invariant unobserved traits** — whether a user is
  naturally an extrovert with 10% higher baseline retention. It's subtracted
  out. So TWFE compares a user's post-opt-in retention to *their own* history.
- **$\lambda_t$ (time fixed effects)** absorb global shocks (a Week-4 outage).

**The catch — and it's the reason this isn't a silver bullet:** TWFE only fixes
*time-invariant* confounders. If the user's social motivation **spiked in the
exact week they joined** (a time-varying confounder), TWFE is still biased. And
under staggered opt-in with heterogeneous effects, you inherit every
[Lecture 3 §3.3](../03-difference-in-differences/3.3-staggered-did.md) caveat.
It's a better tool for opt-in than PSM, not a perfect one.

## 7. Common misreadings

**"High AUC means I've controlled for confounding."**
It means you predict *treatment* well from *observed* $X$. It's silent on
unobserved $U$. Prediction ≠ causation. §4.

**"A near-perfect propensity model is the goal."**
It destroys overlap — perfectly separated groups have no twins. Aim for AUC
~0.75, not 0.99. §4.

**"My Love plot is flawless, so the estimate is valid."**
The Love plot only shows balance on *measured* covariates. It cannot see the
confounder you didn't collect. §5.2.

**"With enough features, PSM removes selection bias."**
PSM removes the *curse of dimensionality* for measured covariates. Selection on
*unobservables* is untouched. §3.

**"An A/B test of a nudge gives the feature's effect."**
It gives the ITT of the nudge. The feature effect needs the IV/Wald step
(ITT_Y ÷ ITT_adoption). §6.2.

**"TWFE solves opt-in confounding."**
Only the *time-invariant* part. A motivation spike in the opt-in week is
time-varying and still biases it. §6.3.
