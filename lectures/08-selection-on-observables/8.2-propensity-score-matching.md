# 8.2 — Propensity Score Matching, End to End

> **Part of:** [Lecture 8 — Selection on Observables](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§8.1](./8.1-propensity-and-unconfoundedness.md) — the propensity score and its two assumptions.

We build the whole pipeline on one running example: the DoorDash opt-in promo.
PSM targets the **ATT** — the effect on the drivers who actually opted in.

**The setup.** *"Opt in Friday: complete 10 deliveries this weekend, get a \$50
bonus."* $D = 1$ if the driver tapped Opt-In, $0$ if they saw the banner and
ignored it. Outcome $Y$ = deliveries completed *next* week. The confound:
motivated drivers are more likely to opt in **and** more likely to keep driving
regardless of the bonus.

---

## 1. Why matching estimates the ATT (the algebra you asked about)

For a treated driver $i$, the true effect is $\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)$. We
observe $Y_i(1) = Y_i$; the counterfactual $Y_i(0)$ is missing. Matching finds a
control twin $j(i)$ with nearly the same propensity, $e(X_i) \approx
e(X_{j(i)})$, and — *because their treatment probabilities were identical* —
uses that twin's observed outcome $Y_{j(i)}$ as a stand-in for the missing
$Y_i(0)$:

```math
\hat{\tau}_i = Y_i - Y_{j(i)}
```

Average over all treated units:

```math
\hat{\tau}_{\text{ATT}} = \frac{1}{N_T} \sum_{i \in T} \left(Y_i - Y_{j(i)}\right)
```

Sum-of-differences = difference-of-sums, so this collapses to exactly the
formula in your notes:

```math
\hat{\tau}_{\text{ATT}} = \operatorname{mean}\!\left(Y_{\text{treated, matched}}\right) - \operatorname{mean}\!\left(Y_{\text{control, matched}}\right)
```

**Two things this makes explicit.** It's the **ATT**, not the ATE, because we
average over the *treated* units and impute *their* counterfactuals — we learn
nothing about drivers who'd never opt in. And **the unmatched controls are
thrown away**: a control driver with no treated twin never enters the sum. That
discarding is why matching "throws away data," and why §8.3 (weighting) exists.

---

## 2. The six steps

### Step 1 — Feature engineering & the propensity model

**Snapshot the driver's universe at Thursday 11:59 PM**, before the banner
appears — you can never use post-treatment variables. Covariates $X$:
`historical_weekly_deliveries_avg`, `deliveries_last_7_days`, `driver_rating`,
`vehicle_type`, `days_since_last_dash`, and an intent proxy `app_opens_thursday`.

Fit `Model.fit(X, D)` — logistic regression or XGBoost — to get $e(X)$ for every
driver.

> **Do not overfit.** If AUC ≈ 0.99 the features perfectly separate opt-ins from
> opt-outs, so no control has a treated twin and matching drops everything. AUC
> ≈ **0.75** is the sweet spot ([§8.1 §4](./8.1-propensity-and-unconfoundedness.md#4-overlap-and-the-r--auc-illusion)).

### Step 2 — Enforce common support (overlap)

Plot mirrored histograms of $e(X)$ for treated vs. control, and trim where they
don't overlap:

- **Left tail:** control drivers with $e(X) = 0.01$ (haven't driven in 6
  months) — no treated driver is that low. **Drop them.**
- **Right tail:** treated drivers with $e(X) = 0.99$ (drive 80 hrs/week) — no
  control is that high. **Drop them.**

Trimming changes your estimand: you're now measuring the effect only for the
**marginal** drivers who realistically could have gone either way — not the
whole population.

### Step 3 — Caliper matching

For each treated driver, find the closest control by propensity — but enforce a
**caliper**: if the nearest control is more than ~**0.05** (standard deviations
of the logit of the propensity) away, **discard the treated driver.** A treated
0.80 matched to a control 0.60 is a bad twin. You **sacrifice sample size to buy
unconfoundedness** — a real trade, and the right one.

### Step 4 — The Love plot (prove balance)

Compute the **standardized mean difference** for every covariate:

```math
\text{SMD} = \frac{\bar{X}_{\text{treated}} - \bar{X}_{\text{control}}}{\sqrt{\left(S^2_{\text{treated}} + S^2_{\text{control}}\right)/2}}
```

Plot before vs. after matching (the "Love plot," after Thomas Love).
`historical_weekly_deliveries` might go from SMD **0.85** (pre) to **0.02**
(post). **Every covariate must be < 0.10 after matching.** One stuck at 0.15
means matching failed — back to Step 1.

**But remember the trap** ([§8.1 §5](./8.1-propensity-and-unconfoundedness.md#5-worked-probe-the-spotify-opt-in)):
a flawless Love plot proves balance on *observed* covariates only. It is silent
on the unobserved confounder. It's necessary, never sufficient.

### Step 5 — The ATT

With a balanced matched set, you've simulated an A/B test. Take the difference
in mean outcomes (§1):

```math
\hat{\tau}_{\text{ATT}} = \operatorname{mean}(Y_{\text{treated, matched}}) - \operatorname{mean}(Y_{\text{control, matched}}) = +3 \text{ deliveries}
```

### Step 6 — Sensitivity analysis (Rosenbaum bounds)

A junior ships Step 5. A staff DS knows the PM will ask: *"What if opt-in
drivers just desperately needed rent this week, and you didn't capture it?"*
Unconfoundedness is untestable — so you **bound** how badly it could fail.

**Rosenbaum's $\Gamma$** answers: *by what factor would an unmeasured confounder
have to multiply the odds of opting in to render the +3 effect insignificant?*

- $\Gamma = 1.1$ → the result dissolves under trivial hidden bias. **Fragile.**
- $\Gamma = 1.8$ → an unmeasured factor would have to make a driver 80% more
  likely to opt in *before* the effect breaks. **Reasonably robust.**
- $\Gamma = 3.0$ → rock solid.

**The final pitch:** *"The \$50 bonus causes +3 deliveries next week. The Love
plot shows the groups are matched on historical behavior, and the sensitivity
analysis ($\Gamma = 1.8$) shows the result survives an unmeasured confounder
that makes opt-in 80% more likely. We can attribute this lift to the
promotion."* Note what $\Gamma$ does *not* do: it doesn't prove there's no
confounder — it quantifies how strong one would have to be. That honesty is the
point.

## 3. Python

```python
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.neighbors import NearestNeighbors

# df: covariates X..., D (opt-in), Y (deliveries next week). SNAPSHOT pre-banner.

# Step 1 — propensity (check AUC ~0.75, not 0.99)
X = df[covariates]
df["ps"] = LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000).fit(X, df["D"]).predict_proba(X)[:, 1]

# Step 2 — common support: trim to the overlapping propensity range
lo = max(df.loc[df.D==1,"ps"].min(), df.loc[df.D==0,"ps"].min())
hi = min(df.loc[df.D==1,"ps"].max(), df.loc[df.D==0,"ps"].max())
df = df[df.ps.between(lo, hi)]

# Step 3 — caliper matching on the LOGIT of the propensity (nearest neighbor)
logit = lambda p: np.log(p/(1-p))
treated, control = df[df.D==1].copy(), df[df.D==0].copy()
caliper = 0.05 * logit(df.ps).std()
nn = NearestNeighbors(n_neighbors=1).fit(logit(control.ps).values.reshape(-1,1))
dist, idx = nn.kneighbors(logit(treated.ps).values.reshape(-1,1))
keep = dist.ravel() <= caliper                     # discard treated with no close twin
matched_t = treated[keep]
matched_c = control.iloc[idx.ravel()[keep]]

# Step 4 — Love plot: SMD per covariate, must be < 0.10 after matching
def smd(a, b):
    return (a.mean()-b.mean())/np.sqrt((a.var()+b.var())/2)
for c in covariates:
    print(f"{c:32s} SMD after = {smd(matched_t[c], matched_c[c]):+.3f}")

# Step 5 — ATT
att = matched_t["Y"].mean() - matched_c["Y"].mean()
print(f"ATT = {att:.2f} deliveries")

# Step 6 — Rosenbaum bounds (production: `rbounds` in R, or `scipy`-based impls)
```

## 4. Common misreadings

**"PSM gives the ATE."**
It gives the **ATT** — the effect on the treated — and only for the *matched*
treated units after trimming. Unmatched controls are discarded. §1.

**"Match on the raw propensity."**
Match on the **logit** of the propensity; the caliper is in logit-SD units.
Raw-probability distances compress near 0 and 1. §Step 3.

**"A tighter caliper is always better."**
Tighter = better balance but fewer matches (less power, narrower estimand).
It's a bias-variance trade, not a free lunch. §Step 3.

**"Love plot balanced → done."**
Balanced on *observed* covariates only. Run Rosenbaum bounds and argue
unconfoundedness qualitatively. §Step 4, §Step 6.

**"A big $\Gamma$ proves there's no confounder."**
$\Gamma$ measures how *strong* a hidden confounder would need to be to overturn
the result — not that none exists. It's a robustness statement, not a proof.
§Step 6.

**"Use post-treatment features if they predict opt-in well."**
Never. Snapshot strictly pre-treatment. A post-banner variable is contaminated
by the treatment itself. §Step 1.
