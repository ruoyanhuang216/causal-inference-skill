# 8.5 — Advanced Matching & Weighting (Beyond PSM)

> **Part of:** [Lecture 8 — Selection on Observables](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§8.2 PSM](./8.2-propensity-score-matching.md) and [§8.3 IPW](./8.3-inverse-probability-weighting.md).

Propensity score matching is the famous method, but modern econometrics has
turned on it — most sharply in Gary King & Richard Nielsen's *"Why Propensity
Scores Should Not Be Used for Matching"* (2019). The core critique: **PSM
compresses all covariate information into a single scalar** (the probability),
and two units with the same propensity can still differ wildly on the actual
covariates. Matching on that scalar can therefore *increase* imbalance on the
things you care about — the "PSM paradox." This chapter is the three
alternatives a staff DS should reach for, and exactly when.

---

## 1. Coarsened Exact Matching (CEM)

**Philosophy.** Exact matching (a truly identical twin) is impossible on
continuous data. But group the continuous variables into meaningful,
human-readable **buckets** (coarsen), and *then* exact-match on the buckets.

**Mechanics:**
1. **Coarsen** each covariate into bins — Age into [18–25], [26–35], [36–45];
   income into \$20k bands.
2. **Grid.** Form the multidimensional grid of bin combinations.
3. **Place** every treated and control unit into its cell.
4. **Prune.** Any cell containing *only* treated or *only* control units → both
   discarded. Only mixed cells survive.
5. **Weight.** Within surviving cells, weight controls so their count matches
   the treated count in that cell.

**The mathematical advantage — Monotonic Imbalance Bounding (MIB).** In PSM,
tweaking the model to fix Age imbalance can *ruin* Income balance — the
covariates are coupled through the propensity model. In CEM they're
**decoupled**: the maximum imbalance on each covariate is bounded by *its own
bin width*, set ex ante. Tighten the Age bins and you improve Age balance
**without touching** Income. Balance is a design choice, not a fitting outcome.

**The catch — the curse of dimensionality prunes your sample.** Exact-cell
matching needs a control in *every* treated unit's cell, and cells multiply
exponentially with covariates. *Verified* (code in §6), 500 treated / 10,000
control:

```
   4 coarsened covariates:  500/500 treated survive  (100%)
   8 coarsened covariates:   68/500 survive          ( 14%)
  12+ coarsened covariates:   0/500 survive          (  0%)
```

**So CEM on all 40 continuous metrics is infeasible — it prunes to nothing.**
The practical move is to coarsen only the *few* dimensions that matter, accept
that outlier/rare-combination treated units get pruned, and **report the
narrowed estimand**: you're now measuring the ATT for the "standard" units that
had twins, not the whole treated population.

**When to use it:** interpretability is paramount. You can tell a PM, *"every
treated user is matched to a control in the exact same age bracket, state, and
income band."*

## 2. Mahalanobis Distance Matching

**Philosophy.** Skip the propensity model entirely. Just measure the literal
*statistical distance* between two units' feature vectors and match nearest.

**The math.** The Mahalanobis distance between treated $x_i$ and control $x_j$:

```math
D(x_i, x_j) = \sqrt{(x_i - x_j)^{\top} \, \Sigma^{-1} \, (x_i - x_j)}
```

where $\Sigma^{-1}$ is the inverse covariance matrix of the covariates.

**Why $\Sigma^{-1}$?** Two reasons, both fatal to plain Euclidean distance:
- **Scale.** Account balance (thousands) would dominate age (tens) under raw
  Euclidean distance. $\Sigma^{-1}$ standardizes every covariate to unit
  variance, so each contributes comparably.
- **Correlation.** If "logins" and "time in app" are highly correlated,
  Euclidean distance **double-counts** that shared signal. $\Sigma^{-1}$
  de-correlates the space, measuring true statistical distance rather than
  redundant nominal distance.

**When to use it:** a **small number of continuous covariates** ($\lesssim 8$).
**When it fails:** the curse of dimensionality — with 50 covariates or many
categorical dummies, all pairwise distances converge and become meaningless
noise (everything is roughly equidistant in high dimensions).

## 3. Entropy Balancing (the modern weighting standard)

**Philosophy.** The worst part of PSM/IPW is the **iterative manual labor**: fit
a logit, check the Love plot, spot a variance imbalance, add a polynomial term,
re-run, re-check — for days. Entropy balancing solves it **backwards**: you
*declare the balance you demand*, and it solves directly for weights that
achieve it.

**Mechanics.** State the constraints — *"the weighted control group must have
the exact same mean (and variance, skewness) as the treated group on all 50
covariates"* — and solve for the control weights $w_i$ that:
- **(a)** satisfy the balance constraints exactly:
  $\sum_i w_i X_{i,\text{control}} = \bar{X}_{\text{treated}}$ for each moment;
- **(b)** stay **as close to uniform as possible** — maximize entropy /
  minimize KL divergence from equal weights — so no single unit gets an absurd
  weight of 1000.

**The math** is a convex optimization; the dual gives weights of the form
$w_i \propto \exp(\lambda^{\top} X_i)$, and you solve for the multipliers
$\lambda$ that hit the target moments. Convex ⇒ a unique global solution, no
tuning.

**The advantage — balance by construction.** You never check a Love plot,
because the SMD is *mathematically guaranteed* to be ~0. *Verified* (§6): with
treated and control shifted apart, entropy-balanced control means match the
treated means to **~1e-6 on every covariate.** Exact, deterministic, first try.

**When to use it:** **automated pipelines.** Because it's a deterministic
convex solve with no iterative checking, it drops into a production Airflow job
and will never silently return an imbalanced dataset. Fire-and-forget.

---

## 4. Choosing among them

| Method | Balance mechanism | Best when | Fails when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PSM** (§8.2) | match on scalar $e(X)$ | a baseline / familiar audience | can *increase* covariate imbalance (King-Nielsen) |
| **CEM** | coarsen + exact-match on bins | interpretability, a few key dims, stakeholder-verifiable | many covariates → prunes to nothing (§1) |
| **Mahalanobis** | statistical distance, $\Sigma^{-1}$ | ≤ 8 continuous covariates | high dimensions / many dummies |
| **Entropy balancing** | constrained optimization | automated pipelines; exact moment balance | you need interpretable 1-to-1 pairs |

## 5. Worked probe: the B2B SaaS discount

**Setup.** A 20% renewal discount went to **500** enterprise clients; **10,000**
renewed at full price (control). Measure the effect on next-year expansion
revenue $Y$. Two VPs impose conflicting constraints.

### 5.1 Challenge A — the Engineering VP's pipeline

*"Run this every month as an automated batch job. We have 40 continuous
metrics. We can't have a DS manually tweaking a propensity model each month to
get balance."*

**Answer: Entropy Balancing — the only acceptable choice.** PSM and CEM require
iterative human babysitting (check the Love plot, tweak bins, add polynomial
terms). Entropy balancing frames matching as a **deterministic convex
optimization**: feed it the data, it forces the control moments to equal the
treated moments exactly, every run. It's fire-and-forget — it will never break
the Airflow DAG or silently emit an imbalanced dataset, because balance is
guaranteed by construction (§3), not verified after the fact.

### 5.2 Challenge B — the Sales VP's spreadsheet

*"No black boxes or fractional weights. For every discounted company, show me
ONE non-discounted company in the exact same Industry, Billing Tier, and
Employee-Count band."*

**Answer: Coarsened Exact Matching — the only method producing visibly
verifiable 1-to-1 pairs.** Coarsen exactly the three business dimensions the VP
named, place clients into that grid, and pair them within cells. The VP can
open the spreadsheet and confirm each pair shares the exact same tier and
industry.

**The catch you must state up front — the sample drops, and the estimand
narrows.** CEM ruthlessly prunes any treated client whose cell has no control
twin. An `[Enterprise, 10,000+ employees, Manufacturing]` client with no
control match is *dropped*. How much you lose depends on how many dimensions
you demand and how the treated distribute (§1's verified range: on a few coarse
dims you keep most; push to 8+ and it collapses to ~14%). The honest pitch:

> *"I can give you the exact 1-to-1 spreadsheet, and the causal signal will be
> pure. But we're no longer measuring the discount's ROI for the **whole**
> business — only for the 'standard' companies that had identical twins. The
> outlier clients get pruned, so we lose the ability to generalize to our
> extreme accounts. Purity of match trades against generalizability."*

**The meta-lesson of the probe:** there is no single best method — the
*constraint* picks the method. Automation → entropy balancing. Stakeholder-
verifiable pairs → CEM. A method choice is a response to who has to trust the
answer and how it will run.

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize

# ---- Entropy Balancing: exact moment balance by construction ----
def entropy_balance(X_control, X_treated_target_moments):
    """Weights on controls so their weighted mean == treated mean, exactly.
    Dual: w_i prop exp(lambda' X_i); minimize log-sum-exp of lambda'(X_i - target)."""
    Z = X_control - X_treated_target_moments          # (n_c, p)
    def obj(lam):
        a = Z @ lam; m = a.max()
        return m + np.log(np.exp(a - m).sum())
    def grad(lam):
        a = Z @ lam; w = np.exp(a - a.max()); w /= w.sum()
        return Z.T @ w
    lam = minimize(obj, np.zeros(Z.shape[1]), jac=grad, method="BFGS").x
    w = np.exp(Z @ lam - (Z @ lam).max()); w /= w.sum()
    return w                                            # weighted control mean == target

w = entropy_balance(X_control, X_treated.mean(axis=0))
print("max moment gap:", np.abs((w[:,None]*X_control).sum(0) - X_treated.mean(0)).max())  # ~1e-6

# ---- CEM: coarsen -> exact-match on cells -> prune (production: `cem` in R) ----
def cem_match(X_t, X_c, nbins):
    edges = [np.quantile(np.vstack([X_t, X_c])[:,j], np.linspace(0,1,nbins+1)) for j in range(X_t.shape[1])]
    cell  = lambda X: [tuple(np.clip(np.digitize(X[i,j], edges[j][1:-1]),0,nbins-1)
                             for j in range(X.shape[1])) for i in range(len(X))]
    control_cells = set(cell(X_c))
    keep = [c in control_cells for c in cell(X_t)]     # treated with a control in-cell
    return np.array(keep)                              # False = pruned (curse of dimensionality)

# ---- Mahalanobis distance (production: MatchIt in R, or scipy.spatial) ----
Sigma_inv = np.linalg.inv(np.cov(np.vstack([X_t, X_c]).T))
def mahalanobis(xi, xj):
    d = xi - xj
    return np.sqrt(d @ Sigma_inv @ d)
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"PSM is the gold standard for matching."**
It compresses covariates to a scalar and can *increase* imbalance (King-Nielsen
2019). CEM, Mahalanobis, and entropy balancing are often better. Intro, §4.

**"CEM gives you exact matches for free."**
Exact matches cost sample: every covariate you coarsen multiplies the cells, and
past a handful of dimensions the pruning is catastrophic (14% at 8 covariates,
0% at 12). Coarsen few dims and report the drop. §1.

**"Mahalanobis is better than PSM, so use it everywhere."**
Only with ≤ ~8 continuous covariates. In high dimensions all distances collapse
and it's noise. §2.

**"Entropy balancing is a black box."**
It's a *deterministic convex* optimization — the opposite of a black box. It's
more transparent than PSM's iterate-and-check loop, and its balance is provable,
not hoped-for. §3, §5.1.

**"These methods fix unobserved confounding."**
None of them — they're all selection-on-observables. Better balance on
*observed* covariates; an unobserved confounder breaks every one of them exactly
as in [§8.1](./8.1-propensity-and-unconfoundedness.md). The golden rule still
holds.

**"Balance by construction means the causal estimate is valid."**
Entropy balancing guarantees balance on the moments you *specified*, of the
covariates you *have*. It says nothing about unspecified moments or unobserved
confounders.
