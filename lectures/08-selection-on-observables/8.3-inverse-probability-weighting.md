# 8.3 — Inverse Probability Weighting

> **Part of:** [Lecture 8 — Selection on Observables](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§8.2](./8.2-propensity-score-matching.md) — matching, which IPW improves on by *not* throwing data away.

Matching discards every unmatched control (§8.2 §1). **IPW uses all the data**
by reweighting it into a *pseudo-population* where treatment is independent of
the covariates — as if it had been randomized.

---

## 1. The idea: build a pseudo-population by reweighting

If treated units are *rare* in some region of covariate space, each one stands
in for many similar untreated units — so **up-weight the rare, down-weight the
common.** Weighting each observation by the inverse of its probability of the
treatment it received creates a synthetic sample in which $D \perp X$, and a
simple weighted difference in means becomes causal.

The unit of analysis is the **individual observation.** Every user $i$ gets a
weight $W_i$ from their *actual* treatment $D_i$ and their propensity $e(X_i)$.

---

## 2. The weights — and the ATT-vs-ATE question you asked

The weights differ by *which population you're simulating*, and this is the
crux of your question.

### For the ATE (effect if you treated *everyone* vs. *no one*)

You reweight **both** arms to look like the **whole population**:

```math
W_i = \frac{1}{e(X_i)} \ \text{ if treated}, \qquad W_i = \frac{1}{1 - e(X_i)} \ \text{ if control}
```

A treated user who was *unlikely* to be treated ($e = 0.1$) is rare and gets a
big weight (10); they represent all the similar users who *weren't* treated.

### For the ATT (effect on the *treated* population)

You leave the treated arm **as-is** and reweight only the controls to *resemble
the treated*:

```math
W_i = 1 \ \text{ if treated}, \qquad W_i = \frac{e(X_i)}{1 - e(X_i)} \ \text{ if control}
```

**Why is the treated weight exactly 1 for the ATT but $1/e(X)$ for the ATE?**
Because of *which population you're simulating*:

- **ATE** asks about the *whole* population. Neither arm, as observed, looks
  like the whole population — the treated arm over-represents high-propensity
  users. So you must reweight the treated arm *too*, by $1/e(X)$, to recover the
  full-population average. Both arms get bent toward the population.
- **ATT** asks only about the *treated* population. The treated arm **already
  is** that population — no reweighting needed, so weight = 1. You only need to
  make the *controls* look like the treated, which is what
  $\frac{e(X)}{1-e(X)}$ does (it up-weights controls who *look* treated, i.e.
  have high $e(X)$).

**The rule:** the group that *is already* your target population keeps weight 1;
the other group gets reweighted toward it. For the ATT the treated are the
target; for the ATE both arms are reweighted toward the population.

---

## 3. The estimand at the unit level

The **Horvitz-Thompson** IPW estimator for the ATE:

```math
\hat{\tau}_{\text{ATE}}^{\text{IPW}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \frac{D_i Y_i}{e(X_i)} \; - \; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \frac{(1 - D_i) Y_i}{1 - e(X_i)}
```

Read it per observation: a treated user contributes $Y_i / e(X_i)$ to the
treated average; a control contributes $Y_i / (1 - e(X_i))$ to the control
average; the difference is the effect.

**A refinement you should always use:** the **Hájek** (self-normalized)
estimator divides each arm by the *sum of its weights* instead of by $N$. It's
still consistent, has lower variance, and is bounded within the range of $Y$ —
the raw Horvitz-Thompson estimator above can wander outside it when weights are
extreme.

---

## 4. The variance danger

Look at the denominators. A treated user with $e(X) = 0.01$ gets weight $1/0.01
= 100$; a control at $e(X) = 0.99$ gets $1/(1-0.99) = 100$; at $0.999$, **1000**.
Their single outcome is multiplied by that weight and can **dominate the entire
estimate.** IPW is *unbiased* (if the propensity is right) but can have enormous
**variance** — the extreme tails explode your standard errors and destroy
power.

Worse, it's **hyper-sensitive to the propensity model.** If your XGBoost
predicted $0.991$ instead of $0.999$, the weight drops from 1000 to 111 — a
9× swing in one user's influence from a rounding-level change in $\hat e$. That
fragility is disqualifying for a production estimate.

This is the same overlap problem from
[§8.1 §4](./8.1-propensity-and-unconfoundedness.md#4-overlap-and-the-r--auc-illusion),
now expressed as variance. There are three fixes, and **each changes what
you're estimating** — so the choice is as much about the *question* as the
variance.

---

## 5. Taming the tails — trimming, overlap weights, and the ATO

### 5.1 Trimming (the brute-force fix)

If the tails break the math, cut them off. Drop every unit with $e(X) < 0.1$ or
$> 0.9$ (a common bound), or Winsorize the weights.

It stabilizes the variance beautifully — but it **changes the estimand.** You're
no longer estimating the ATE for the whole population; you're estimating it for
the *moderate middle*. You must say so out loud: *"this excludes our most loyal
and least engaged users."* And the threshold (0.1? 0.05?) is arbitrary, which
makes the estimand arbitrary too.

*(Stabilized weights — multiply treated by $P(D{=}1)$, control by $P(D{=}0)$ —
keep the ATE and tame the weights somewhat, but don't bound them.)*

### 5.2 Overlap weights (the elegant fix)

Li, Morgan & Zaslavsky (2018): instead of an arbitrary hard cutoff, apply a
*smooth* function that naturally down-weights the extremes. Multiply the ATE
weights by the **variance of treatment assignment**, $e(X)(1 - e(X))$. Watch the
algebra cancel:

```math
\text{Treated: } W_1 = \frac{1}{e(X)} \cdot e(X)(1 - e(X)) = 1 - e(X)
```

```math
\text{Control: } W_0 = \frac{1}{1 - e(X)} \cdot e(X)(1 - e(X)) = e(X)
```

The weights are now $1 - e(X)$ and $e(X)$ — **strictly bounded in $[0, 1]$.**
They can never explode. *Verified* (code in §6): under extreme propensities
where IPW's max weight hits **1000**, the overlap weights top out at **0.999**,
and the estimator's variance is **54× smaller** than IPW's.

The weighting also has a beautiful interpretation — the **region of
equipoise:**

- **A "hard yes" user** ($e = 0.99$), if treated, gets weight $1 - 0.99 =
  \mathbf{0.01}$. *"We already knew they'd take it — we learn almost nothing
  from them. Ignore them."*
- **A "coin toss" user** ($e = 0.50$), if treated, gets weight $1 - 0.50 =
  \mathbf{0.50}$ — the maximum. *"A perfect 50/50 split: clinical equipoise.
  Their behavior is the purest causal signal. Maximize their weight."*

### 5.3 The estimand shift — the ATO

Overlap weights, like trimming, change the target — but to something *principled*
rather than arbitrary. You're now estimating the **ATO — Average Treatment
effect on the Overlap population:** the effect for the users **most on the
fence** about the treatment.

And this is genuinely a different number. *Verified* (§6): with an effect that
rises across users and the equipoise population sitting away from the population
average, the true **ATE is 2.00** while the true **ATO is 2.89** — different
questions, different answers. Crucially, **the ATO is often exactly what a PM
wants:** *"if we push our marginal, undecided users to adopt, what's the ROI?"*
The whole-population ATE is a fiction here anyway (you'd never force your
never-adopters to adopt); the effect on the persuadable middle is the decision-
relevant one.

### 5.4 The menu

| Target | Weights (treated / control) | What it answers | Weight behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ATE** | $1/e$ / $1/(1-e)$ | force everyone vs. no one | explodes at the tails |
| **ATT** | $1$ / $e/(1-e)$ | effect on the treated | explodes at $e \to 1$ |
| **ATO** | $1-e$ / $e$ | effect on the persuadable middle | **bounded in $[0,1]$** |

**Trimming and overlap weights fix the variance by *changing the estimand*.
AIPW (§8.4) fixes it while *keeping* the ATE** — it applies the big weights to
*residuals* instead of raw outcomes. If you specifically need the ATE, reach for
AIPW; if the equipoise population is the right target (and it often is), overlap
weights are the more elegant tool.

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np

# df: D (0/1), Y (outcome), ps (estimated propensity, TRIMMED to overlap)
e = df["ps"].clip(0.05, 0.95)      # trim extreme scores first
D, Y = df["D"].values, df["Y"].values

# ---- ATE weights: reweight BOTH arms to the population ----
w_ate = np.where(D == 1, 1/e, 1/(1-e))

# ---- ATT weights: treated = 1, controls reweighted toward the treated ----
w_att = np.where(D == 1, 1.0, e/(1-e))

# ---- ATO (overlap) weights: treated = 1-e, control = e. BOUNDED in [0,1]. ----
w_ato = np.where(D == 1, 1-e, e)                  # never explodes -- no trimming needed

# ---- Hajek (self-normalized) estimator; swap w_ate -> w_ato for the ATO ----
def hajek(w):
    t = (w * D * Y).sum() / (w * D).sum()
    c = (w * (1-D) * Y).sum() / (w * (1-D)).sum()
    return t - c
print(f"IPW ATE (Hajek) = {hajek(w_ate):.3f}")
print(f"ATO (overlap)   = {hajek(w_ato):.3f}")    # effect on the persuadable middle

# ---- Inspect the weights BEFORE trusting the estimate ----
print(f"max IPW weight     = {w_ate.max():.0f}")  # 100+ => overlap problem
print(f"max overlap weight = {w_ato.max():.3f}")  # <= 1 by construction
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"IPW and matching give the same thing."**
Matching discards unmatched controls and targets the ATT; IPW uses everyone and
can target the ATE, ATT, or ATO via the weights. §1–2, §5.

**"The treated always get weight $1/e(X)$."**
Only for the **ATE**. For the **ATT** the treated keep weight 1; for the **ATO**
they get $1 - e(X)$. §2, §5.

**"IPW is unbiased, so it's safe."**
Unbiased ≠ low variance. One user at $e = 0.99$ can blow up the estimate and the
SEs — and a rounding-level change in $\hat e$ swings their weight 9×. §4.

**"Trimming and overlap weights are two ways to get the same ATE."**
Neither gives the ATE. Trimming gives an *arbitrary* overlap-region estimand
(depends on the cutoff); overlap weights give the *principled* **ATO** (the
persuadable middle). If you truly need the ATE, use AIPW. §5.

**"The ATO is just a variance trick."**
It's a *different estimand* — the effect on the on-the-fence population — and
it's often the one the business actually wants ("what's the ROI of nudging the
undecided?"). Verified to differ from the ATE (2.89 vs 2.00). §5.3.

**"Overlap weights need trimming too."**
No — $1 - e$ and $e$ are already bounded in $[0,1]$. The whole point is that no
cutoff is needed. §5.2.

**"Use the Horvitz-Thompson estimator by default."**
Prefer **Hájek** (self-normalized): same consistency, lower variance, stays
within the outcome range. §3.
