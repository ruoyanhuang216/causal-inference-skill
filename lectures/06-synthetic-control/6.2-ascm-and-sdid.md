## 1. The problem both methods solve

Classic SCM has a hard wall: the convex-hull constraint ($\sum w_j = 1$,
$w_j \ge 0$) means the synthetic unit is a weighted *average* of the donors, so
**it cannot extrapolate beyond them.** In tech this bites constantly — your
treated market is a giant. California, New York, the whole US. If the treated
unit sits *above every donor* in baseline level, classic SCM **flatlines**: no
convex combination can reach it, the pre-fit has a persistent gap, and the
post-period "effect" is contaminated by that gap.

*Verified* (code in §5). New York with a **+20,000** baseline level above the
entire donor pool, true effect **500**:

```
  Classic SCM: pre-period gap = 17,131   <- cannot close (convex hull can't extrapolate)
               effect estimate = 17,184  <- garbage: the +20,000 offset IS the "effect"
```

The estimate is 34× the truth. **ASCM and SDID are two different escapes from
this wall** — and choosing between them is a genuine architectural decision
(§4).

---

## 2. Augmented SCM (ASCM) — patch the gap with ML

**Ben-Michael, Feller & Rothstein (2021).** ASCM keeps SCM's weights but *adds
a bias-correction term* to close the residual pre-fit gap:

1. Compute the standard SCM weights $\hat{w}$.
2. Because the treated unit is an outlier, the pre-fit has a gap (bias).
3. Run a **ridge regression** on the covariates to estimate that bias, and
   **add the correction back** to the synthetic counterfactual.

```math
\hat{Y}^{\text{ASCM}}_{1,t} = \underbrace{\sum_j \hat{w}_j Y_{j,t}}_{\text{SCM}} + \underbrace{\hat{\eta}\!\left(X_{1} - \textstyle\sum_j \hat{w}_j X_{j}\right)}_{\text{ridge bias-correction}}
```

The ridge term lets the counterfactual **break out of the convex hull** and
extrapolate to the outlier, giving a near-perfect pre-fit. ASCM is **always at
least as good as SCM** in pre-fit — it *nests* it (zero correction = classic
SCM).

**The danger — and it's the trap in §4:** the escape is *extrapolation*. ASCM
uses a linear model to reach a region of the outcome space where you have **no
donor support**. If the covariate-outcome relationship isn't linear out at that
extreme scale, the extrapolation produces garbage. ASCM is right when the pre-
fit gap is *modest* and the extrapolation is *short*. It is dangerous when
you're asking ridge to bridge a chasm.

---

## 3. Synthetic DiD (SDID) — absorb the gap with a fixed effect

**Arkhangelsky, Athey, Hirshberg, Imbens & Wager (2021).** SDID's insight is
that the two workhorses each fix half the problem:

- **DiD** equally-weights controls (crude) but handles baseline level
  differences perfectly, via **unit fixed effects** (intercept shifts).
- **SCM** brilliantly weights controls but treats all pre-periods equally when
  fitting.

SDID takes the best of both. It applies **two** sets of weights:

- **Unit weights $\omega_j$** (like SCM) — find donors whose pre-trend *shape*
  matches the treated unit.
- **Time weights $\lambda_t$** — find the weighted average of *past periods*
  that best predicts the post-period, so volatile or irrelevant early history
  gets downweighted (feature launches in December → November matters, last
  January barely does).

Then it runs the doubly-weighted panel through a **two-way fixed-effects**
regression. **The unit fixed effect absorbs any constant baseline gap** — no
extrapolation required.

```math
\hat{\tau}^{\text{SDID}} = \arg\min_{\tau, \mu, \alpha, \beta} \sum_{i,t} \left(Y_{it} - \mu - \alpha_i - \beta_t - \tau D_{it}\right)^2 \; \hat{\omega}_i \, \hat{\lambda}_t
```

The $\alpha_i$ (unit FE) is the whole trick: it says *"I acknowledge synthetic
New York starts \$20,000 lower; I subtract that out and measure only the change
in post-launch trajectory."* SDID is doubly robust — it survives an imperfect
*level* match (the FE handles it) **and** volatile pre-trends (the time weights
handle it). It's arguably the most robust observational panel estimator in
common use.

## 4. Worked probe: the New York supply-chain launch

**Setup.** You launch a supply-chain overhaul in **New York**, with 40 states
as donors. Classic SCM's pre-fit is terrible — the synthetic line sits a
constant **\$20,000 below** the true New York line for the entire pre-period. A
junior DS says:

> *"Classic SCM failed because New York is too big. Pivot to ASCM — the ridge
> regression will extrapolate and push the synthetic line up \$20,000 to match
> New York perfectly."*

### 4.1 Is the junior right about ASCM? — No, and it's dangerous

They're *mechanically* correct that ASCM uses ridge to break the convex hull.
But relying on it to bridge a **\$20,000** gap is a serious error:

- **The structural flaw:** ASCM would use a *linear* model to extrapolate far
  outside the donor pool's support to reach New York's scale. If the
  covariate-outcome relationship isn't perfectly linear at that extreme, the
  extrapolation is garbage — you're using ML to *guess* what happens at a scale
  you have **zero historical data for**.
- **The tell:** if the \$20,000 is a pure *baseline level* difference (New York
  is just bigger), forcing ridge to synthesize it from covariates is the wrong
  tool. You don't need to *extrapolate* a level gap — you need to *subtract* it.

### 4.2 How SDID handles it structurally — the intercept shift

SDID handles the gap *effortlessly*, via the **unit fixed effect $\alpha_i$**,
with no risky extrapolation:

- SDID uses SCM-style weights to match the *shape* (pre-trend) of the donors to
  New York. In this scenario the shapes already match — they're parallel lines
  separated by \$20,000.
- Then the DiD engine's unit FE **absorbs the constant \$20,000**. It measures
  strictly the *change in post-launch trajectory*, netting out the baseline.

*Verified* (§5): with New York \$20,000 above the hull and a true effect of
**500**, de-meaning each series first (the fixed effect) and then fitting
weights on the *shape* gives a clean pre-fit (gap ≈ 0) and recovers
**496.6** — versus classic SCM's biased **17,184**.

### 4.3 The whiteboard answer

> *"The junior is right that ASCM can break the convex hull, but wrong to trust
> it here. ASCM would ask a linear ridge model to extrapolate \$20,000 outside
> the donor support — fragile if the relationship bends at scale. If the gap is
> a pure baseline level difference, SDID is the right tool: SCM weights match
> the pre-trend *shape*, and the DiD unit fixed effect subtracts the \$20,000
> intercept outright. **Extrapolation (ASCM) vs. intercept shift (SDID)** — and
> for a parallel-gap outlier, the intercept shift is strictly safer."*

Being able to draw **linear extrapolation vs. fixed-effect intercept shift** on
a whiteboard is what separates "I import `from pysynth import ascm`" from "I
know the linear algebra underneath."

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize

def convex_fit(y_pre, D_pre):
    J = D_pre.shape[1]
    return minimize(lambda w: np.sum((y_pre - D_pre @ w) ** 2), np.full(J, 1/J),
                    bounds=[(0, 1)] * J,
                    constraints=[{"type": "eq", "fun": lambda w: w.sum() - 1}],
                    method="SLSQP").x

# ---- Classic SCM on a level-outlier treated unit: FLATLINES ----
w = convex_fit(NY[:T_pre], donors[:T_pre])
synth = donors @ w
print(f"classic SCM effect = {(NY[T_pre:] - synth[T_pre:]).mean():,.0f}  (biased by the level gap)")

# ---- SDID essence: DE-MEAN each series first (the unit fixed effect), ----
# ---- THEN fit weights on the shape, THEN read the post-vs-pre gap. ----
ny_dm  = NY - NY[:T_pre].mean()                      # unit FE for the treated
don_dm = donors - donors[:T_pre].mean(axis=0)        # unit FE for each donor
w_sdid = convex_fit(ny_dm[:T_pre], don_dm[:T_pre])   # weights on SHAPE, not level
synth_dm = don_dm @ w_sdid
sdid = (ny_dm[T_pre:] - synth_dm[T_pre:]).mean()
print(f"SDID effect = {sdid:,.1f}   (intercept shift absorbed the level gap)")
# classic SCM effect = 17,184 ; SDID effect = 496.6 (true 500)

# ---- Production ----
# SDID : synthdid (R), pysynthdid (Python)  -- includes time weights + jackknife SE
# ASCM : augsynth (R)                        -- ridge-augmented, with CV for the penalty
```

## 6. Common misreadings

**"ASCM is strictly better than SCM, so always use it."**
ASCM is strictly better in *pre-fit*, but that fit can come from **dangerous
extrapolation**. Better in-sample fit ≠ better counterfactual. §2, §4.1.

**"A level outlier just needs a bigger/better donor pool."**
If *no* donor is as big as the treated unit, no convex combination reaches it —
that's the wall. You need extrapolation (ASCM) or a fixed effect (SDID), not
more donors. §1.

**"SDID is just SCM with extra steps."**
The extra steps are the point: the *unit fixed effect* handles level gaps SCM
can't, and the *time weights* handle volatile pre-trends. That's why it's
doubly robust. §3.

**"Use ASCM for a baseline level gap."**
A pure level gap should be *subtracted* (SDID's FE), not *extrapolated* (ASCM's
ridge). ASCM shines when the gap is a genuine covariate-shape mismatch, not a
constant offset. §4.

**"SDID needs a perfect pre-fit like SCM."**
No — the FE tolerates an imperfect level match and the time weights tolerate
volatile trends. That's the whole advantage. §3.
