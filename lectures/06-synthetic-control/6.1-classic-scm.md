# 6.1 — Classic Synthetic Control

> **Part of:** [Lecture 6 — Synthetic Control & Counterfactual Estimators](./README.md)
> **Builds on:** [Lecture 3 §3.1 — Geo-DiD](../03-difference-in-differences/3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md). SCM is what geo-DiD becomes when you stop averaging the controls equally.

---

## 1. The core philosophy

Geo-DiD (Lecture 3 §3.1) averages the control cities *equally*, and if that
average drifts from the treated city's pre-trend, parallel trends is dead on
arrival. Launch in New York growing 5% a month, control average growing 2%,
and your DiD counts the 3% gap as an effect that isn't there.

**Synthetic control's move:** stop averaging equally. Instead, *learn*
weights on the donor cities so the weighted blend tracks New York's
pre-treatment trajectory as closely as possible. Maybe

```math
\widehat{\text{NY}} = 0.59 \cdot \text{Boston} + 0.41 \cdot \text{Austin} + 0 \cdot \text{Seattle} + 0 \cdot \text{Denver}
```

reproduces New York's pre-period almost exactly. Then you use that blend as
the counterfactual. **It reframes control-group construction as an
optimization problem: find the weights that force the pre-trends parallel** —
rather than assuming parallel trends and hoping.

Geo-DiD is the special case where every weight is pinned to $1/J$. Synthetic
control frees the weights. That's the entire idea; the rest is mechanics —
and the modern extensions (§6.2 ASCM/SDID, §6.3 Matrix Completion) are all
answers to *"what if even the weighted fit isn't good enough?"*

## 2. The method in five steps

### Step 1 — Define the target and the donor pool

- **Treated unit:** $Y_1$ (New York).
- **Donor pool:** $J$ untreated units $Y_2, \dots, Y_{J+1}$ (Boston, Austin,
  Seattle, Denver, …).
- **Time:** $T_{\text{pre}}$ periods before treatment, $T_{\text{post}}$ after.

The donor pool must be **clean**: no donor can have been treated, and none can
be contaminated by spillover from the treated unit (SUTVA again). A donor that
got a smaller version of the same intervention poisons the counterfactual.

### Step 2 — Learn the weights (the optimization)

Find weights $W = (w_2, \dots, w_{J+1})$ minimizing the pre-period fit error
between New York and the synthetic blend:

```math
\min_{W} \; \sum_{t=1}^{T_{\text{pre}}} \left( Y_{1,t} - \sum_{j=2}^{J+1} w_j Y_{j,t} \right)^2
```

subject to the **classic Abadie constraints**:

```math
w_j \geq 0 \quad \text{(no shorting a city)} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \sum_{j} w_j = 1 \quad \text{(convex hull)}
```

These two constraints do real conceptual work:

- **Non-negativity** forbids a city contributing *negatively*. You can't build
  "New York" by subtracting Seattle. This keeps the synthetic unit
  interpretable and inside the space of real cities.
- **Sum-to-one (convex hull)** means the synthetic unit is a weighted
  *average*, so it can never extrapolate outside the range of the donors. If
  New York is bigger than every donor city, no convex combination can match it
  — and that failure is a *feature*: it tells you honestly that your donor pool
  can't reproduce the treated unit, rather than silently extrapolating. **This
  exact limitation is what ASCM and SDID exist to solve (§6.2).**

The convex-hull constraint also induces **sparsity**: the optimizer typically
lands on a handful of nonzero weights and zeroes the rest, which is why
synthetic controls are interpretable ("60% Boston, 40% Austin") where a ridge
regression would smear small weights across all donors.

**Industry note.** With hundreds of donors, the strict constraints overfit the
pre-period. Modern practice relaxes them: **ridge / L2 penalty** (allows small
negative weights, trades interpretability for stability), or **Bayesian
structural time series** with spike-and-slab priors (Google's CausalImpact —
see [Lecture 2 §2.4](../02-experimentation/2.4-geo-experiments.md#4-estimation)).
The bias-variance trade is real: strict constraints = interpretable but
overfit-prone; relaxed = stable but you can no longer say "60% Boston."

### Step 3 — Freeze the weights, build the counterfactual

The weights were learned on the **pre-period only**. Freeze them and project
forward:

```math
\hat{Y}_{1,t}^{\text{synth}} = \sum_{j=2}^{J+1} \hat{w}_j Y_{j,t} \qquad \text{for } t > T_{\text{pre}}
```

The crux: the post-period synthetic values are **out-of-sample**. You never fit
to them. That's what makes the post-period gap a credible counterfactual
rather than a curve you drew through the data.

### Step 4 — The treatment effect

Observed minus synthetic, per period, then averaged:

```math
\tau_t = Y_{1,t} - \hat{Y}_{1,t}^{\text{synth}}, \qquad \widehat{\text{ATT}} = \frac{1}{T_{\text{post}}} \sum_{t > T_{\text{pre}}} \tau_t
```

### Step 5 — Inference by permutation (in-space placebos)

You have **one** treated unit, so there's no sampling distribution and
classical standard errors don't apply. The fix is a **permutation test** —
turn every donor into a fake treated unit:

1. Pretend Boston was treated. Build "synthetic Boston" from the *other*
   donors. Compute its placebo gap.
2. Repeat for every donor.
3. Compare New York's real gap to the distribution of placebo gaps. If it's
   larger than 95% of them, the effect is significant at ≈ 0.05.

The full inferential logic — and why you rank by the **post/pre RMSPE ratio**,
not the raw gap — is §4.

## 3. Diagnostics: is the fit real, or fragile?

A synthetic control is only as trustworthy as its pre-period fit and its
robustness. Four checks, and the first two are the ones reviewers demand.

### 3.1 Pre-treatment fit (RMSPE)

The **root mean square prediction error** over the pre-period:

```math
\text{RMSPE}_{\text{pre}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T_{\text{pre}}} \sum_{t \le T_{\text{pre}}} \left(Y_{1,t} - \hat{Y}^{\text{synth}}_{1,t}\right)^2}
```

A large pre-RMSPE means the synthetic never tracked the treated unit, so the
post-period gap is meaningless — you can't attribute a divergence to treatment
if the two lines never matched to begin with. **A bad pre-fit is not a small
problem; it invalidates the whole design** (and is exactly the trigger for
§6.2's ASCM/SDID).

### 3.2 The post/pre RMSPE ratio — the inference workhorse

Do **not** rank the treated unit against placebos by *absolute* gap size — a
placebo city that fit terribly in the pre-period will have a big post gap for
reasons unrelated to treatment. Rank by the **ratio**:

```math
\text{ratio} = \frac{\text{RMSPE}_{\text{post}}}{\text{RMSPE}_{\text{pre}}}
```

This rewards the unit with a *near-perfect pre-fit* (small denominator) and a
*large post-divergence* (big numerator) — the true signature of a treatment
effect. A placebo with a huge post gap *and* a huge pre gap has a ratio near 1
and is correctly flagged as noise, not signal. This ratio is what the
permutation p-value (§4) is built on.

### 3.3 Leave-one-out donor robustness

SCM weights are sparse — "Synthetic Chicago is 80% Boston, 20% Austin." That
concentration is a **vulnerability**: if Boston had its own localized shock
exactly when your treatment launched, your counterfactual is ruined and you'd
never know.

**The test:** iteratively drop the *heaviest-weighted* donor from the pool and
re-run the whole optimization.

- **Robust:** the synthetic line barely moves — the algorithm redistributes
  Boston's weight to similar cities (Philadelphia, Seattle) and the estimate
  holds.
- **Fragile:** the line swings dramatically — your entire result rested on one
  donor, and you should not ship it.

### 3.4 Sparsity / interpretability

Report the weights. A handful of interpretable, nonzero weights is a feature;
a fit that needs 30 donors each at 3% is a sign you're overfitting the
pre-period (§7). Sparse, nameable weights are also what make the LOO test
(§3.3) meaningful.

## 4. Inference in depth: why not cluster-robust SEs?

A natural question coming out of [Lecture 3 §3 (DiD standard
errors)](../03-difference-in-differences/3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#3-inference-the-standard-error-is-where-did-dies):
DiD fixed its SE problem with cluster-robust SEs at the treatment-assignment
level. **Does SCM do the same?**

**No — and the reason is the few-clusters problem from [Lecture 3 §3.4
(few clusters)](../03-difference-in-differences/3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#34-the-catch-that-ties-the-lecture-together-few-clusters),
taken to its limit.** CRVE is consistent in the *number of clusters*. Classic
SCM has **one treated unit** — one California, one launch city. That's a single
cluster in the treatment arm; there's no sampling distribution to average over,
so a cluster-robust variance isn't merely unreliable, it's *undefined*.

So SCM swaps sampling-based inference for **randomization-based inference** —
the permutation test of §2 Step 5. The question changes shape:

- **DiD asks** (Neyman): "accounting for correlated errors, is the effect
  distinguishable from sampling noise?" → CRVE.
- **SCM asks** (Fisher): "is the treated unit's gap larger than what this exact
  procedure produces on units that were *not* treated?" → the placebo
  distribution.

### 4.1 How the weighting is handled

Doesn't estimating the weights ($0.59\,\text{Boston} + 0.41\,\text{Austin}$)
add uncertainty a naive gap ignores? It does — and permutation captures it
**for free**, because **the weights are re-fit inside every placebo run.**
Pretend Boston is treated, and you solve the whole optimization again from the
remaining donors. Every placebo carries its own freshly-estimated weights, so
the weight-fitting variability is already in the reference distribution. You
never compute an SE — **you compute a rank.**

### 4.2 Why an analytic SE isn't available

The weights solve a **constrained optimization** ($w_j \ge 0$, $\sum w_j = 1$),
and most constraints **bind** (Seattle and Denver get exactly zero). An
estimator on the boundary of its parameter space is **not asymptotically
normal**, so the delta method — which linearizes around an interior point — has
no valid expansion. The sparsity that makes SCM interpretable is what kills the
analytic SE. Permutation sidesteps it entirely.

### 4.3 Verified

Permutation on a **20-donor** panel (a larger pool than §5's four-donor fit,
so the p-value floor is low enough to matter):

```
  true effect = 0:   treated RMSPE-ratio = 1.13,  median placebo = 2.36  ->  p = 0.86
  true effect = 15:  treated RMSPE-ratio = 19.0,  median placebo = 2.80  ->  p = 0.048
```

With no effect the treated unit is unremarkable (p = 0.86); with a real effect
its gap is the most extreme of all 21 units (p = 1/21 ≈ 0.048).

The **floor** on your p-value is $1/(J+1)$: **ten donors caps your best
possible p-value at 0.09** — you can't reach 5% no matter how large the effect.
A credible SCM needs a reasonably large donor pool for *inference*, not just
fit.

### 4.4 The spectrum: how many treated units?

| Treated units | Inference |
|---|---|
| **1** | Permutation / in-space placebos. Cannot cluster. |
| **A handful** | Jackknife over treated units — **SDID** (§6.2) supplies this |
| **Many** | CRVE becomes meaningful again — back in [Lecture 3 §3](../03-difference-in-differences/3.1-classic-did-and-geo-controls.md#3-inference-the-standard-error-is-where-did-dies)'s world |

Modern single-unit alternative: **conformal inference** (Chernozhukov,
Wüthrich & Zhu 2021) permutes residuals over *time* rather than units — for one
treated unit with a long pre-period.

## 5. Worked example

An actual fitted run (code in §6). Donors: Boston, Austin, Seattle, Denver. The
DGP makes New York $0.6\,\text{Boston} + 0.4\,\text{Austin}$ in the pre-period,
then adds a **+15** effect post.

```
  Fitted convex-hull weights (pre-period only):
    Boston   0.592      <- true 0.60
    Austin   0.408      <- true 0.40
    Seattle  0.000      <- zeroed by convex-hull sparsity
    Denver   0.000      <- zeroed
    pre-period RMSE: 0.69

  Recovered ATT = +15.29   (true +15.0)
```

The optimizer recovered the generative weights without being told them, zeroed
the two irrelevant donors (interpretable sparsity), and reading the
out-of-sample gap recovered the effect. The "0.6 Boston + 0.4 Austin" story
isn't hand-waving — it's what the optimizer does when the donor pool can
genuinely reconstruct the treated unit.

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize

def synthetic_control(Y_treated, Y_donors, T_pre):
    """Y_treated (T,), Y_donors (T,J), T_pre pre-periods.
    Returns weights, synthetic series, per-period effect."""
    Dpre, ypre = Y_donors[:T_pre], Y_treated[:T_pre]
    J = Y_donors.shape[1]
    loss = lambda w: np.sum((ypre - Dpre @ w) ** 2)
    constraints = [{"type": "eq", "fun": lambda w: w.sum() - 1.0}]  # sum to 1
    bounds = [(0.0, 1.0)] * J                                       # non-negative
    w = minimize(loss, np.full(J, 1 / J), bounds=bounds,
                 constraints=constraints, method="SLSQP").x
    synth = Y_donors @ w                    # frozen weights, projected forward
    return w, synth, Y_treated - synth      # weights, synthetic, tau_t

w, synth, effect = synthetic_control(NY, donors, T_pre=12)
print("weights:", dict(zip(names, w.round(3))))                    # Boston .592, Austin .408
print(f"pre RMSPE: {np.sqrt(np.mean(effect[:12]**2)):.2f}  ATT: {effect[12:].mean():.2f}")

# ---- Inference: rank by post/pre RMSPE RATIO, not raw gap (section 3.2) ----
def placebo_ratios(units, T_pre):
    out = {}
    for j in range(units.shape[1]):
        _, _, eff = synthetic_control(units[:, j], np.delete(units, j, axis=1), T_pre)
        pre, post = np.sqrt(np.mean(eff[:T_pre]**2)), np.sqrt(np.mean(eff[T_pre:]**2))
        out[j] = post / max(pre, 1e-8)
    return out
# p-value = share of placebo ratios >= the treated unit's ratio

# ---- LOO donor robustness (section 3.3): drop the heaviest donor, re-fit ----
heaviest = int(np.argmax(w))
w2, synth2, eff2 = synthetic_control(NY, np.delete(donors, heaviest, axis=1), T_pre=12)
print(f"ATT without heaviest donor: {eff2[12:].mean():.2f}  (stable => robust)")

# ---- Production: pysyncon, SparseSC; CausalImpact for the BSTS variant ----
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"Fit the weights on all the data."**
Fit on the **pre-period only**. The post-period must stay out-of-sample or the
gap is a curve you drew through the outcome. §2.

**"Great pre-period fit proves it worked."**
A near-perfect fit with many donors is a red flag for *overfitting*. The honest
checks are a parsimonious donor pool, the placebo distribution, and LOO
robustness. §3.

**"Use standard errors / cluster like in DiD."**
One treated unit is one cluster — CRVE is undefined. Inference is permutation,
ranked by RMSPE ratio. §4.

**"Rank placebos by the size of the effect."**
Rank by the **post/pre RMSPE ratio**. A placebo that fit badly pre-period will
have a big post gap that means nothing. §3.2.

**"The weights are 80% one city — that's clean and interpretable."**
It's also *fragile*. Run the leave-one-out test; if dropping that donor swings
the result, you have a one-city bet, not an estimate. §3.3.

**"Synthetic control removes the parallel-trends assumption."**
It *enforces* the pre-trend match rather than assuming it — but still assumes
the match would have *continued* absent treatment. That's parallel trends on
the synthetic control, still untestable post-period. More credible, not
eliminated.
