# 6.5 — CausalImpact (Bayesian Structural Time Series)

> **Part of:** [Lecture 6 — Synthetic Control & Counterfactual Estimators](./README.md)
> **Prerequisites:** [§6.1 Classic SCM](./6.1-classic-scm.md) (the counterfactual idea) and [§6.4 ITS](./6.4-interrupted-time-series.md) (the single-series baseline this fixes).

Google's economics team (Brodersen et al. 2015) built **CausalImpact** because
ITS (§6.4) is defenseless against seasonality and concurrent events. The fix:
instead of only extrapolating your own past, borrow *other time series* — 49
other states, Google Trends, the S&P 500, weather — that share the same seasonal
and macro environment but were **not** touched by the treatment. It's synthetic
control (§6.1) reincarnated as a Bayesian time-series model.

---

## 1. The architecture

CausalImpact fuses two pieces:

- A **state-space (structural) time-series model** — captures trend and
  seasonality (weekly, monthly, yearly cycles) that DiD's flat parallel-trends
  assumption can't.
- A **Bayesian regression on the control series** — builds the counterfactual
  from the donor pool.

It fits both on the **pre-period**, projects the counterfactual forward, and
reports the treated-minus-counterfactual gap with **Bayesian credible
intervals** — the risk bounds the business wants.

### Spike-and-slab priors — automatic control selection

With hundreds of candidate controls, a plain regression overfits
catastrophically. CausalImpact uses a **spike-and-slab prior**:

- **The spike:** a large probability mass *exactly at zero* — the prior *assumes
  almost every control is useless* and forces its coefficient to zero.
- **The slab:** a wide, flat distribution — a control only escapes the spike if
  the data show *overwhelming* predictive power (Nevada genuinely tracks
  California).

The result: the model **automatically selects the 3–4 most robust controls** out
of hundreds, dodging the overfitting that would wreck a naive regression. It's
the Bayesian cousin of synthetic control's sparse convex weights (§6.1).

---

## 2. The danger zone — contaminated controls

**The golden rule: control series must be strictly independent of the
treatment.** Violate it and the bias has a *direction* you must know cold.

**Cannibalization → overestimation.** Run a promo in New York, use New Jersey as
control. NJ residents cross the bridge to use the NY promo, so **NJ sales
*drop*.** The model reads NJ's drop as "the macro-economy is crashing" and
predicts NY *would have* crashed too. Since NY actually rose, the estimated
effect is **massively inflated.** *Verified* (code in §5): true effect **+8.0**,
a clean control recovers **7.8**, but a cannibalized control (which drops during
the promo) reports **14.0** — nearly double.

**Halo → underestimation.** Run a national TV ad, use a "non-targeted"
demographic as control. The ad goes viral and the control demographic sees it
anyway, so the **control rises.** The model predicts the treated series was going
to rise organically, and the estimated effect **shrinks toward zero.**

The mnemonic: a control that moves *with* the treatment (cannibalized down, or
halo'd up) tilts the counterfactual and biases the effect — down-moving control →
overestimate, up-moving control → underestimate.

## 3. The falsification playbook — the in-time placebo

Before any CausalImpact chart reaches leadership, prove the model isn't
hallucinating:

1. Go to the pre-treatment period (say 3 months before the real launch).
2. Pretend you launched on a random Tuesday where **nothing happened.**
3. Run CausalImpact on that fake intervention.
4. **The check:** the estimated effect must be **≈ 0, with the 95% credible
   interval covering zero.** If the fake launch "generated \$1M," your controls
   are broken (contaminated or non-predictive) and the model is invalid.

Slide the fake date across the whole pre-period and confirm the false-positive
rate is near nominal. This is the BSTS analog of SCM's placebo tests (§6.1) and
the ITS sanity check (§6.4).

## 4. DiD vs. CausalImpact — the decision matrix

Both build a counterfactual for a treated unit; pick by the constraint.

| Choose **DiD** ([Lecture 3](../03-difference-in-differences/)) when… | Choose **CausalImpact (BSTS)** when… |
|---|---|
| **Auditability** — Finance/Legal/Regulators must follow the math (a linear equation, not MCMC) | **Complex seasonality** — weekly + monthly + yearly cycles that flat parallel trends can't capture |
| **SQL-only** environment — DiD is one query; you can't run MCMC in a warehouse | **Hundreds of controls** — need spike-and-slab to auto-filter a noisy covariate matrix |
| **Short pre-period** — 2 weeks of history; BSTS needs deep history to learn the state-space | **Risk bounds** — the business needs Bayesian credible intervals for the worst case |

The through-line: **DiD is transparent and cheap; BSTS is flexible and
probabilistic.** Neither is "better" — the setting picks.

## 5. Worked probe: the angry VP (defending a model without alienating the room)

**The scenario.** Your flawless, placebo-tested CausalImpact shows a
multi-million-dollar ad campaign had **zero** causal impact. The VP of Marketing
is furious, calls your "Bayesian synthetic controls" a black box, and points to a
simple Excel chart: revenue rose **20%** the week the ad launched. How do you run
this meeting?

**The staff-level answer is a communication strategy, not a statistics lecture.**
Junior DSs die on the hill of mathematical purity — arguing spike-and-slab priors
for an hour while the VP loses all trust. You **build a bridge with
transparency.**

**Step 1 — meet them on their terms.** Run the *simplest* model they'd accept —
a plain **difference-in-differences**. If it *agrees* with the BSTS (both near
zero), present the DiD: two simple parallel lines. *"We ran the complex model to
be safe, but the simple math confirms it — the campaign didn't move revenue."*
You win in five minutes without opening the black box.

**Step 2 — if they diverge, use the simple model to break itself.** Suppose the
Excel says +20%, DiD says +15%, BSTS says 0. Don't defend the BSTS internals.
Instead, *use DiD to expose why DiD is wrong:*

> *"I wanted to believe the 20% lift, so I ran a simple difference-in-differences.
> But look at the pre-period — our treatment and control groups don't move in
> parallel during winter. Parallel trends is broken here, so the simple DiD is
> mathematically invalid: it's capturing our winter seasonality, not the ad. I
> had to use a dynamic model to strip that seasonality out — and once I did, the
> true ad effect went to zero."*

You've used the *simple* model to demonstrate *why the complex one was
necessary* — disarming the stakeholder with their own preferred tool instead of
a lecture. **Clarity buys the trust that precision alone never will.**

## 7. Common misreadings

**"CausalImpact is a black box."**
It's a *transparent* structural model (trend + seasonality + selected controls)
with a falsification test. The staff move is to explain it via the *simpler*
model it corrects, not its internals. §5.

**"Any related series makes a good control."**
Only ones *unaffected by the treatment.* A cannibalized control overestimates
(verified 14 vs 8), a halo'd control underestimates. §2.

**"A tight credible interval means the answer is right."**
Only if the controls are clean and the placebo passes. Contaminated controls give
a confident *wrong* answer. Run the in-time placebo first. §3.

**"BSTS beats DiD, so always use it."**
DiD wins on auditability, SQL-only pipelines, and short pre-periods. The
constraint picks. §4.

**"Revenue rose 20% at launch, so the ad worked."**
That's the raw before/after with no counterfactual — the exact error CausalImpact
exists to correct. Seasonality or a concurrent event can produce the whole 20%.
§5.
