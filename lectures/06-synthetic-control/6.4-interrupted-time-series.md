# 6.4 — Interrupted Time Series (ITS)

> **Part of:** [Lecture 6 — Synthetic Control & Counterfactual Estimators](./README.md)

When you can't run an A/B test and you have **only one treated series with no
control** — a nationwide price change, a global PR event, a site-wide redesign —
the crudest counterfactual is your own past. ITS is the "meat cleaver": draw a
line at the intervention and check whether the trend *snapped.*

---

## 1. Segmented regression

Fit a single regression with terms that turn on at the intervention time $T_0$
(where $D_t = \mathbb{1}[t \ge T_0]$):

```math
Y_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 t + \beta_2 D_t + \beta_3 (t - T_0) D_t + \epsilon_t
```

Each coefficient is a distinct piece of the story, and you must be able to name
them on a whiteboard:

- **$\beta_1$ — pre-trend slope.** How the metric was growing *before* the
  intervention.
- **$\beta_2$ — immediate level change.** The vertical *jump or drop* on the day
  itself ("the site crashed → an instant −10,000 DAU").
- **$\beta_3$ — change in slope.** The long-run acceleration/deceleration ("no
  day-1 jump, but daily growth steepened by 2%").

Separating the **level** change ($\beta_2$) from the **slope** change ($\beta_3$)
is the whole value of ITS: a one-time shock looks very different from a lasting
change in the growth rate.

---

## 2. The two fatal flaws

### 2.1 Concurrent events — the dealbreaker

ITS has **no control group**, so it cannot separate your intervention from
*anything else* that happened at the same time. Launch on November 25 and the
metric jumps — but November 25 is **Black Friday.** ITS attributes the entire
organic holiday spike to your feature. Any co-occurring shock (a competitor
outage, a macro event, a marketing push) contaminates $\beta_2$ and $\beta_3$
with no way to disentangle it. This is the single reason to prefer CausalImpact
(§6.5): *controls* absorb the concurrent shock.

### 2.2 Autocorrelation — mandatory Newey-West

Time-series errors are **serially correlated** — today's residual looks like
yesterday's. Use naive OLS standard errors and your confidence intervals are
wildly too narrow, because the effective sample size is a fraction of $T$.

*Verified* (code in §4): with AR(1) errors and a **true effect of zero**, naive
OLS flags a "significant" level change **44% of the time** (nominal 5%) — a
false-positive machine. **Newey-West (HAC) standard errors** are mandatory; they
cut it to ~21% here. That Newey-West *still* doesn't fully fix it at high
autocorrelation is itself the lesson: a single series with serial correlation is
low-information, which is why controls (§6.5) dominate.

---

## 3. When to use ITS

Only when you genuinely have **no usable control series** and a **clean window**
with no concurrent events, and you've applied Newey-West. It's the honest
baseline — transparent, one regression, auditable — but its assumptions (nothing
else happened; the pre-trend extrapolates) are strong. The moment you can find
control series that share the seasonality, escalate to CausalImpact (§6.5).

## 4. Python

```python
import numpy as np, statsmodels.api as sm

# t = time index, T0 = intervention period, Y = the single treated series
D = (t >= T0).astype(float)
X = np.column_stack([t, D, (t - T0) * D])           # trend, level jump, slope change
# --- MUST use Newey-West (HAC) SEs -- naive OLS gives 44% false positives ---
fit = sm.OLS(Y, sm.add_constant(X)).fit(cov_type="HAC", cov_kwds={"maxlags": int(len(t)**0.25)})
print(fit.summary())     # coef[2]=level change (beta2), coef[3]=slope change (beta3)

# Sanity: an in-time placebo (fake T0 in the pre-period) should give ~0 -- see 6.5.
```

## 5. Common misreadings

**"The metric jumped at launch, so the launch caused it."**
Not if anything else happened that day (Black Friday). ITS can't separate
concurrent shocks — that needs a control (§6.5). §2.1.

**"It's significant, ship it."**
With naive OLS SEs on autocorrelated data, "significant" is a 44%-false-positive
artifact. Use Newey-West. §2.2.

**"β₂ is the effect."**
β₂ is only the *immediate level* change. A treatment can have zero jump but a
large *slope* change (β₃) — or vice versa. Report both. §1.

**"ITS is as good as synthetic control."**
No — it has no counterfactual beyond extrapolating your own pre-trend, so it's
fully exposed to concurrent events and seasonality. It's the baseline SCM /
CausalImpact improve on. §3.
