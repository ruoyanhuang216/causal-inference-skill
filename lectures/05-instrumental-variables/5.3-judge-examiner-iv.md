## 1. The design: randomness in who decides

Whenever a **human is randomly assigned** to make a binary-ish decision — a
judge sentencing, an underwriter approving credit, a moderator banning, a
collection agent calling — you have an instrument hiding in plain sight.
Humans vary in **leniency**, and if cases are routed to them at random, that
leniency is exogenous to the case.

> User A is randomly assigned to underwriter **John** (approves 80%). User B
> to **Sarah** (approves 40%). Whether you drew a lenient or strict decider is
> random, but it strongly predicts your treatment. That's an instrument.

The treatment $D$ is the decision (approved / incarcerated / dollars
collected); the outcome $Y$ is downstream (retention, recidivism, repayment);
the instrument $Z$ is **the assigned decider's leniency.**

---

## 2. The instrument: leave-one-out leniency

You don't use the raw decider identity — you use their **leniency**, measured
as their approval rate. But with a critical correction:

**Leave-one-out (LOO):** compute John's leniency from *all his cases except
the one you're instrumenting.*

```math
Z_i = \frac{1}{n_{j(i)} - 1} \sum_{k \,:\, j(k)=j(i),\; k \neq i} D_k
```

**Why LOO?** If you included case $i$'s own outcome in its decider's leniency
score, you'd manufacture a **mechanical correlation** between the instrument
and the treatment — $D_i$ would literally be a component of $Z_i$. The
instrument would predict the treatment partly by containing it, biasing the
first stage and the whole design. Dropping the own observation breaks that
loop. (This is a *jackknife* instrument, and it also fixes the
many-instruments problem — §4.)

---

## 3. The three assumptions, specialized

| Assumption | Judge-IV form | How it breaks |
|---|---|---|
| **Independence** | Cases are randomly routed to deciders | Complex/severe cases funneled to specific deciders → leniency confounded with case difficulty |
| **Exclusion** | The decider affects $Y$ **only** through the decision $D$ | The decider does something *else* that touches $Y$ — a call, advice, a script (§5, §6) |
| **Monotonicity** | A more-lenient decider is weakly more likely to treat **every** case type | A decider lenient overall but strict on *one segment* → defiers (§5) |

Independence you test: check that pre-treatment case characteristics are
balanced across deciders (as-good-as-random routing). Exclusion and
monotonicity are the ones that get attacked in review — the next two sections
are exactly those attacks.

---

## 4. Many instruments, and why LOO also fixes them

The tempting first stage regresses treatment on **one dummy per decider** — 50
underwriters, 50 dummies. This is a **many-instruments** problem, and it has a
specific finite-sample pathology: **2SLS is biased back toward OLS**, the very
confounded estimate you used IV to escape. With many (individually weak)
instruments, the first stage overfits, and $\hat D$ starts to reproduce the
endogenous $D$.

*Verified* (code in §7): 50 deciders, true effect **2.0**, a confounder that
biases OLS up to **2.67**.

```
  OLS (biased)             : 2.67
  2SLS, 50 decider dummies : 2.13   <- 19% of the way back toward the OLS bias
  2SLS, LOO leniency (1 iv): 2.02   <- recovers the truth
```

The dummy specification lands **19% of the way from the truth to the OLS
bias**. Collapsing the 50 dummies into a **single LOO leniency instrument**
removes it. LOO does double duty: it kills the mechanical correlation (§2)
*and* the many-instruments bias. This is the first fix for any judge-IV design
built on decider dummies.

## 5. Worked probe: defending a Ramp underwriter IV

**Setup.** Ramp routes credit-limit-increase requests randomly to 50
underwriters and uses LOO underwriter leniency as an instrument for the credit
limit $D$, to estimate its effect on transaction volume $Y$. First-stage F =
45. The result is large and positive. The Head of Risk attacks two
assumptions.

### 5.1 The monotonicity challenge — "John hates SaaS, Sarah loves SaaS"

*"John is lenient overall, Sarah is strict. But John hates SaaS while Sarah
loves it. Does this break the design?"*

**Yes — it creates defiers, and it breaks the LATE.**

Monotonicity requires that a more-lenient decider is weakly more likely to
approve **every** case type. The leniency instrument assumes a *consistent
ranking* of deciders across all cases. John-hates-SaaS destroys that:

- For a **non-SaaS** case: John (lenient) approves, Sarah (strict) denies. The
  leniency instrument pushes toward approval. ✅ complier direction.
- For a **SaaS** case: John (lenient overall, but anti-SaaS) *denies*, Sarah
  (strict overall, but pro-SaaS) *approves*. A SaaS startup assigned to the
  "lenient" instrument gets a **worse** outcome. ❌ **defier direction.**

SaaS applicants are **defiers** relative to the scalar leniency instrument —
the instrument moves them *opposite* to everyone else. Monotonicity fails.

**The math consequence:** the IV estimand is a weighted average where compliers
enter positive and defiers enter **negative**. Defiers partially cancel
compliers; the 2SLS coefficient is attenuated and can even flip sign relative
to the true effect. You can no longer read it as "the effect for compliers."
(Frandsen, Lefgren & Leslie 2023 give a formal test for exactly this — a
testable implication of monotonicity + exclusion in judge designs.)

### 5.2 The exclusion challenge — the 30-minute Zoom call

*"When our lenient underwriters approve, they don't just click 'Approve' —
they get on a 30-minute Zoom to congratulate the founders, give financial
advice, and teach them our spend-management software."*

**This instantly destroys 2SLS validity, because it's a second channel.**

Exclusion requires the underwriter to affect transaction volume **only through
the credit limit**. The Zoom call is a **backdoor path**: $Z$ (underwriter) →
*consulting/onboarding* → $Y$, bypassing $D$ (the limit) entirely.

So when transaction volume rises, you cannot tell whether it was the **higher
limit** or the **30 minutes of free expert consulting and software training**.
2SLS attributes the *entire* effect — limit plus consulting — to the credit
limit, biasing $\beta$ **upward**. The instrument is contaminated by a channel
that has nothing to do with the treatment you're trying to measure.

Note the first-stage F of 45 is irrelevant to both attacks: a strong first
stage says nothing about monotonicity or exclusion. **Relevance is the easy
assumption; these two are where designs die.**

## 6. Design review: your collection-agent IV

You're using **agent effectiveness** as an instrument for **kept amount** (the
dollars collected on the first call) to measure the effect on **60-day
payments**. Here is the full staff-level review.

**Your design:**
- First stage: `kept_amount ~ agent (≈50 agents) + covariates`
- Second stage: `payments_60d ~ fitted_kept_amount + covariates`
- Instrument: random assignment of borrowers to agents (independence verified
  statistically across loan dimensions *and* by business logic).
- Agents aren't involved in later-stage interventions, and later interventions
  don't correlate with agents.

### 6.1 Verdict: yes, this is a textbook examiner IV

You have the core engine:

- **Independence ✅** — you verified random assignment across loan dimensions
  and confirmed it operationally. This is exactly the balance test §3 asks for.
- **Relevance ✅** — agents differ in collection ability (check the F, §6.4).
- **Exclusion** — plausible *for later interventions* (you confirmed none
  correlate with agents), but there's a subtler within-call threat (§6.3).

### 6.2 What you're identifying: an ACR, not a LATE

Your treatment — **kept amount** — is **continuous dollars**, not a binary
decision. So you are *not* estimating a standard LATE. You're estimating the
**Average Causal Response** (Angrist & Imbens 1995, [README §3](./README.md#3-the-estimand-late-or-acr-for-continuous-treatment)):

> $\beta_1$ = for the **marginal dollar** of kept-amount that agent
> effectiveness moves, 60-day payments rise by $\beta_1$ — averaged across the
> kept-amount distribution, weighted by where agents move the most borrowers.

And it's **local to compliers**: borrowers whose immediate payout is *sensitive
to agent persuasiveness*. It says nothing about **always-payers** (who pay
regardless of who calls) or **never-payers** (who hang up on everyone). When
you report it, say so: *"per marginal dollar collected on the call, for
borrowers whose payment responds to agent skill — not the whole book."*

### 6.3 The danger zone: within-call exclusion (same as the Zoom call)

Your exclusion defense handles *separate later* interventions. But the sharp
threat is the **Ramp Zoom call, exactly** — does a good agent affect 60-day
payments through any channel *other than the immediate kept amount*, **on the
same call**?

- Does a high-performing agent spend five extra minutes setting up an
  **auto-pay schedule**?
- Do they use a stricter **warning script** that changes the borrower's future
  willingness to pay?
- Do they **restructure** the loan or coach the borrower?

If yes, then the agent moves 60-day payments through a channel that *isn't*
the immediate kept amount, and your $\beta_1$ **over-attributes** to kept
amount — it soaks up the credit for the auto-pay setup and the scary script.
This is the exact structure of §5.2's Zoom call: one instrument, multiple
channels, exclusion violated.

**What's genuinely clean regardless:** the **reduced form** — the effect of
*being assigned a more effective agent* on 60-day payments — is always valid
under independence. What requires the exclusion restriction is
**attributing** that effect to *kept amount specifically.* If agents have
multiple within-call levers, you can honestly report "more effective agents
raise 60-day payments by X" but *not* "kept amount causes Y" — the mediation
claim needs kept amount to be the sole channel.

**How to defend or fix it:**
- **Argue** the call's only durable lever is money collected — if agents read
  a fixed script and auto-pay/restructuring happen in a *separate*, uncorrelated
  workflow, you're clean. Document this.
- **Measure the other channels** (auto-pay flag, promise-to-pay, restructure
  flag set *on the call*). If they're rare or balanced across agents, exclusion
  holds. **Don't just "control" for them** — they're post-treatment mediators,
  and conditioning on them is bad-control territory (a later lecture). Use them
  to *argue* the channel is negligible, not as regression controls.

### 6.4 The four upgrades to run

**A. Switch from 50 agent dummies to a LOO leniency instrument.** Your first
stage uses ~50 agent dummies — a many-instruments design that biases 2SLS
toward OLS (§4; verified 19% of the way back to the OLS bias). Replace them
with a single continuous instrument: each borrower's agent's **mean kept
amount on all *other* calls** (leave-one-out, §2). This removes both the
mechanical correlation and the finite-sample bias.

**B. Report the effective F, clustered at the agent level** (Montiel Olea &
Pflueger 2013). Your errors are almost certainly correlated within agent, so
the classic F > 10 is invalid — use the effective F, and remember the honest
bar is well above 10.

**C. Test monotonicity — the reverse-rank check.** Are good agents good for
*everyone*? Split borrowers by a pre-treatment covariate (high vs. low credit
score). Compute each agent's LOO effectiveness within each group and scatter
them against each other. A strong positive correlation supports monotonicity;
an agent who's best for high-score borrowers but *worst* for low-score
borrowers is a defier generator (the SaaS problem of §5.1). Frandsen-Lefgren-
Leslie (2023) formalizes this.

**D. Placebo outcome — the independence stress test.** Replace the outcome
with something determined *before* the call (the borrower's historical
6-month payment rate). Re-run 2SLS. $\beta_1$ **must be statistically zero.** A
nonzero placebo means assignment isn't truly random — agents are getting
systematically different borrowers, and your independence assumption is false.

Run these four and the design goes from "structurally sound" to "defensible in
a Principal review."

## 7. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.api as sm
# tsls() helper from 5.1

# df: borrower rows with agent (id), D (kept_amount), Y (payments_60d), covariates

# ---- Upgrade A: LOO leniency instead of 50 agent dummies ----
g = df.groupby("agent")["D"]
df["Z_loo"] = (g.transform("sum") - df["D"]) / (g.transform("count") - 1)

tau = tsls(df["Y"].values, df["D"].values, df["Z_loo"].values.reshape(-1, 1),
           X=df[["covariate"]].values)          # continuous D -> this is the ACR
print(f"ACR (per marginal dollar kept) = {tau:.3f}")

# Contrast: the biased many-dummy version (do NOT ship this)
dummies = pd.get_dummies(df["agent"], drop_first=True).astype(float).values
tau_dummies = tsls(df["Y"].values, df["D"].values, dummies)
print(f"50-dummy 2SLS = {tau_dummies:.3f}  (biased toward OLS -- see section 4)")

# ---- Upgrade B: first-stage F, clustered at the agent level ----
fs = sm.OLS(df["D"], sm.add_constant(df[["Z_loo", "covariate"]])).fit(
    cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["agent"]})
print(f"first-stage F on Z_loo = {fs.tvalues['Z_loo'] ** 2:.1f}")

# ---- Upgrade C: monotonicity reverse-rank check ----
def loo_effectiveness(sub):
    gg = sub.groupby("agent")["D"]
    return ((gg.transform("sum") - sub["D"]) / (gg.transform("count") - 1)) \
        .groupby(sub["agent"]).mean()
hi = loo_effectiveness(df[df.credit_score >= df.credit_score.median()])
lo = loo_effectiveness(df[df.credit_score <  df.credit_score.median()])
rank_corr = pd.concat([hi, lo], axis=1).corr().iloc[0, 1]
print(f"agent rank correlation across subgroups = {rank_corr:.2f}  (want strongly +)")

# ---- Upgrade D: placebo outcome (pre-call history) -- must be ~0 ----
tau_placebo = tsls(df["hist_payment_rate"].values, df["D"].values,
                   df["Z_loo"].values.reshape(-1, 1), X=df[["covariate"]].values)
print(f"placebo (pre-call outcome) = {tau_placebo:.3f}  (must be ~0)")
```

## 8. Common misreadings

**"Use the raw judge approval rate as the instrument."**
Include the own case and you manufacture a mechanical correlation between $Z$
and $D$. Use leave-one-out. §2.

**"50 agent dummies make a strong first stage, so I'm fine."**
Many weak instruments bias 2SLS toward OLS (19% in the verified case).
Collapse to a single LOO instrument. §4.

**"My treatment is continuous, so it's still a LATE."**
Continuous treatment gives the **ACR** — the effect per marginal unit, for
responsive units. Different object; name it correctly. §6.2.

**"The agents don't do any later interventions, so exclusion holds."**
The threat isn't *later* channels — it's *within-call* ones (auto-pay setup,
scripts). If the agent moves the outcome through anything but the immediate
kept amount, $\beta_1$ over-attributes. §6.3.

**"F = 45, so the design is solid."**
Relevance says nothing about monotonicity or exclusion — the two assumptions
that actually get designs killed. §5.

**"A lenient judge is lenient, so monotonicity holds."**
Only if lenient on *every* segment. A judge lenient overall but strict on one
type generates defiers, and the LATE breaks. §5.1.
