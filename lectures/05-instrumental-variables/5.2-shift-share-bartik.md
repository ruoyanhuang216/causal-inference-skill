# 5.2 — Shift-Share (Bartik) Instruments

> **Part of:** [Lecture 5 — Instrumental Variables](./README.md)
> **Prerequisite:** [§5.1](./5.1-standard-2sls.md) — a Bartik instrument is a *constructed* $Z$ plugged into standard 2SLS. The four assumptions still apply.

---

## 1. The construction

Sometimes you have no natural instrument, but the structure of the problem
hands you one. Units (cities, merchants) are composed of **sectors**, and the
world delivers **shocks** to those sectors. Combine a unit's *exposure* to
each sector with the *global shock* to that sector, and you have an instrument
that predicts the unit's treatment without (you hope) touching its confounders.

```math
Z_i = \sum_{k=1}^{K} s_{ik} \, g_k
```

- $s_{ik}$ — **the share**: unit $i$'s baseline exposure to sector $k$ (80% of
  Merchant A's ad budget is on Facebook). Fixed at a **pre-treatment** period,
  so current outcomes can't feed back into it.
- $g_k$ — **the shock (shift)**: the aggregate shock to sector $k$ (Facebook's
  global ad efficiency drops 20% after iOS 14.5).

The instrument is "how hard did unit $i$ get hit by the mix of global shocks,
given its historical exposure." You then use $Z_i$ as the instrument for the
endogenous treatment $D_i$ (marketing spend, employment) in ordinary 2SLS.

The name: the **shares** are the exposure, the **shifts** are the shocks —
"shift-share." Originally Bartik (1991) for local labor markets; now
everywhere.

---

## 2. The identification debate — and why you must pick a side

For thirty years people used Bartik instruments without a clear account of
*why* they were valid. Between 2020 and 2022 two papers formalized it, and
they locate the exogeneity in **opposite places.** A staff-level answer states
which one you're relying on.

### 2.1 Share-based exogeneity — Goldsmith-Pinkham, Sorkin & Swift (2020)

**The claim:** the shocks $g_k$ don't carry the identification. The design is
valid because the **initial shares $s_{ik}$ are as-good-as-randomly assigned.**

Mechanically, this is equivalent to using the *shares themselves* as a set of
instruments (one per sector). The exogeneity you need is that a unit's
historical exposure mix is uncorrelated with its unobserved quality.

**The threat:** if a merchant's decision to lean 80% on Facebook is correlated
with its unobserved business quality (its margins, its industry, its
demographic), the shares are endogenous and exclusion fails.

### 2.2 Shock-based exogeneity — Borusyak, Hull & Jaravel (2022)

**The claim:** the shares can be **completely endogenous** business decisions.
The design is valid because the **global shocks $g_k$ are as-good-as-random**,
and there are *many* roughly-independent shocks. The shares are just weights
determining how much of each random shock a unit absorbs.

**The threat:** the shocks must be genuinely idiosyncratic. If a "shock" is a
long-run macro trend the units already anticipated and positioned for, it
isn't exogenous.

### 2.3 The rule

**These are different assumptions, not two views of one.** Share-based needs
*random shares, endogenous shocks OK*. Shock-based needs *random shocks,
endogenous shares OK*. Which one is defensible depends entirely on your
setting — and choosing wrong is an instant fail in review (§5).

---

## 3. Diagnostics

**Rotemberg weights** (for share-based designs). A Bartik estimate is a
weighted average of the sector-by-sector IV estimates; the Rotemberg weights
say *which sectors drive your result*. If 90% of the weight sits on one
sector, your instrument is fragile — the whole causal claim rests on one
share's exogeneity, and you should stress-test exactly that one.

**Pre-trend placebo tests.** The instrument must not predict the outcome in
the years *before* the shock. If high-Facebook-share merchants were already
growing faster *before* iOS 14.5, their shares are confounded with a growth
trajectory, and the design is picking that up rather than the shock.

**Balance / share-exogeneity checks** (share-based): are high-share units
comparable to low-share units on observables? **Shock count and dispersion**
(shock-based): do you have enough roughly-independent shocks for the
law-of-large-numbers argument, or is one shock doing all the work?

## 4. Worked probe: Shopify and iOS 14.5

**Setup.** You're a lead DS at **Shopify**, measuring the causal effect of
**merchant marketing spend $D$** on **GMV $Y$**. You can't regress directly —
successful merchants spend more (endogeneity). You build a Bartik instrument:

- **Shares $s_{ik}$:** each merchant's historical budget split across Google,
  Facebook, TikTok, Pinterest, Snap.
- **Shocks $g_k$:** **iOS 14.5 App Tracking Transparency** — a massive,
  permanent drop in ad efficiency that hit platforms *differently* (Facebook
  devastated, Google Search barely touched).

**Which framework — share-based or shock-based?**

### 4.1 The answer: shock-based (Borusyak), and it's the *only* defensible one

**Why share-based (Goldsmith-Pinkham) is indefensible here.** It would require
the *shares* — how merchants split their ad budget — to be as-good-as-random.
They are the opposite of random:

- A fast-fashion, impulse-buy, highly-visual merchant naturally pours 90% into
  Facebook/Instagram and TikTok.
- A high-ticket, intent-driven B2B office-equipment merchant pours 90% into
  Google Search.

So a merchant's share mix is tightly correlated with its **industry, margins,
and demographic** — enormous unobserved confounders for GMV. Claiming the
shares are random gets you failed on the spot: they're the product of
**endogenous sorting** by business type.

**Why shock-based (Borusyak) works.** It *allows* the shares to be fully
endogenous — they're just weights. The identification rests entirely on the
**shock**: iOS 14.5.

- Did any single Shopify merchant's unobserved quality cause Tim Cook to
  change Apple's privacy policy? No.
- Could a merchant have opted out of it? No — it was a systemic change to the
  entire mobile ecosystem.

From the perspective of any individual merchant, iOS 14.5 was an **act of
God** — massive, unforeseeable, exogenous. Because the *shock* is clean, the
Borusyak framework lets you use the (endogenous) share mix purely to measure
*how hard each merchant got hit*, producing a clean instrument for marketing
spend.

**Staff framing:** *"I'm relying on shock-based exogeneity. Marketing shares
are obviously endogenous — fast-fashion sorts into Facebook, B2B into Google
Search — so a share-based (Goldsmith-Pinkham) design fails immediately. But
iOS 14.5 is a textbook exogenous shock: no merchant caused it or could avoid
it. Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel lets the shares be endogenous weights and puts the
identifying burden entirely on the shock, which here is defensible. I'd
support it with pre-trend tests — high-Facebook-share merchants shouldn't have
been diverging in GMV *before* iOS 14.5."*

The general lesson: **a small number of large, plausibly-exogenous shocks →
shock-based; many plausibly-random exposure shares → share-based.** One
dominant act-of-God shock is the signature of a Borusyak design.

## 5. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm

# shares: (n_units, K) pre-period exposure, rows sum to 1
# shocks: (K,) global shock to each sector
# D: endogenous treatment (marketing spend), Y: outcome (GMV)

# ---- Construct the Bartik instrument ----
Z = shares @ shocks                          # Z_i = sum_k s_ik * g_k

# ---- Plug into standard 2SLS (see 5.1) ----
tau = tsls(Y, D, Z.reshape(-1, 1))           # coef on D
print(f"Bartik 2SLS effect = {tau:.3f}")

# ---- First-stage strength ----
F = sm.OLS(D, sm.add_constant(Z)).fit(cov_type="HC1").tvalues[1] ** 2
print(f"first-stage F = {F:.1f}")

# ---- Rotemberg weights: which sector drives the estimate? ----
# alpha_k proportional to  g_k * (share_k' * D_tilde), where D_tilde is the
# residualized treatment. If one weight dominates, the design is fragile.
# (use the ShiftShareSE / Bartik R packages, or Goldsmith-Pinkham's code)

# ---- Pre-trend placebo: Z must NOT predict PRE-shock outcome growth ----
# tsls(Y_pre_growth, D, Z)  -> must be ~0
```

## 6. Common misreadings

**"Bartik instruments are automatically valid — everyone uses them."**
They're valid only under *one* of two distinct assumptions (share- or
shock-based), and you must argue which. Using one without stating its
assumption is the classic failure. §2.

**"The shares are historical, so they're exogenous."**
Historical ≠ random. Fast-fashion sorts into Facebook, B2B into Google. If
shares reflect industry, they're endogenous and share-based identification
fails. §4.1.

**"My instrument is strong, so it's valid."**
Strength (relevance) is necessary, not sufficient. A Bartik instrument can
have a huge F and still fail exclusion if the shares (or shocks) are
confounded. §2, §3.

**"One sector drives 90% of the weight — that's fine, the F is high."**
The Rotemberg weights say your entire causal claim rests on one share's
exogeneity. That's fragility, not strength — stress-test that sector. §3.

**"Shock-based needs the shares to be exogenous too."**
No — shock-based *allows* fully endogenous shares. That's the whole point;
the burden moves entirely to the shock. §2.2.
