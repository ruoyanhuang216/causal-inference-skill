## 1. The philosophy: filtering out the confounder

When treatment $D$ is correlated with the error — when highly-motivated users
both adopt the feature and retain better — OLS is biased. It sees adopters
retaining and credits the feature, when motivation is doing part of the work.

An instrument $Z$ is a **filter**. It moves $D$ through a channel that has
nothing to do with the confounder, so the $Z$-driven part of $D$ is clean.
Regress $Y$ on *that* part and the confounder drops out. You pay for this with
a narrower estimand (§3, README) and two untestable assumptions (§2), but when
you have no design and a real confounder, it's the tool.

---

## 2. The four assumptions, and which ones bite

(Full table in [README §2](./README.md#2-the-four-assumptions).) Restated with
teeth:

1. **Relevance** — $\text{Cov}(Z, D) \neq 0$. The instrument must genuinely
   move treatment. *Testable* via the first-stage F (§4.1). A weak first
   stage is the quiet killer (§4.2).
2. **Exclusion** — $Z$ affects $Y$ *only* through $D$. No direct path, no
   backdoor. **Untestable — defend with domain logic.** This is where IV dies.
3. **Independence** — $Z$ is as-good-as-random (perhaps given covariates $X$).
   Partly checkable with balance tests on pre-treatment covariates.
4. **Monotonicity** — $Z$ moves $D$ the same direction for everyone. No
   defiers. Needed to interpret the estimate as a clean LATE (Lecture 1 §4).

Assumptions 1 and 2 pull against each other in a way worth internalizing: the
instruments that are *obviously* relevant (they clearly move treatment) are
often the ones with the most plausible backdoor to the outcome. A strong,
clearly-exogenous instrument is rare and precious.

---

## 3. The mathematics: two stages, one ratio

**First stage** — isolate the exogenous variation in $D$:

```math
\hat{D}_i = \pi_0 + \pi_1 Z_i + X_i \gamma + u_i
```

Predict treatment from the instrument (and controls). $\hat{D}$ is the part of
$D$ explained by $Z$ — the confounder-free part.

**Second stage** — the causal effect:

```math
Y_i = \beta_0 + \tau_{IV} \hat{D}_i + X_i \delta + \epsilon_i
```

Regress the outcome on *predicted* treatment. $\tau_{IV}$ is the estimate.
(Run this through a real 2SLS routine, not two manual OLS steps — the manual
second stage gives the right point estimate but *wrong standard errors*,
because it treats $\hat{D}$ as known when it was estimated.)

**Reduced form** — the instrument's raw effect on the outcome:

```math
Y_i = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 Z_i + X_i \theta + \nu_i
```

And the identity that ties it together:

```math
\tau_{IV} = \frac{\alpha_1}{\pi_1} = \frac{\text{reduced form}}{\text{first stage}}
```

**2SLS is the reduced form divided by the first stage** — the Wald ratio from
Lecture 1, now with covariates. This is the single most useful fact for
sanity-checking an IV:

> **Always run the reduced form, and look at it first.** If $Z$ has no raw,
> significant relationship with $Y$ (a null reduced form), your 2SLS is dead
> on arrival — dividing a zero numerator by a small denominator produces
> noise, not a finding. A significant 2SLS with an insignificant reduced form
> is a red flag that you're dividing by a near-zero first stage.

*Verified* (code in §6): confounded $D$ with true effect **2.0**. OLS returns
**2.835** (biased up by the confounder). First stage $\pi_1 = 0.70$, reduced
form $\alpha_1 = 1.41$, and $\alpha_1 / \pi_1 = 1.41/0.70 = \mathbf{2.01}$ —
2SLS recovers the truth exactly, and equals the ratio.

## 4. Diagnostics

### 4.1 First-stage F — the relevance test

Report the F-statistic on the excluded instrument(s) in the first stage.

- **Classic rule (Stock-Yogo):** $F > 10$.
- **Modern:** with heteroskedastic or clustered errors, the old F is
  unreliable — use the **effective F-statistic** (Montiel Olea & Pflueger
  2013). And for honest 5% inference the true bar is far higher than 10 — Lee,
  McCrary, Moreira & Porter (2022) put it near **104**.

### 4.2 Why weak instruments are dangerous

Look at $\tau_{IV} = \alpha_1 / \pi_1$. A weak first stage means $\pi_1 \approx
0$ — a near-zero denominator. Two things follow, both bad:

- **Standard errors explode.** Dividing by a tiny, noisily-estimated number
  produces enormous, unstable estimates.
- **The estimate is biased *toward OLS*.** In finite samples, weak-instrument
  2SLS drifts back toward the very confounded OLS estimate you used IV to
  escape. A weak instrument gives you OLS's bias with IV's variance — the
  worst of both.

This is why "my F is 8 but the effect is significant" is not reassuring: the
significance and the bias are both artifacts of the weak first stage.

### 4.3 The rest of the toolkit

- **Reduced form** (§3): significant, same sign as the structural estimate.
- **Over-identification (Hansen J):** *only* with more instruments than
  endogenous regressors — tests whether they all imply the *same* estimate. A
  rejection means at least one instrument violates exclusion. (It can't tell
  you *which*, and it's useless when all your instruments share the same
  flaw.)
- **Placebo instruments / outcomes:** the instrument must **not** predict
  *pre-treatment* outcomes. If it does, independence or exclusion is broken —
  the instrument is correlated with something it shouldn't be. (This is the
  test §5.3 recommends for the collection-agent design.)
- **Exclusion:** untestable. Write down every channel from $Z$ to $Y$ that
  doesn't pass through $D$, and argue each one is negligible.

## 5. When to reach for IV (and when not to)

IV is the right tool when **all** of these hold:

- treatment is genuinely confounded (OLS is biased),
- you have no exploitable design (no experiment, rollout, or cutoff),
- you can find a variable that moves treatment,
- and — the hard part — that variable plausibly has *no other path* to the
  outcome.

If the "instrument" is only *weakly* exogenous, you're often better off with a
transparent OLS plus a sensitivity analysis (Lecture 6 territory) than with an
IV whose exclusion restriction you can't defend. **A bad instrument is worse
than no instrument**, because it launders a confounded estimate through
impressive-looking machinery and makes it *harder* to challenge. The
strongest IVs come from genuine randomness — lotteries, random assignment of
cases (§5.3), or plausibly-exogenous shocks (§5.2).

## 6. Python

```python
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm

# ---- 2SLS by hand via projection (linearmodels.IV2SLS in production) ----
def tsls(Y, D, Z, X=None):
    """Y outcome, D endogenous (n,), Z instruments (n,k), X controls (n,m)."""
    n = len(Y)
    cols = [np.ones(n)] + ([X] if X is not None else [])
    Xmat = np.column_stack(cols + [D])                 # regressors incl. D
    Zmat = np.column_stack(cols + [Z])                 # instruments incl. controls
    Pz = Zmat @ np.linalg.solve(Zmat.T @ Zmat, Zmat.T) # projection onto instrument space
    beta = np.linalg.solve(Xmat.T @ Pz @ Xmat, Xmat.T @ Pz @ Y)
    return beta[-1]                                     # coef on D

# ---- The Wald identity: 2SLS = reduced form / first stage ----
ols = sm.OLS(Y, sm.add_constant(D)).fit().params[1]
pi1 = sm.OLS(D, sm.add_constant(Z)).fit().params[1]    # first stage
a1  = sm.OLS(Y, sm.add_constant(Z)).fit().params[1]    # reduced form -- LOOK AT THIS FIRST
print(f"OLS (biased) = {ols:.3f}")
print(f"first stage  = {pi1:.3f}   reduced form = {a1:.3f}")
print(f"Wald a1/pi1  = {a1/pi1:.3f}   2SLS = {tsls(Y, D, Z.reshape(-1,1)):.3f}")
# OLS 2.835 | first stage 0.70, reduced form 1.41 | Wald 2.01 = 2SLS 2.01 (true 2.0)

# ---- First-stage F (use effective F with clustered/robust SEs) ----
fs = sm.OLS(D, sm.add_constant(Z)).fit(cov_type="HC1")
F = fs.tvalues[1] ** 2
print(f"first-stage F = {F:.1f}  ({'ok-ish' if F > 10 else 'WEAK'}; modern bar ~104)")

# ---- Placebo: instrument must NOT predict a pre-treatment outcome ----
# tsls(Y_pretreatment, D, Z)  -> must be ~0
```

## 7. Common misreadings

**"IV removes the need to worry about confounders."**
It swaps unconfoundedness (observe every confounder) for exclusion (the
instrument has no other channel). Both untestable — you've moved the burden,
not lifted it. README §5.

**"My F is 8, but the result is significant, so it's fine."**
Weak instruments make 2SLS biased toward OLS *and* inflate SEs. Significance
under a weak first stage is an artifact. Use the effective F; the bar is far
above 10. §4.2.

**"2SLS is significant, so the instrument works."**
Check the reduced form. A significant 2SLS with a null reduced form means
you're dividing by a near-zero first stage — noise dressed as a finding. §3.

**"IV gives the average treatment effect."**
LATE (binary) or ACR (continuous) — the effect for compliers only. Not the
ATE. README §3.

**"The Hansen J test passed, so my instruments are valid."**
J only tests whether instruments *agree*. If they share the same exclusion
violation, they agree and J passes while all are invalid. It's a consistency
check, not a validity proof. §4.3.

**"I'll just run two OLS regressions for 2SLS."**
The point estimate is right; the standard errors are wrong (they ignore that
$\hat D$ was estimated). Use a real 2SLS routine. §3.
