Causal Inference Analysis

Effect of State Minimum Wage Increases on Restaurant Employment

Staggered Difference-in-Differences · Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021)

ATT 2010 -- 2020 3,047 counties 29 treated states

Why This Question Matters

  • Minimum wage increases are among the most debated labor policies — 29 states raised MW above federal level between 2010-2020
  • Restaurants employ ~10% of US workers, operate on thin margins (~5%), and have high labor cost shares (~30%)
  • Classic empirical question since Card & Krueger (1994), but prior work used simple 2-group DiD — staggered adoption requires modern methods

Research Design

Setup

  • Treatment: State MW increase above $7.25 federal minimum
  • Outcome: Log county-level restaurant employment
  • Data: QCEW, quarterly, 2010-2020
  • Control: 21 never-treated states

Identification

  • Method: Staggered DiD (Callaway & Sant'Anna)
  • Assumption: Parallel trends — absent MW increase, treated states would have followed the same employment trajectory as controls
  • Why not TWFE: Biased with heterogeneous treatment effects under staggered adoption

Main Result

-1.5%
ATT = -0.0147  |  95% CI: [-0.025, -0.004]  |  p = 0.007

Restaurant employment fell by ~1.5% in states that raised their minimum wage. For the average treated state with 45,000 restaurant workers, this translates to roughly 675 fewer jobs.

Event study shows: no pre-trends (F=0.87, p=0.54), effects emerge gradually, stabilize by quarter +4.

Limitations

  • External validity: Results cover 2010-2020 MW levels ($7.25-$15); may not extrapolate to $15+/hr policies
  • Spillovers: Labor migration between treated and control states could attenuate estimates
  • Hours vs. headcount: We measure employment counts, not hours worked — firms may cut hours instead of jobs
  • Welfare incomplete: Employment losses must be weighed against wage gains for retained workers

Takeaway

State minimum wage increases caused a modest but robust 1.5% decline in restaurant employment, concentrated in urban areas and states with larger increases.

The result survives all 7 robustness dimensions: specification sensitivity, sample stability, alternative inference, sensitivity to unobservables (δ=2.34), placebo tests (6/6 passed), and cross-method comparison (5 estimators agree).

Policy implication: anticipate modest restaurant employment reductions when raising MW, but a full welfare assessment requires weighing these losses against the wage gains for retained workers.
